# Overview summary

Energy captures how production, transport, pricing, and security of oil, gas, power, and related inputs affect inflation, state behavior, and industrial competitiveness.

## Why it matters

It matters because energy is both a macro input and a geopolitical lever, so changes in supply or price can cascade across many markets at once.

## Where it applies

Relevant in commodities, geopolitics, inflation, utilities, industrial production, and energy-exporting states.

## Common signals

- supply disruptions or spare-capacity changes
- transport chokepoints and infrastructure vulnerability
- policy moves affecting output or pricing
- industrial and consumer sensitivity to energy-cost shifts

## Common failure modes

- treating energy as only a commodity story when it is also a policy and security story
- overreading short-term price noise without supply context
- missing second-order inflation and political effects

## Interactions

Energy often interacts with `macro`, `trade`, `conflicts`, and `sanctions`.
