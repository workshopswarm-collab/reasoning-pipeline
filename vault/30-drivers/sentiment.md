# Overview summary

Sentiment captures whether the crowd’s tone, optimism, fear, or enthusiasm is amplifying or suppressing prices, expectations, and interpretation beyond hard fundamentals.

## Why it matters

It matters because many markets move not just on facts but on how those facts are emotionally or socially received.

## Where it applies

Relevant across politics, crypto, sports, tech, consumer brands, and macro-sensitive assets.

## Common signals

- rapid re-rating without equivalent fundamental change
- strong narrative crowding
- asymmetry between attention and hard evidence
- reflexive price or popularity moves

## Common failure modes

- treating sentiment as meaningless noise when it can drive real outcomes
- treating sentiment as full proof of fundamentals
- confusing temporary enthusiasm with durable demand

## Interactions

Sentiment often interacts with `media-narratives`, `macro`, `product-launches`, and `elections`.
