# Overview summary

Polling captures how measured voter preference, methodology, turnout assumptions, and polling error shape political expectations and market pricing.

## Why it matters

It matters because polling is one of the clearest political inputs, but it is often misused as a direct forecast rather than one noisy measurement inside a larger election process.

## Where it applies

Relevant in elections, party leadership contests, referendum-style votes, and public-opinion-driven political markets.

## Common signals

- poll trend durability across firms
- sample quality and likely-voter assumptions
- divergence between polling and turnout or fundamentals
- whether new polls are changing the median picture or just adding noise

## Common failure modes

- reading one poll as the story
- ignoring house effects and turnout assumptions
- treating polling error as random when it may be structurally biased in a given cycle

## Interactions

Polling often interacts with `elections`, `sentiment`, `media-narratives`, and `leadership-changes`.
