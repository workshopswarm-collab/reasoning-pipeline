# Summary

Courts are a core political and market institution because legal interpretation can change institutional power, policy durability, and regulatory boundaries.

## Key facts extracted

- The Supreme Court has final appellate power over major constitutional questions in the U.S.
- Court composition and jurisdictional structure matter for long-run policy interpretation.
- Legal outcomes can matter as much as electoral outcomes in some domains.

## Possible impact on the question

Helpful when a political or regulatory market depends on whether litigation risk is genuinely outcome-shaping or mostly narrative noise.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- The source directly explains what courts do, how judicial review works, and why legal process can shape policy outcomes.
- It supports baseline claims about courts as institutional actors rather than just political symbols.

## What is uncertain

- It does not predict how a specific court will rule or how quickly litigation will change policy reality.
- Legal authority, timing, and enforcement often move on different clocks.

## Why this source may matter

Useful because markets often treat legal action as immediate policy resolution when courts usually operate through slower institutional channels.
