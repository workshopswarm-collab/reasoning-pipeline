# Overview summary

The courts subdomain is the institutional layer of judicial authority, case selection, doctrinal drift, composition, enforcement limits, and interaction with other branches of government. It is distinct from the `legal` driver because it focuses on the court system itself rather than legal pressure as a general causal mechanism.

## Why this subdomain matters

This subdomain matters because courts are often slow-moving but highly consequential nodes in the political system. Researchers need a stable note for judicial process, authority, and institutional constraints so they do not reduce every court story to a one-off ruling headline.

## Core conclusions

- Courts shape outcomes through procedure, doctrine, and institutional authority, not just final opinions.
- Judicial composition and case selection can matter as much as the headline dispute itself.
- Court impact depends on enforcement, administrative follow-through, and interaction with legislatures and executives.
- Court research should separate legal reasoning from political narrative without pretending the two are unrelated.

## Main evidence clusters

- jurisdiction and institutional authority
- case selection and procedural posture
- composition and ideological balance
- doctrine, precedent, and standard of review
- enforcement and compliance limits
- interaction with legislatures, agencies, and executives

## Important recurring objects

- supreme and constitutional courts
- lower courts and appellate circuits
- judges and judicial blocs
- litigants, agencies, and executives
- constitutions, statutes, and precedents
- injunctions, orders, and enforcement mechanisms

## Important recurring drivers

- legal
- governance
- leadership-changes
- media-narratives

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- treating a filing, hearing, or injunction as equivalent to final resolution
- ignoring jurisdiction, standing, or procedural posture
- reading court outcomes only as partisan narrative while neglecting doctrine and enforceability
- assuming one ruling cleanly settles a broader political conflict

## Missing coverage

- better court-process source maps and procedural-reference notes
- stronger links between judicial institutions and administrative follow-through
- more explicit guidance on doctrinal versus political interpretation

## Most fragile assumptions

- that headline court language maps directly to practical implementation
- that judicial ideology alone predicts every outcome
- that legal conflict resolves political conflict cleanly or quickly
