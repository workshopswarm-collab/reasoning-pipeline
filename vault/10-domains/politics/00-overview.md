# Synthesis summary

Politics research should focus on institutions, parties, leaders, elections, legislation, courts, and public opinion as interacting systems of power.

## Core conclusions

- Politics is fundamentally about allocation of power and decisions through institutions.
- Elections are only one layer; legislation, courts, appointments, and executive action also matter.
- Public opinion and polling matter because they influence incentives and narratives, not because they are identical to outcomes.
- Political research should distinguish structural institutional rules from fast-moving campaign or narrative dynamics.

## Main evidence clusters

- election mechanics
- party competition
- polling and public opinion
- institutional rules
- judicial and legislative processes

## Conflicts between inputs

- politics mixes cooperative procedures with conflict, so consensus signals and adversarial signals can coexist.

## Missing information

- country-specific political systems
- polling-method quality notes
- electoral calendar files
- party and candidate dossier templates

## Most fragile assumptions

- that polling equals outcome
- that campaign narratives override institutional constraints
