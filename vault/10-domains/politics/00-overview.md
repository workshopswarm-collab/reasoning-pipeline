# Overview summary

Politics is the domain of institutional power, parties, leaders, elections, legislation, courts, and public opinion. Good political research should distinguish stable rules of the system from faster-moving campaign, media, and coalition dynamics.

## Why this area matters

Politics matters because institutions allocate power, shape incentives, and determine how public preferences, elite competition, and state authority translate into outcomes. Many apparently event-driven questions in this domain are really questions about institutional structure and political competition.

## Core conclusions

- Politics should be read through institutions first and narrative second.
- `elections`, `polling`, and `courts` currently justify standalone subdomains because they have distinct process and evidence structures; other topics should stay in the parent domain or research layer unless they earn more separation.
- Public opinion, legal procedure, and elite strategy interact, but should not be collapsed into a single story.
- Country-specific rules matter enough that generic political intuition is often misleading.

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- treating polling as equivalent to outcome rather than one input into political incentives
- overweighting campaign narrative while underweighting institutional constraints
- conflating legal procedure, public opinion, and elite strategy
- ignoring country-specific political rules when comparing cases

## Missing coverage

- country-specific political systems
- polling-method quality notes
- electoral calendar files
- party and candidate dossier templates
- better coverage of legislative and judicial process baselines

## Most fragile assumptions

- that polling equals outcome
- that campaign narratives override institutional constraints
- that all political systems can be analyzed with the same default assumptions

## Recommended next research steps

- keep cycle-specific campaign and procedural developments in `40-research/`
- deepen coverage across `institutions`, `elections`, `polling`, and `courts`, especially with more country-specific system notes
- expand polling-quality, legislative-process, and institutional-architecture guidance across the politics branch
