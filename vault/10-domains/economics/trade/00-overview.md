# Overview summary

The trade subdomain is the structural layer of tariffs, market access, supply chains, export controls, customs systems, and cross-border production networks. It is distinct from the `trade` driver note, which explains how trade pressure changes outcomes in a given case.

## Why this subdomain matters

Trade matters because it is both a macro transmission system and a policy architecture. A dedicated subdomain note is useful when researchers need to orient around institutions, routes, counterparties, and policy instruments rather than treating trade as a generic macro backdrop.

## Core conclusions

- Trade is a system of rules, logistics, and dependencies, not just a top-line macro variable.
- Tariffs and export controls matter through enforcement and substitution pathways, not just announcement effects.
- Country, sector, and supply-chain structure often matter more than aggregate trade rhetoric.
- Trade research usually sits at the intersection of economics, geopolitics, and industrial policy.

## Main evidence clusters

- tariffs and market-access rules
- export controls and industrial-policy restrictions
- customs, shipping, and logistics channels
- bilateral dependencies and rerouting behavior
- trade-balance and demand indicators
- sector-level supply-chain concentration

## Important recurring objects

- trade ministries and regulators
- exporters, importers, and logistics intermediaries
- ports, shipping routes, and chokepoints
- customs regimes and tariff schedules
- strategically sensitive industries and inputs

## Important recurring drivers

- trade
- sanctions
- diplomacy
- macro

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- treating trade as only a headline policy story instead of a routing and dependency system
- assuming announced restrictions are fully enforceable
- ignoring substitution, rerouting, and grey-market adaptation
- using national-level framing when the real signal is sector-specific

## Missing coverage

- better source maps for customs, shipping, and sector-level trade data
- stronger guidance on export-control enforcement and evasion
- clearer linkage between trade institutions and strategic industries

## Most fragile assumptions

- that policy announcements cleanly map to real trade disruption
- that aggregate trade metrics capture sector-specific vulnerabilities
- that supply chains are rigid rather than adaptive
