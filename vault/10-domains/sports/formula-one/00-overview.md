# Overview summary

Formula 1 research should focus on the interaction between driver quality, team engineering, reliability, development pace, strategy, regulations, and race-calendar context.

## Why this subdomain matters

Formula 1 matters for prediction work because it generates repeated markets across race winners, podiums, driver championships, constructor championships, and intra-team comparisons. It is a structurally good retrieval domain because the same teams and drivers recur across the full season.

## Core conclusions

- F1 markets are driven by both driver talent and car quality; separating the two is essential.
- Team development pace and reliability are often as important as headline speed.
- Single-race results can be noisy; championship markets require a longer-horizon view.
- Strategy, qualifying position, and track-specific fit can create major short-run variance.
- Public narrative often overreacts to one weekend; stable edge usually shows up over multiple events.

## What to track

- driver pace and consistency
- constructor competitiveness
- qualifying versus race pace
- reliability and mechanical attrition
- upgrade cycles and regulation sensitivity
- track fit, weather, and calendar context
- intra-team dynamics and team orders

## Retrieval implications

Good F1 retrieval should usually connect a market to:
- the series (`formula-1`)
- the main teams involved
- the main drivers involved
- structural performance drivers such as reliability, engineering, and seasonality

## Open questions

- which early-season signals are most predictive of title durability
- how quickly markets incorporate team upgrade cycles
- which teams are being over- or underpriced due to brand and media narrative
