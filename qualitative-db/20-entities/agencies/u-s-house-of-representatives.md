# Summary

The U.S. House is a high-yield prediction-market institution because majority control, speaker stability, budget initiation, and coalition fragmentation often determine whether U.S. politics can produce actionable policy.

## What this entity is

The lower chamber of Congress, where partisan seat distribution, leadership durability, and caucus discipline shape legislative momentum.

## Why it matters

The House matters because it is often the clearest institutional test of whether parties can translate electoral outcomes into real governing capacity.

## Current state

Research should focus on majority size, speaker authority, caucus fragmentation, committee control, impeachment/investigatory posture, and whether internal divisions block legislative execution.

## Key strengths

- direct relevance to budget and legislative initiation
- highly sensitive to small shifts in seat distribution
- strong readthrough on coalition discipline and governing realism

## Key weaknesses

- internal party fragmentation can overwhelm formal majority status
- headline narratives can miss procedural and leadership fragility

## Important recent changes

- remains one of the most informative institutions for judging whether electoral wins translate into governable power

## Open questions

- how often narrow House majorities produce signal versus noise in market-implied governing expectations
