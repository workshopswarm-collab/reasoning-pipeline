# Summary
I lean **Yes**, but less confidently than the market. My working estimate is **~72%** versus the market-implied **85.4%**.

## Question being evaluated
Will Iran initiate a qualifying drone, missile, or air strike that impacts Iraqi territory or an Iraqi embassy/consulate in March 2026 under the market’s stated resolution criteria?

## Current lean
Yes-lean, but the market appears overconfident.

## Prior / starting view
Starting prior was that Iraq should be a natural spillover theater in a regional Iran war, which points toward Yes. But the contract wording immediately lowered confidence because many plausible Iraq incidents do not qualify.

## Evidence supporting the claim
- **Iraq is inside the active regional battlespace.** Current conflict summaries for the 2026 Iran war list Iraq among countries attacked by Iran and report Iraq casualties during the conflict. That makes a qualifying event plausible rather than hypothetical.
- **Structural exposure is high.** Iraq’s geography, weak sovereignty, and deep Iran-linked militia penetration make it one of the most likely theaters for Iranian coercive action.
- **Historical precedent exists.** Iran has previously conducted direct strikes into Iraqi territory when deterrence or internal-security logic demanded it.
- **War-spillover logic favors Iraq.** If Iran is broadening pressure across regional nodes, Iraq is one of the lowest-friction targets.

## Evidence against the claim
- **Contract-filter risk is large.** Direct Iranian attribution, launch origin, and physical impact are all required.
- **Proxy substitution risk is large.** Iraq is exactly the kind of theater where Tehran may prefer proxies or deniable partners over overt direct action.
- **Interception risk matters.** Failed or intercepted missiles/drones do not count.
- **Reporting/consensus risk matters.** Even a real event can miss resolution if the date, attribution, or impact details remain muddy.

## Ambiguous or mixed evidence
- The broader war context is strongly suggestive, but many available summaries are secondary and compress distinctions that the market treats as decisive.
- Iraq being “attacked by Iran” in conflict overviews is directionally useful but not sufficient by itself to prove a contract-valid event.
- Iraq’s proxy density cuts both ways: it raises escalation probability overall but lowers confidence that the escalation comes in a contract-qualifying form.

## Conflict between inputs
The main conflict is **interpretive and contract-based**, not factual.
- One interpretation: Iraq’s inclusion in the attacked-country set makes Yes highly likely or already effectively done.
- Competing interpretation: a lot of those incidents may fail the contract definition because they are proxy-linked, intercepted, or insufficiently attributed.
- Evidence that resolves this: a specific March Iraq event with consensus reporting on direct Iranian responsibility, impact location, and timing.

## Key assumptions
- At least one Iraq event meets the direct-attribution and impact tests.
- Tehran accepts the diplomatic cost of an overt strike on Iraqi soil rather than relying only on proxies.
- A qualifying event, if it happened, is reported clearly enough for market resolution.

## Key uncertainties
- Whether a qualifying Iraq impact event already occurred in March under the exact contract definition.
- Whether available reporting cleanly distinguishes direct Iranian strikes from PMF / militia activity.
- Whether any Iraq-bound projectiles were intercepted before impact.

## Disconfirming signals to watch
- Reporting converges that Iraq incidents were conducted by proxies.
- Reported projectiles toward Iraq were intercepted or only produced debris fall.
- Consensus reporting fails to establish launch origin from Iranian territory or explicit Iranian military responsibility.
- Resolution discussion shifts toward ambiguity rather than confirmation.

## What would increase confidence
- A Reuters/AP/BBC-quality report specifying that Iranian missiles or drones launched by Iranian forces impacted Iraqi territory in March.
- Official Iranian claim of responsibility for an Iraq strike.
- Independent confirmation that the incident was not merely a proxy action or an interception.

## Net update logic
The war backdrop and Iraq’s structural exposure moved me toward Yes. The narrow resolution language and Iraq’s extreme proxy contamination pulled me back down from the market. Net result: still Yes-leaning, but with a substantial haircut versus the market because the main underpriced risk is **contract invalidation of intuitive escalation paths**.
