# Summary
The evidence supports **YES**, but not as comfortably as a 79.25% market price suggests. The core risk-manager point is that the movie looked strong in ordinary box-office terms while still being dangerously close to the contract line.

## Question being evaluated
Will *Project Hail Mary* post a domestic second-weekend gross greater than **$54M**?

## Current lean
Lean **YES**, but with lower confidence than the market.

## Prior / starting view
A film opening above $80M with strong audience/critical response and no obvious second-weekend challenger would normally be favored to hold well enough to clear $54M.

## Evidence supporting the claim
- **Very strong opening base**: The Numbers lists an $80.5M opening weekend. A film starting from that level does not need an extraordinary hold to finish above $54M in weekend two.
- **Healthy realized hold**: The final second-weekend drop was only **-32%**, which is better than the typical collapse pattern for a front-loaded blockbuster.
- **Still #1 in weekend two**: The film remained the domestic #1 with $54.54M, while the #2 title grossed only $12.2M.
- **Broad distribution support**: Theater count expanded from 4,007 to 4,077, reducing exhibitor-side constraints.
- **Positive reception context**: Rotten Tomatoes language suggests strong critical and audience response, which is consistent with decent week-two retention.

## Evidence against the claim
- **Threshold geometry was tight**: Final reported weekend was only **$54.5376M**, or about **$0.54M above the line**.
- **Revision / finalization risk mattered**: With a margin this small, an ordinary late adjustment, softer Sunday actual, or small source discrepancy could have flipped the contract.
- **Narrative strength != contract safety**: Being #1, having a strong hold, and receiving good reviews are all compatible with failing a narrowly placed threshold.
- **Long runtime adds throughput friction**: At 156 minutes, the film had fewer daily show slots than shorter mainstream titles, which modestly caps upside.

## Ambiguous or mixed evidence
- **Strong reviews and word of mouth** are positive for retention, but do not directly answer whether the line was set a little too high.
- **Weak competitive field** helps holdovers, but may already have been fully appreciated by the market.

## Conflict between inputs
There is little explicit factual conflict in the sources used here. The main disagreement is **weighting-based**:
- bullish interpretation: the film’s strong opening, positive reception, and mild drop make YES highly likely
- risk-manager interpretation: those facts support YES directionally, but the market may still have overpaid for certainty because the contract cutoff sat very close to plausible final outcomes

Evidence that would resolve this better ex ante would be: the exact studio Sunday estimate in relation to $54M, and historical revision behavior for similar large March releases sitting near a contract strike.

## Key assumptions
- Strong week-two retention would survive final reporting with a nontrivial cushion.
- Positive reception would offset front-loading enough to keep the movie above the strike.
- No late-stage revision would erase the thin margin.

## Key uncertainties
- How much of the market’s 79.25% reflected real informational edge versus confidence spillover from strong general box-office narratives.
- What estimate level traders were anchoring to at close.

## Disconfirming signals to watch
- Any final or near-final estimate landing inside a narrow $54.0M-$54.5M band.
- Evidence of late downward revisions in comparable contracts.
- Sunday actual underperforming studio estimate.

## What would increase confidence
- A confirmed final at least $1M-$2M above the strike.
- Independent Box Office Mojo final showing a similarly comfortable margin.
- Reliable contemporaneous estimate materially above $54M before market close.

## Net update logic
The film’s strength clearly pushed the probability above 50%. But the critical update from a risk perspective is that **the final realized edge over the line was tiny**. That means the market price was not just a bet on strong performance; it was a bet that strong performance would translate into a safely above-strike final. The first part was right. The second part was less secure than 79% implied.

## Suggested downstream use
Use this as a forecast-calibration input and as a reminder that threshold markets should be stress-tested for **margin-to-strike**, not just directional thesis quality.
