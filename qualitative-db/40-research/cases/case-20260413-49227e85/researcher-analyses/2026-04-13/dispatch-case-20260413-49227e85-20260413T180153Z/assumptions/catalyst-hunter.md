# Assumption

If DeepSeek intended to launch a qualifying V4 to the general public by April 15, 2026, there would likely already be stronger official prelaunch signaling, staging artifacts, or credible independent reporting by April 13, 2026.

## Why this assumption matters

The bearish timing view depends less on proving V4 is impossible and more on judging that the remaining window is unusually short for a major flagship release that must be clearly announced and publicly accessible.

## What this assumption supports

- A probability estimate below the market price.
- The view that the most important catalyst is an explicit official DeepSeek announcement, and that no such catalyst is yet visible.
- The interpretation that absence of visible launch preparation this late is meaningful evidence against `Yes`.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Official DeepSeek surfaces publicly list prior major releases and still identify the live API models as V3.2.
- A qualifying `Yes` requires more than rumors; it requires public accessibility plus clear official positioning as the next V-series successor.
- Major public AI launches usually leave at least some pre-deadline traces across official docs, distribution pages, or credible reporting.

## What would falsify it

- An official DeepSeek announcement of DeepSeek V4 or another clearly designated V-series successor to V3 made publicly accessible before the deadline.
- Credible reporting, rapidly confirmed by official DeepSeek surfaces, that a public open beta or open waitlist has gone live.

## Early warning signs

- New DeepSeek docs/news entries referencing V4.
- New public model pages or repos clearly labeled as the next V-series flagship.
- Multiple credible outlets reporting an imminent open release window.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The estimate would need to move sharply higher, likely above market, because the market contract is extremely event-driven and a single official accessibility announcement could settle the case quickly.
