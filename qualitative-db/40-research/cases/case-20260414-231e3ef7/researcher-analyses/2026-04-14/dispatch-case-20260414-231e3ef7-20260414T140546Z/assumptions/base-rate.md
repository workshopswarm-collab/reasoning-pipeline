# Assumption

A two-point lead after 12 of 14 rounds in an eight-player Candidates Tournament usually converts to an eventual tournament win absent extraordinary collapse or a source/reporting error.

## Why this assumption matters

The base-rate case depends less on chess-specific tactical narratives than on the structural fact that very little schedule remains and the nearest chasers have limited opportunities to erase a two-point gap.

## What this assumption supports

- A very high probability estimate for Sindarov.
- A view that the market is directionally right even if slightly overconfident.
- A conclusion that remaining risk is concentrated in late collapse/tie mechanics and source-of-truth latency rather than in ordinary competitive uncertainty.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Only two classical rounds remain.
- The closest chaser is already two full points behind.
- In elite round-robins, large late leads are uncommon and therefore typically highly conversion-favorable when they occur.
- The tournament has explicit tiebreak procedures, so even a catch-up does not automatically mean Sindarov loses; it means his current lead would need to evaporate first.

## What would falsify it

- Official results showing Sindarov losing round 13 while the nearest rival wins, sharply narrowing the gap.
- Official standings showing a tie for first after round 14.
- Evidence that the published standings snapshot used here is stale or incorrect.

## Early warning signs

- Any verified official report reducing the lead to one point or less.
- Evidence of illness, withdrawal risk, or disciplinary issue affecting Sindarov.
- Discovery that tie-break or qualification rules were misunderstood.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The estimate would need to move down materially and shift from a lead-conversion base-rate framing to a coin-flip or modest-favorite framing depending on the updated standings.
