# Summary
FIDE round reports for rounds 9-12 consistently describe Sindarov as the clear tournament leader with a narrowing but still substantial edge. Round 9 says he still led by 1.5 points after missing a win versus Bluebaum. Round 10 says he moved to a full two-point lead with four rounds left after beating Praggnanandhaa. Round 11 says he still held a two-point lead with three rounds left after drawing Caruana. Round 12 says he drew Nakamura and with two rounds remaining moved one step closer, while Giri missed a chance to close the gap.

# Key extracts
- Round 9 FIDE report: "With five rounds remaining, Sindarov still leads by 1.5 points."
- Round 10 FIDE report: "With only four rounds remaining, Sindarov now leads the field by a full two points."
- Round 11 FIDE report: "With only three rounds remaining, he still holds a two-point lead."
- Round 12 FIDE report: "With two rounds remaining, he moves one step closer to winning the Candidates."
- Round 12 FIDE report quotes Sindarov: "I was aiming for forcing draw lines; a fast draw is ideal for my situation."
- Round 12 FIDE report: Giri "was unable to convert the advantage into a full point" and faces a "must-win situation with the white pieces against tournament leader Javokhir Sindarov."

# Why it matters
These are effectively primary tournament-status sources because FIDE is both organizer and stated primary resolution source for the market. They show the mechanism behind the near-certain market pricing: large lead, few rounds left, and nearest chaser failing to convert a key chance.

# Risk-manager take
The same sources also surface the main fragility: the market is not yet settled. The round-12 report still frames round 13 against Giri as live and meaningful, and Sindarov himself chose safety rather than a mathematically settled state. So operationally this remains a high-probability but unresolved tournament state, not a closed case.
