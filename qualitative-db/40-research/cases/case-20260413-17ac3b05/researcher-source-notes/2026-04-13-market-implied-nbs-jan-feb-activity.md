# Summary
This primary official activity release is the closest direct pre-resolution macro read for Q1 2026. It does not state GDP directly, but it shows broad January-February stabilization and improvement across industry, services, retail, infrastructure investment, and trade, which is directionally consistent with a mid-5-ish Q1 GDP print rather than a sharp downside miss.

## Key facts extracted
- Industrial value added in January-February rose 6.3% y/y.
- Services production rose 5.2% y/y.
- Retail sales rose 2.8% y/y, faster than last December.
- Fixed-asset investment rose 1.8% y/y; infrastructure investment rose 11.4% y/y.
- Exports and imports both showed strong growth in the first two months.
- The release still flags external environment pressure, geopolitical risk, and domestic operational difficulties.

## Evidence directly stated by source
- NBS describes the economy as having "got off to a robust and promising start."
- Multiple major activity indicators improved versus late-2025 comparisons.
- NBS explicitly notes persistent problems and challenges despite the stronger start.

## What is uncertain
- This is not the GDP release itself.
- Official Chinese macro releases can embed presentational bias and may overstate strength at the margin.
- March data could still move the quarter enough to matter near the 5.0/5.5 bracket boundary.

## Why this source may matter
A market pricing 74% for the 5.0-5.5 bracket is plausibly assuming that early-quarter activity is good enough to keep headline Q1 growth near the policy-comfort zone but not so strong as to push clearly above 5.5.

## Possible impact on the question
This source supports the market's central case more than an immediate bearish miss case. It is especially relevant because the contract resolves on the initial official GDP print, and this source is from the same institution that will publish that print.

## Reliability notes
Useful as a direct official nowcast input but not fully independent. Best treated as one primary directional source, to be balanced with contextual caution about official-data reliability and the missing March observation.
