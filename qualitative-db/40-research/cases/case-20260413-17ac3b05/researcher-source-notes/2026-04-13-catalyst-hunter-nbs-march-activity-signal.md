# Summary

Official March and Jan-Feb releases suggest Q1 activity was good enough to keep a 5.0%-5.5% GDP print plausible, but the mix is not cleanly one-way because manufacturing and exports improved while real estate and some demand/employment subcomponents remained soft.

## Key facts extracted

From the Jan-Feb national economy release:
- Industrial production rose 6.3% y/y in Jan-Feb.
- Services production rose 5.2% y/y in Jan-Feb.
- Retail sales rose 2.8% y/y in Jan-Feb.
- Fixed-asset investment rose 1.8% y/y in Jan-Feb, but real-estate development investment fell 11.1% y/y.
- Exports and imports both showed strong early-year growth.
- CPI was positive and PPI deflation narrowed but remained negative.
- NBS commentary said the economy got off to a good start while also warning about external environment and domestic development challenges.

From the March PMI release:
- Manufacturing PMI rose to 50.4 from 49.0 in February.
- Production index rose to 51.4 and new orders to 51.6.
- New export orders improved to 49.1, still just below 50.
- Non-manufacturing business activity index was 50.1, only slightly expansionary.
- Construction business activity remained below 50 at 49.3.
- Employment readings remained weak in both manufacturing and non-manufacturing.
- Composite PMI output index was 50.5, indicating mild overall expansion.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- Official monthly indicators improved into March after softer February readings.
- The official signal mix still includes obvious weak spots, especially real estate and labor-related subindexes.

## What is uncertain

- These are partial indicators, not the GDP print itself.
- NBS releases are official and timely but not independent from the final publishing institution.
- Mapping monthly activity indicators to the exact Q1 y/y GDP bracket remains partly inferential.

## Why this source may matter

This is the most relevant pre-release catalyst evidence set available immediately before the Q1 GDP release window. It informs whether the first print is likely to stay inside the market's bracket.

## Possible impact on the question

The data mix leans toward a bracket-consistent middle outcome rather than an obvious miss high or miss low, but not with enough force to justify extreme confidence.

## Reliability notes

Strong on recency and directness to the release institution; weaker on independence because the same official statistical system produces both the lead indicators and the final GDP release.
