# Assumption

China's initial Q1 2026 GDP release will remain close to the recent policy-targeting pattern and will not be knocked materially outside the 5.0%-5.5% band by a late-quarter shock or unusual statistical presentation change.

## Why this assumption matters

The base-rate case is not that true underlying growth is known with precision; it is that official reported GDP usually remains clustered near policy-consistent ranges unless there is a substantial disturbance.

## What this assumption supports

- A probability estimate above 50% for the 5.0%-5.5% bracket.
- Rough agreement with a market that expects the official print to land in-range.
- Downweighting vivid single-indicator narratives relative to the broader pattern of policy-managed official growth reporting.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- China has strong incentives for quarterly growth reporting continuity around policy targets.
- The official Jan-February 2026 data set does not show an obvious collapse inconsistent with a low-5s print.
- The market settles on the initial NBS release, which historically tends to provide a clean headline number rather than a noisy mid-quarter-style estimate.

## What would falsify it

- March activity data showing a sharp synchronized deterioration in industry, retail, services, and labor.
- A major trade, financial, public-health, or policy shock before the Q1 release.
- Evidence that the initial NBS print methodology or release path changed in a way that makes prior quarter clustering a weak guide.

## Early warning signs

- A sharp downside surprise in March PMI or trade data.
- Abrupt deterioration in property, employment, or deflation indicators.
- Signals of release delay, unusual wording, or administrative disruption around the April GDP publication.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The probability for the 5.0%-5.5% bracket should fall materially, with most downside going to the below-5.0 bucket unless the failure is instead an upside macro surprise pushing the print above 5.5.
