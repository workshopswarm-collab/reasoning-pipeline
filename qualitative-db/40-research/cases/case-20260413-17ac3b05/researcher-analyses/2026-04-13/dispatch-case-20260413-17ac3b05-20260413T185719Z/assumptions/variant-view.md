# Assumption

The initial Q1 2026 NBS GDP print will remain policy-smoothing and headline-stable enough that weak property and consumption data are more likely to compress growth toward the lower half of the 5.0-5.5 band than to push the first print below 5.0.

## Why this assumption matters

The final probability depends on whether headline GDP behaves like a relatively stable managed aggregate or whether underlying weakness is strong enough to break that stability in the first print.

## What this assumption supports

- A moderate yes view rather than a high-conviction yes view.
- A probability estimate below market but still above 50%.
- The claim that the neglected risk is downside-to-below-5.0, not upside-above-5.5.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- The Jan-Feb NBS activity release shows enough support from industrial output, exports, and infrastructure to keep aggregate growth elevated.
- China’s official headline macro series are often relatively smooth compared with underlying sector stress.
- The market settles on the initial print only, which favors the stability interpretation over later revisions.

## What would falsify it

- A materially weak March activity backdrop or sharper-than-expected deterioration in property/consumption that shows up in the initial GDP release.
- An unexpectedly transparent or less-smoothed initial print that falls below 5.0.
- A materially stronger-than-expected March push that instead drives the print above 5.5.

## Early warning signs

- March PMIs and retail/industrial updates disappointing versus Jan-Feb.
- Renewed property contraction broadening beyond current weakness.
- Official commentary shifting from "good start" framing toward explicit growth concern.

## What changes if this assumption fails

If the print is less headline-stable than assumed, the below-5.0 tail should be marked up materially and the current moderate yes lean should be reduced or reversed.
