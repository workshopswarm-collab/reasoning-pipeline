# Assumption

The best pre-release estimate comes from reading official March and Jan-Feb activity data as informative-but-imperfect clues to the initial NBS Q1 2026 GDP print, with no separate late-breaking policy or reporting change before the April release.

## Why this assumption matters

The thesis depends on treating the official monthly flow as a useful nowcast for the official quarterly print rather than assuming a large unexplained divergence.

## What this assumption supports

- A modestly above-market probability that the initial Q1 GDP print lands inside 5.0%-5.5%.
- The view that the main repricing catalyst is the April GDP release itself, not a prior soft-data headline.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Jan-Feb activity releases showed generally positive headline momentum.
- March PMI data improved back above 50 in manufacturing and composite output.
- The market contract explicitly settles on the initial NBS quarterly release, making official pre-release signals more relevant than slower external debates.

## What would falsify it

- A credible late pre-release signal showing activity deteriorated sharply in late March.
- A material change or delay in NBS release practice.
- The actual Q1 GDP release printing outside the 5.0%-5.5% band despite the mixed-positive indicator set.

## Early warning signs

- A sudden deterioration in high-frequency trade, property, or credit commentary from credible sources before release.
- Official messaging that emphasizes downside shock or unusual statistical treatment.
- Evidence that March improvement was narrow and not broad enough to support the aggregate print.

## What changes if this assumption fails

Confidence should fall and the probability should move lower if downside deterioration appears, or lower on confidence even if direction is unchanged if source-of-truth timing becomes less stable.

- Main catalyst-hunter finding for this dispatch.
- Source notes on release mechanics and March activity signals.
