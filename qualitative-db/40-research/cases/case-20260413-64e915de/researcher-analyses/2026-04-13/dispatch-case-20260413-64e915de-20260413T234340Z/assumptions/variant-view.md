# Assumption

A near-threshold crypto asset that is still below the target after a large impulse move can fail to print the final increment within a short dated window more often than an extreme market probability implies.

## Why this assumption matters

The variant view depends on distinguishing “very close” from “effectively certain.” If this distinction is not informative, then the market’s 90.5% pricing is probably fair.

## What this assumption supports

- A modestly lower probability than the market despite bullish momentum.
- The interpretation that the strongest variant case is overconfidence, not outright bearishness.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Binance data showed ETH high at 2394.71 during the verification snapshot, meaning the contract was not yet won despite strong momentum.
- Short-dated threshold markets are sensitive to path dependence: a reversal, liquidity fade, or one failed breakout can matter more than broad medium-term bullishness.
- Extreme probabilities can be fragile when many traders are effectively extrapolating the same recent move.

## What would falsify it

- A prompt verified trade through $2,400 on the governing settlement source.
- Strong evidence that the contract resolves from a venue/source where ETH had already crossed $2,400.

## Early warning signs

- ETH re-tests the 2390s repeatedly with increasing spot follow-through.
- Broader crypto beta remains strong and BTC-led risk appetite continues expanding.
- Multiple major venues begin printing highs above $2,400.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The market’s extreme probability would look justified or even conservative, and the variant view should collapse toward agreement with consensus.
