# Assumption

A one-week ETH threshold-touch market trading above 90% usually reflects either an already-nearby price level or market participants expecting normal crypto volatility to supply at least one qualifying wick before expiry.

## Why this assumption matters

The base-rate view depends on interpreting a threshold-touch market differently from a close-above market. In volatile crypto, touching a round number over a week is materially easier than sustaining it.

## What this assumption supports

- A probability estimate that remains high even though current verified external price was still below $2,400.
- A view that the market's extreme pricing is not obviously irrational in this contract type.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

ETH was already trading in the mid-$2,300s on the verification pass, leaving a remaining gap of only about 1% from the target. For a seven-day window in crypto, a 1% threshold breach is usually within ordinary noise if no strong adverse catalyst appears.

## What would falsify it

- Clarified contract rules showing the market requires something stricter than a simple threshold touch.
- Independent higher-frequency price sources consistently showing ETH repeatedly failing near $2,400 with weakening momentum.
- A sharp regime shift that drives ETH materially away from the target early in the window.

## Early warning signs

- Failure to make new weekly highs after multiple attempts near $2,400.
- Market structure deterioration across major crypto benchmarks.
- New information indicating the settlement source uses a narrower reference than a broad spot touch.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The probability should move down materially because the case for agreeing with the market is mostly about ordinary short-horizon volatility plus proximity to the threshold.
