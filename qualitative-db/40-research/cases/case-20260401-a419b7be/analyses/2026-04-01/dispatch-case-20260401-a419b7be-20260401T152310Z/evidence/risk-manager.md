# Summary

## Question being evaluated
Will the active CME Crude Oil (CL) futures contract settle at $90 or higher on the final trading day relevant to March 2026 market resolution?

## Current lean
Leaning yes, but with lower confidence than the market price implies.

## Prior / starting view
Before reviewing current sources, the obvious prior was that a price already near the low 90s during an active Middle East supply shock makes $90+ likely. The risk-manager question is whether the market is overpaying for confidence in that near-term premium persisting through settlement.

## Evidence supporting the claim
- EIA March STEO: Brent jumped to $94 by March 9 after military action in the Middle East; EIA says the key upside risk is extended Hormuz closure and expects Brent to average $91 in 2Q26. This supports a still-elevated front-month crude complex.
- EIA global oil page: effective Hormuz closure is disrupting a chokepoint carrying nearly 20% of global oil supply; some regional production is already shut in. This is a direct supply-and-logistics shock, not just narrative heat.
- IEA March Oil Market Report: estimates at least 8 mb/d of crude production curtailed, plus further condensate/NGL losses and major export disruption. That is large enough to sustain a material war premium if it persists.
- IEA notes member-country emergency stock releases help but are a stop-gap, not a durable fix absent shipping normalization. That limits confidence in rapid downside.

## Evidence against the claim
- EIA's own forecast is conditional on disruptions peaking in early April and then easing; it expects global production to outpace consumption later in 2026, inventories to build by 1.9 mb/d in 2026, and Brent to fall to $70 by 4Q26. The structural backdrop is not persistently bullish outside the conflict premium.
- IEA cut 2026 oil demand growth by 210 kb/d and says higher prices plus weaker economic outlook are already eroding demand. Demand destruction is a real cap if the shock does not worsen.
- Emergency stock draws of 400 mb and existing global inventories above 8.2 billion barrels create a meaningful buffer against prolonged price spikes.
- OPEC+ had agreed to start increasing production in April 2026. Even if modest, that is directionally bearish if transit conditions normalize.

## Ambiguous or mixed evidence
- Brent-based outlooks are highly relevant but not identical to CME WTI settlement; spread behavior and front-month settlement mechanics could matter.
- CME contract/resolution mechanics mean the exact active month and settlement timestamp matter, so a late fade in front-month WTI could break a superficially bullish headline narrative.
- War headlines can produce overshoots that do not hold into official settlement.

## Conflict between inputs
There is limited direct conflict between the major energy sources used. The main disagreement is interpretive: how persistent the disruption premium will be versus how quickly buffers, rerouting, and normalization compress it.

## Key assumptions
- The Hormuz/shipping disruption remains material long enough to keep the active CL contract near or above $90 into settlement.
- WTI tracks the conflict premium closely enough that Brent-focused evidence maps onto this market.
- Emergency stock releases and demand destruction do not neutralize the shock before settlement.

## Key uncertainties
- Exact persistence of insurance/shipping impairment through Hormuz
- Speed of producer recovery and restart of shut-in production
- How much of current price is durable physical tightness versus transient panic premium
- Exact front-month WTI settlement behavior at the relevant resolution date

## Disconfirming signals to watch
- Clear resumption of tanker traffic and insurance through Hormuz
- Fast narrowing in crude risk premium and front-month spreads
- Official settlement prints slipping back into the high 80s despite continued conflict headlines
- Producer guidance or market data showing curtailed supply recovering sooner than expected

## What would increase confidence
- Continued evidence of constrained tanker movement through Hormuz into the settlement window
- Additional credible estimates of sustained multi-mb/d supply disruption
- Official front-month CME settlements holding above $90 for multiple sessions approaching resolution

## Net update logic
The evidence supports a yes lean because the current disruption is physically large and directly relevant to front-month oil pricing. But the market-implied confidence looks somewhat rich because the bullish case rests heavily on persistence of a geopolitical/logistics shock that both EIA and IEA describe as highly path-dependent. The main risk is not that $90+ is implausible; it is that confidence is too high for a situation where normalization, emergency stocks, and softening demand could pull settlement below the threshold.

## Suggested downstream use
Use as decision-maker review input and as a check against overconfidence in the market-implied baseline.
