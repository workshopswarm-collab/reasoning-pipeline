# Summary

EIA's March 2026 STEO says the current price spike is being driven by Middle East war risk and effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz, but its base case still assumes disruptions ease and then global oil production outpaces consumption for the rest of 2026, leading to inventory builds and lower prices later in the year.

## Key facts extracted

- Brent rose from about $71/b on February 27 to $94/b on March 9 after the onset of military action in the Middle East.
- EIA says the Strait of Hormuz was effectively closed to most shipping traffic as of its forecast cut-off.
- EIA's base case assumes shut-in production peaks in early April and then gradually eases as transit resumes.
- EIA expects Brent to average $91/b in 2Q26.
- After flows normalize, EIA expects global oil production to outpace consumption, with inventories rising by 1.9 mb/d in 2026 and 3.0 mb/d in 2027.
- EIA expects Brent to fall to an average of $70/b in 4Q26 and $64/b in 2027.
- OPEC+ agreed on March 1 to begin increasing production in April 2026 by 206 kb/d.

## Evidence directly stated by source

The source explicitly frames the current oil rally as a large geopolitical risk premium tied to shipping disruption through Hormuz, not as a clean structural shortage. It also explicitly states that once flows are reestablished, inventories should build materially and prices should decline.

## What is uncertain

- Duration of Hormuz disruption.
- Whether military escalation damages infrastructure beyond shipping constraints.
- Whether OPEC+ supply policy changes in response to the crisis.
- Exact mapping from Brent outlook to CME WTI active-month settlement for late March.

## Why this source may matter

This is a high-credibility official baseline for separating transient war premium from underlying supply-demand balance. It is especially relevant because the market question is about a high absolute price threshold that may be reachable mainly through sustained disruption.

## Possible impact on the question

This source supports a variant view that a market heavily pricing $90+ may be overconfident if it is extrapolating the crisis premium too mechanically. The current move can justify elevated odds, but EIA's own base case still points to normalization and renewed surplus after the shock.

## Reliability notes

Government energy analysis; high credibility for directional baseline and scenario framing, but still model-dependent and explicitly assumption-sensitive in a fast-moving conflict environment.
