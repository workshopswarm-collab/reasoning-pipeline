# Summary

Independent bird references broadly agree that bald eagle incubation is roughly 34-36 days, with Animal Diversity Web describing about 35 days. This supports a narrow expected hatch window once egg-laying chronology is known, but it does not by itself justify near-certainty on one exact ET calendar day because incubation varies and the contract uses visible full emergence rather than first pip.

## Key facts extracted

- Cornell All About Birds lists incubation period: 34-36 days.
- Audubon lists incubation: 34-36 days.
- Animal Diversity Web says eggs are generally incubated for about 35 days.
- These sources describe normal biological timing but not this nest’s exact egg chronology.
- Hatchling condition at hatching is described, reinforcing that hatch is a biological process rather than a single easily timestamped instant.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- Incubation period is not a single exact number; it is a range or approximate duration.
- Timing varies somewhat by geography and nest context.

## What is uncertain

- Exact laying dates for the three eggs in the Traverse City nest were not independently established in this note.
- Whether parents began sustained incubation immediately after first, second, or third egg is not resolved here.
- The gap between first pip and full emergence is not quantified here, though contract wording makes that gap decision-relevant.

## Why this source may matter

These sources provide independent biological context against overconfidence. If the market is pricing one day above 94%, the relevant question is not whether April 11 is plausible, but whether the probability mass on adjacent days and rule/timestamp edge cases is small enough to justify that confidence.

## Possible impact on the question

This contextual evidence supports April 11 as plausible but argues against treating it as near-certain absent direct nest-specific chronology and direct live observation confirmation.
