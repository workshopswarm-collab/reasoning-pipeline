The key assumption carrying this market-implied review is that the contract’s phrase “general tariff rate” aggregates multiple China-wide tariffs that are both (a) generally applicable to imports from the PRC and (b) in effect at the checkpoint, even if they arise from different legal authorities.

Why this assumption seems justified:
- The contract explicitly says the general tariff rate includes any general tariff the U.S. imposes on all imports plus any tariff on top of that on Chinese imports, and its example is additive.
- The contract explicitly excludes item-specific exceptions or increases, which suggests the central distinction is general-vs-item-specific, not single-authority-vs-multi-authority.
- The White House executive-order chain indicates a 10% reciprocal China tariff remained in effect through November 10, 2026, and a separate 10% China duty tied to the synthetic-opioid emergency became effective on November 10, 2025.

Main residual ambiguity:
- The market asks for the “general tariff rate” rather than naming HTS headings or instructing an exact legal formula, so a resolver could in principle debate whether both China-wide duties should be summed. My view is that the contract text strongly favors summing them.
