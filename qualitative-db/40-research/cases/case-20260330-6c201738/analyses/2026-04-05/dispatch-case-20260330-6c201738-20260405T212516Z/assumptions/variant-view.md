# Core assumption

The operative market-relevant “general tariff rate” on China should be interpreted as the sum of broad-based layers that apply generally to Chinese imports, not just the all-country baseline.

## Why this assumption is needed

The contract explicitly says to include both:
- any general tariff the U.S. imposes on all imports, and
- any general tariff imposed on Chinese imports.

That wording makes the market about stacked general tariffs rather than the single global baseline. The critical assumption is therefore that the China-specific fentanyl IEEPA tariff and the China-specific reciprocal tariff count as general-to-China layers, while item-specific Section 232 or Section 301 product exceptions do not.

## Practical consequence

Under this reading:
- 10 percent global baseline alone would land inside the bracket,
- but 10 percent global baseline plus 20 percent China fentanyl tariff already pushes the general China tariff to at least 30 percent,
- and if the China reciprocal rate remains operative, the total is much higher still.

## Main fragility

The main fragility is not the arithmetic; it is the legal/administrative status of these layers by March 31, 2026. If later official action or a controlling legal development removes the China-specific layers while leaving only a 10 percent universal tariff, the market could flip into the 5 to 15 percent bracket.

## Why this is still acceptable

The official contract language strongly favors additive treatment of broad China-specific layers, and the official White House orders give explicit effective dates and pause structure. The remaining uncertainty is therefore about future policy persistence, not current interpretation of the stack.
