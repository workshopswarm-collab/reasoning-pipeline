# Summary

The evidence strongly supports a current official tariff stack on China that is well above the 5 to 15 percent bracket. The live variant view is not that the market should be Yes, but that the market’s near-certainty may still underweight one realistic path to Yes: if by March 31, 2026 the administration has rolled back or lost key China-specific tariff layers and only a broad 10 percent universal tariff remains in effect.

## Question being evaluated

Whether the U.S. general tariff rate on China, as defined by the market contract, will be between 5 percent and 15 percent on March 31, 2026 at 12:00 p.m. ET.

## Current lean

Lean No. Current official-policy stack points comfortably above 15 percent.

## Prior / starting view

Starting view was that the market was probably right but perhaps overconfident, because this contract is unusually sensitive to timing, pauses, and rate-stacking logic.

## Evidence supporting the claim

Evidence supporting Yes (5 to 15 percent) is limited and mostly conditional rather than direct.

1. A universal 10 percent tariff exists as an official baseline.
- Source: White House fact sheet and April 2 reciprocal tariff order.
- Why it matters causally: if China-specific layers are removed later while the baseline survives, the market could fall into the 5 to 15 bracket.
- Direct or indirect: direct for existence of baseline; indirect for March 2026 state.
- Weight: medium.

2. The case is legally and administratively fragile because multiple tariff layers depend on emergency authorities and later policy choices.
- Source: Tax Foundation summary noting IEEPA tariffs were later ruled illegal and that a temporary Section 122 tariff could replace some broad tariff authority.
- Why it matters causally: the March 2026 answer may depend on which tariff authorities remain operative rather than today’s headline rate.
- Direct or indirect: indirect/contextual.
- Weight: medium.

## Evidence against the claim

1. China-specific fentanyl tariff was raised to 20 percent by official order on March 3, 2025.
- Source: White House March 3, 2025 order.
- Why it matters causally: even before counting reciprocal China tariffs, this single China-specific general layer pushes the stack above the bracket if additive treatment is correct.
- Direct or indirect: direct.
- Weight: high.

2. Universal 10 percent tariff took effect April 5, 2025.
- Source: White House fact sheet and April 2, 2025 order.
- Why it matters causally: under contract wording, this stacks with China-specific general tariffs.
- Direct or indirect: direct.
- Weight: high.

3. China was explicitly excluded from the 90-day suspension that reverted other countries to the 10 percent baseline.
- Source: White House April 9, 2025 modification order.
- Why it matters causally: this is the clearest official signal that China was meant to remain in a separate, much higher tariff regime than the rest of the world.
- Direct or indirect: direct.
- Weight: high.

4. China reciprocal rate was raised to 125 percent effective April 10, 2025.
- Source: White House April 9, 2025 modification order.
- Why it matters causally: if still operative near resolution, the market is decisively No.
- Direct or indirect: direct.
- Weight: very high.

## Ambiguous or mixed evidence

- Tax Foundation’s 2026 summary is useful context but appears to describe later legal and policy developments through a modeling lens, not the contract’s named primary source of truth.
- A legal ruling against IEEPA tariffs could matter a lot, but only if it changes what is officially “in effect” by the resolution time and how Polymarket interprets any administration-versus-courts mismatch.

## Conflict between inputs

The main conflict is not between contemporaneous factual sources; it is between:
- official 2025 White House tariff orders showing a very high China stack, and
- later contextual analysis suggesting some IEEPA tariff layers were ruled unlawful and partially replaced.

This is mainly a timing and source-hierarchy conflict. It would be resolved by later official Trump-administration tariff guidance, HTSUS/implementation updates, or an explicit consensus of credible March 2026 reporting on what tariffs are actually in effect at noon ET on the resolution date.

## Key assumptions

- The market’s “general tariff rate” means adding broad all-country and broad China-specific tariff layers.
- Item-specific exceptions and increases do not count.
- Paused or merely announced tariffs do not count, so only operative layers at the resolution timestamp matter.

## Key uncertainties

- Whether the 125 percent China reciprocal layer survives to March 31, 2026.
- Whether the 20 percent China fentanyl layer survives to March 31, 2026.
- Whether legal invalidation or replacement authority leaves only a 10 percent broad tariff in force by the resolution timestamp.

## Disconfirming signals to watch

- Official administration rollback removing China-specific layers.
- Official administration statement or HTSUS update making China subject only to a 10 percent general tariff by March 31, 2026.
- Credible consensus reporting close to resolution that broad China-specific IEEPA layers are no longer in effect.

## What would increase confidence

- A later official White House or USTR order confirming the China-specific stack remains in force into 2026.
- A close-to-resolution customs or implementation source confirming the operative China general tariff stack at noon ET on March 31, 2026.

## Net update logic

The evidence moved the view from “maybe the contract could simplify to the 10 percent baseline” to “No is still the right call unless later policy action strips out China-specific general layers.” The most important update was the explicit official exclusion of China from the suspension that reverted most other countries to 10 percent, because it shows China was intentionally carved into a different tariff regime. What gets downweighted is any simplistic reading that the universal 10 percent baseline alone settles the market.

## Suggested downstream use

Use this as an input for forecast synthesis and risk review. The important takeaway is not simple contrarianism; it is that the main residual Yes path is a future rollback/legal-status path, not a present-tense misread of current official rates.
