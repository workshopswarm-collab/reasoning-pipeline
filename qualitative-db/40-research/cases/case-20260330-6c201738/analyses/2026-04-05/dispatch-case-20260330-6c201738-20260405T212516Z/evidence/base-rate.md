# Summary

The evidence nets to a high-probability but not fully settled view that the relevant general tariff rate will fall in the 5%-15% bracket, most plausibly around 10%, because official and contextual evidence point to a temporary Section 122 general surcharge as the operative broad tariff layer while item-specific tariffs are excluded.

## Question being evaluated

Will the U.S. general tariff rate on imports from China, as defined by the market contract, be between 5% and 15% on 2026-03-31 at 12:00 PM ET?

## Current lean

Lean yes / in range.

## Prior / starting view

Starting outside-view prior: broad statutory tariff brackets usually move less often than headlines suggest, and extreme market confidence still needs rule-audit because tariff markets are vulnerable to confusion between announced, effective, average, and item-specific rates.

## Evidence supporting the claim

- Federal Register result for the 2026-02-25 Section 122 temporary import surcharge proclamation.
  - Direct/primary.
  - Matters because it is official evidence that a general surcharge exists as a customs duty.
  - Weight: high.
- Tax Foundation 2026 tariff analysis.
  - Indirect/contextual.
  - Matters because it frames the live general-rate scenario as 10% while treating 15% as an alternative scenario and distinguishes statutory from effective rates.
  - Weight: medium.
- Market contract wording itself.
  - Direct for interpretation.
  - Matters because it excludes item-specific exceptions/increases and excludes paused/not-yet-effective announcements.
  - Weight: high.

## Evidence against the claim

- The official source surfaced via excerpt does not yet display the full operative text, exact percentage, or exact expiration/effective mechanics in the fetched snippet.
  - Direct uncertainty.
  - Weight: high.
- Tariff policy is politically changeable before the target date.
  - Indirect but important.
  - Weight: medium.
- If a new official administration action raises the general China-relevant rate above 15% before 2026-03-31, the current view breaks.
  - Forward-looking disconfirming path.
  - Weight: medium.

## Ambiguous or mixed evidence

- Tax Foundation discusses weighted average applied rates across the import basket, while the market uses a more stylized additive general-rate definition. Useful context, not settlement text.
- Official search and White House fetches were incomplete/low-yield in extraction, leaving some source-of-truth ambiguity around exact wording.

## Conflict between inputs

Main disagreement is not factual yet but interpretive:
- whether to read broad analytical sources about average applied tariff levels as strong evidence for the bracket
- versus requiring a stricter legal-text reading of the exact surcharge in effect on the date.

This is mostly source-hierarchy and rate-calculation conflict, not basic existence conflict.

## Key assumptions

- Section 122 surcharge remains in effect on the target date or is replaced by another general rate still within the bracket.
- No later official action pushes the general China-relevant rate above 15%.
- Section 232 and product-specific tariffs remain excluded under the contract.

## Key uncertainties

- Exact official percentage from full legal text.
- Exact expiration date mechanics relative to 2026-03-31.
- Possibility of superseding presidential action.

## Disconfirming signals to watch

- New White House / Federal Register proclamation changing the surcharge percentage.
- Official pause, expiration, or replacement before 2026-03-31.
- Credible consensus reporting that the governing source of truth uses a different additive logic.

## What would increase confidence

- Full text of the 2026-02-25 proclamation with exact rate and duration.
- A later USTR or White House summary confirming the rate still in effect near March 2026.
- A second independent legal/trade-policy explainer matching the same interpretation.

## Net update logic

The outside-view prior already favored a non-extreme broad tariff bracket rather than a dramatic product-specific reading. The evidence strengthened that prior by indicating a real official general surcharge and by showing independent contextual analysts centering the 10% case. The remaining uncertainty is mostly legal-text completeness and later-policy-change risk, not a strong competing current-rate case above 15%.

Use as an orchestrator synthesis input and as a checklist for later closer-to-resolution verification, especially around exact effective timing and any superseding official action.
