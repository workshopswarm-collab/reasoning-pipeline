# Assumption

The market's ~95.95% price appears to assume that Rumen Radev's new coalition has already consolidated enough dispersed anti-establishment and center-left support to finish first in a fragmented field, even without strong publicly retrievable polling evidence.

## Why this assumption matters

Without this assumption, the jump from "PB is a serious contender" to "PB is almost certain to win the most seats" is too large. The market price only makes sense if hidden or thinly distributed evidence points to broad electoral dominance rather than mere plausibility.

## What this assumption supports

- Treating PB as the default favorite.
- Explaining why the market is willing to look through PB's novelty and lack of parliamentary baseline seats.
- Viewing fragmented opposition as an advantage that compounds Radev's personal popularity.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Radev is nationally known and appears to be the central public face of PB.
- Independent media attention treated the launch as electorally serious rather than symbolic.
- Bulgaria's fragmented party system means first place can be achieved with a relatively modest vote share.

## What would falsify it

- Independent polling showing GERB-SDS or another bloc ahead of PB.
- Official or credible reporting that PB's organizational rollout is weaker than the market assumes.
- Evidence that Radev's appeal does not convert efficiently into party-list votes across constituencies.

## Early warning signs

- Late-campaign reports emphasizing list chaos, weak ground game, or low mobilization.
- Credible reporting of PB slipping behind GERB-SDS or another incumbent party in vote intention.
- Disputes over whether PB's coalition mechanics fit cleanly with the contract's listed abbreviation and resolution logic.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The market should move from "near lock" to merely "one of the leading contenders," and PB's probability of winning the most seats would likely fall materially because the field is too fragmented to justify a 95%+ confidence level without strong empirical support.
