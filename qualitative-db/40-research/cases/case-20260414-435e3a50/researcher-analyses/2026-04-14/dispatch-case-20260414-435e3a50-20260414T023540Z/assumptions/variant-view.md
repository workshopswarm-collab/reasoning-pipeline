# Assumption

The market is over-extrapolating the March 2026 cut, while the Bank of Russia still has enough inflation-risk sensitivity to pause in April even if the broader easing cycle remains intact.

## Why this assumption matters

The main variant case for disagreeing with a 91.5% implied probability of another cut depends on this. If the Bank is willing to pause between cuts, then the correct probability of an April decrease is high but materially below near-certainty.

## What this assumption supports

- An own probability estimate below the market price.
- A "roughly agree on direction, disagree on confidence" interpretation.
- The thesis that the strongest alternative to consensus is an April hold rather than a hike or a collapse of the easing narrative.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- The policy rate is already down to 15.0% after multiple consecutive cuts, so some easing has already been delivered.
- Secondary macro context still flags proinflationary risks, suggesting the remaining path of cuts need not be uninterrupted meeting to meeting.
- Central banks often pause within an easing cycle to assess inflation persistence, especially when the market has become highly confident in continued cuts.

## What would falsify it

- Credible official communication before 24 April clearly signaling that disinflation is ahead of plan and more easing is immediately warranted.
- Fresh official inflation/activity data showing a sharper-than-expected deterioration that makes a pause hard to justify.
- Broad, independent preview coverage showing overwhelming economist consensus for an April cut with little discussion of pause risk.

## Early warning signs

- Bank of Russia commentary downplaying inflation persistence.
- Clear deterioration in growth or demand indicators.
- Multiple independent previews converging on an immediate cut as the base case.

## What changes if this assumption fails

If pause risk is not real, the market's >90% confidence is more justified and my estimate should move closer to the market-implied probability.
