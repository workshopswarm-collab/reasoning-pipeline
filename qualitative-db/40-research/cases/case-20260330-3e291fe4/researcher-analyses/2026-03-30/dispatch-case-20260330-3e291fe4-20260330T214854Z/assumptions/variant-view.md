# Assumption

The market is implicitly assuming that a national AAA reading of **$3.990 on March 30** is effectively the same as a likely **$4.000+** print by March 31, even though the contract requires the actual threshold to be posted.

## Why this assumption matters

If that assumption is wrong, then the market is overpricing narrative closeness to the threshold. This contract is resolved by a precise published retail average, not by crude oil strength or general statements that gas is "basically at $4."

## What this assumption supports

- a high market-implied Yes probability around 0.775
- the belief that one remaining day is enough for residual upward momentum to finish the move
- treating $3.990 as almost equivalent to a Yes state

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- AAA itself said on March 26 that the national average could reach $4 in the coming days.
- The latest daily move from March 29 to March 30 was +1 cent.
- Energy/geopolitical pressure and seasonal demand are still directionally supportive.

## What would falsify it

- AAA prints anything below **4.000** on March 31.
- A March 31 AAA update shows stabilization or a minor downtick despite elevated oil and war risk.

## Early warning signs

- reporting increasingly emphasizes that the pace of increase has slowed
- state-level and regional averages flatten below round-number levels
- crude/oil headlines stop transmitting one-for-one into retail prices

## What changes if this assumption fails

The bullish market thesis collapses quickly because there is no more time left for gradual pass-through. The case becomes a clean example of a market overvaluing narrative momentum relative to an exact resolution threshold.
