# Assumption

The late-March national gasoline upswing has not already exhausted itself, so the AAA daily national average can still add roughly one more cent by the March 31 reading.

## Why this assumption matters

My bullish base-rate estimate depends less on a new shock arriving and more on simple continuation. If the uptrend has already peaked at exactly $3.99, then a market priced near 78% could still be too high. If continuation remains the dominant state, the market may be slightly low.

## What this assumption supports

- a probability estimate above the market-implied 77.5%
- the interpretation that one extra cent is a modest continuation move rather than a fresh breakout
- the use of recent daily and weekly trend data as meaningful evidence for the final day

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- AAA shows 3/30 at $3.990 versus 3/29 at $3.980.
- AAA said on 3/26 that the national average could reach $4 in the coming days.
- EIA's latest weekly trend shows a broad and steep national increase into late March.
- Reuters/AAA context suggests the upstream oil and conflict backdrop remained supportive of elevated retail prices late in the month.

## What would falsify it

- AAA's 3/31 print remains below $4.000
- clear evidence that retail prices flattened nationally despite still-high spot/oil levels
- a late-day reversal in the AAA national average publication convention or interpretation that leaves 3.990 short of the target bracket

## Early warning signs

- AAA state-level updates showing broad stabilization rather than further increases
- oil/gasoline futures retracing sharply before the final daily read-through
- evidence that the 3/30 national average represented a local peak rather than an intermediate step

## What changes if this assumption fails

If the uptrend stalls exactly below the threshold, then the market's confidence was too high and the correct base-rate takeaway becomes that being one cent short is still a real barrier when a daily average must actually print the number.
