# Summary

The national gasoline spike is real, but the best current evidence says the retail pass-through may be **maturing rather than accelerating**. That matters because the market needs one final AAA push through a hard threshold in essentially one remaining daily print.

## Key facts extracted

- Reuters snippet from 2026-03-11: the national average retail gasoline price crossed **$3.50** that week, the highest since May 2024.
- AAA Newsroom on 2026-03-05: national average jumped to **$3.25**, with Middle East conflict and normal spring transition both cited.
- AAA Newsroom on 2026-03-26: national average reached **$3.98**, up **$1.00** from a month earlier.
- EIA weekly U.S. regular gasoline prices: **3.502** on 03/09, **3.720** on 03/16, **3.961** on 03/23.
- KVUE/AP relay on 2026-03-30: national average was **$3.990**, only **1 cent** above Sunday and up just **3.4 cents** from a week earlier.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- The major repricing already occurred earlier in March.
- Weekly official retail data and AAA reporting show a strong rise, but the latest step-up has become much smaller than the earlier shock phase.
- Seasonal demand and geopolitical oil pressure are active, but they are no longer producing the same explosive day-to-day retail acceleration seen earlier in the month.

## What is uncertain

- Retail gas can still move another cent nationally if crude and wholesale pressure keep transmitting through.
- We do not have a same-day forward-looking wholesale gasoline or rack-price series embedded in this note.
- EIA is weekly and therefore less precise than AAA for the exact final threshold event.

## Why this source may matter

This source set helps separate "big March move already happened" from "another threshold-crossing increment is likely tomorrow." Those are not the same question, and the market may be blurring them.

## Possible impact on the question

This evidence should push against a very high-confidence Yes view. The retail series has already done most of its violent repricing; the remaining question is whether the national average still has enough residual momentum to add the last penny in time.

## Reliability notes

- EIA and AAA are strong source classes for U.S. retail gasoline tracking.
- Reuters snippet is useful for timeline anchoring but weaker here because it is only snippet-level, not full text.
