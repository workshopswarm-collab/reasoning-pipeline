# Assumption

The market's high implied confidence assumes the April 21, 2026 referendum will actually proceed on schedule and remain politically viable despite the contract's explicit legal-challenge and postponement sensitivity.

## Why this assumption matters

A very high market price can be wrong even if the amendment would likely win a clean vote, because the contract resolves no if the vote never occurs by November 3, 2026 or is definitively cancelled. That makes process continuity almost as important as ballot popularity.

## What this assumption supports

- any estimate materially above 50%
- any view close to the market's 89% implied probability
- any claim that yes is close to settled absent polling

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- The Virginia Department of Elections officially lists the measure for the April 21, 2026 special election.
- The official explanation presents the measure as operationally ready, with a proposed map already approved by the General Assembly subject to voter approval.
- Special-election statewide referenda usually happen once formally scheduled unless blocked or delayed by litigation or politics.

## What would falsify it

- a court order staying or postponing the referendum
- credible reporting that pending legal challenges are likely to remove the measure from the ballot
- official Virginia election materials changing the election date or status
- a breakdown in administrative readiness that pushes the vote past the contract deadline

## Early warning signs

- court filings or hearing dates centered on ballot access or constitutional validity
- state election guidance changing from firm scheduling language to contingency language
- credible local reporting emphasizing unresolved litigation rather than campaign persuasion
- lack of independent media confirmation that the vote is proceeding normally as election day approaches

## What changes if this assumption fails

If the schedule or ballot access becomes unstable, the market should be repriced sharply lower because the contract wording makes non-occurrence by the deadline a no even if underlying public sentiment would otherwise favor passage.
