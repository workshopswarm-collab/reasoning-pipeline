# Assumption

The market's 0.89 price is implicitly assuming that the referendum both proceeds on schedule and that Yes has a real but not fully public advantage in turnout, persuasion, or private polling.

## Why this assumption matters

If that assumption is wrong, the market is overconfident: legal friction or a stronger-than-priced fairness backlash could push the true probability much lower.

## What this assumption supports

- A still-favorable but sub-market estimate for passage.
- The view that the market is probably directionally right on Yes but too compressed toward certainty.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- The referendum is officially on the ballot and early voting is underway.
- Major Democratic validators are publicly urging Yes and emphasizing the amendment's temporary nature.
- The market may be incorporating polling or field data not visible in the public sources reviewed here.

## What would falsify it

- Credible public polling showing No ahead or Yes near coin-flip.
- A fresh court action that halts, delays, or functionally nullifies the April 21 vote before election day.
- Authoritative reporting that early-vote composition materially favors No.

## Early warning signs

- More reporting centered on voter confusion or ballot-language backlash.
- Additional legal rulings undermining implementation or election timing.
- Cross-outlet evidence that anti-gerrymandering messaging is dominating swing or independent voters.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The market should be marked down sharply from extreme-confidence territory; a mid-60s or lower estimate would become more plausible.
