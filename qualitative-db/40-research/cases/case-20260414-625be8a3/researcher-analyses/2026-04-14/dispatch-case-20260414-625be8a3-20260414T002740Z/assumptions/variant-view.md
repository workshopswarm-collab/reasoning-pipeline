# Assumption

The market is modestly overpricing straightforward voter approval because it underweights procedural risk from legal challenge, postponement, or low-salience special-election turnout.

## Why this assumption matters

Without this assumption, the official scheduling page plus favorable ballot framing could justify a market price near current levels. The variant thesis depends on the idea that process risk and turnout fragility are still material enough to prevent a near-certainty view.

## What this assumption supports

- A probability estimate below the market's 0.89 implied probability.
- A view that the best credible disagreement is not "the amendment is likely to lose badly," but "the market may be too confident because multiple conditions must all hold."

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- The market contract itself foregrounds pending legal challenges and deadline-dependent No paths.
- Special elections typically create turnout and salience risk relative to general-election environments.
- No strong independent evidence was found in this run showing overwhelming public support, robust elite consensus, or absent litigation risk sufficient to justify near-lock odds.

## What would falsify it

- Credible reporting that the legal challenges have been resolved or neutralized with little remaining postponement risk.
- Independent polling or campaign evidence showing overwhelming statewide approval with organized bipartisan support.
- Administrative confirmation that voting logistics and legal posture are stable enough that the only meaningful remaining uncertainty is voter preference on election day.

## Early warning signs

- Courts declining to disturb the special-election schedule.
- Major news outlets reporting little serious opposition beyond symbolic dissent.
- Early voting or public polling indicating broad support well above 55%.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The estimate should move materially upward, potentially toward or above the market, because the remaining path would mostly be ordinary majority approval on a ballot question with fairness-oriented language.
