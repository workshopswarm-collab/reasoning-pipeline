# Assumption
The market’s explicit statement that qualifying legislation includes Public Law 118-49 means the substantive reauthorization condition has already been satisfied, leaving only operational or interpretive settlement risk.

## Why this assumption matters
This assumption is carrying almost the entire bullish view. If it is wrong, then the market reverts from a near-settled contract-reading case to a live congressional timing and enactment-risk case.

## What this assumption supports
- A probability estimate materially above the current market price.
- The view that legislative failure before April 19, 2026 is not the main downside.
- The conclusion that source-of-truth ambiguity and contract handling are the key risks.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption
- The market text itself expressly says qualifying legislation includes Public Law 118-49.
- The contract requires passage by both chambers and signature by the President, which a Public Law by definition has already satisfied.
- Contextual statutory material indicates Section 702 was extended in 2024, which is consistent with the named Public Law reference.

## What would falsify it
- Official Congress.gov or other government source showing Public Law 118-49 did not in fact reauthorize FISA Title VII including Section 702.
- A clarified market ruling that Public Law 118-49 is excluded despite the current wording.
- Evidence that the market was intended to ask only about a future 2026 reauthorization vehicle and that the current text is erroneous.

## Early warning signs
- Persistent failure of official trackers to map Public Law 118-49 cleanly to the market description.
- Market comments or support clarifications indicating unresolved interpretation disputes.
- Credible legal or congressional reporting saying the 2024 law does not count for the contract as written.

## What changes if this assumption fails
The estimate should drop sharply because then Congress would still need another qualifying enactment by April 19, 2026, reintroducing timing, politics, and veto-process risk.
