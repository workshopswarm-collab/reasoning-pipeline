# Assumption

Polymarket resolution will treat the contract's explicit statement that qualifying legislation includes Public Law 118-49 as controlling, even though the page also references a Congress.gov tracker that appears tied to a different legislative vehicle.

## Why this assumption matters

The whole market-implied thesis depends on this. If the explicit inclusion controls, `Yes` is already strongly favored on the merits. If the linked tracker is interpreted as the operative source requiring a fresh 119th Congress enactment, the market price would be far too high.

## What this assumption supports

- A high own probability estimate.
- A view that the market is mostly efficient rather than complacent.
- A claim that remaining risk is interpretive, not legislative.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- The market text expressly names Public Law 118-49 as qualifying legislation.
- Public Law 118-49 is official enacted law and directly reforms FISA section 702.
- Markets usually key off explicit resolution text more than imperfectly matched reference links.

## What would falsify it

- Clear platform guidance that only a new 119th Congress bill counts.
- A resolution precedent showing that the named inclusion of Public Law 118-49 is ignored.
- Official market clarification narrowing the source-of-truth logic to the referenced tracker in a way that excludes the 2024 law.

## Early warning signs

- Staff clarification or moderator comments emphasizing H.R. 22 or a new enactment path.
- Sustained market drift downward despite no new legal threat to section 702's operative status.
- Community dispute centered on whether the explicit inclusion was an error.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The market should be repriced much lower because the question becomes whether Congress will pass and sign another qualifying reauthorization vehicle before April 19, 2026.
