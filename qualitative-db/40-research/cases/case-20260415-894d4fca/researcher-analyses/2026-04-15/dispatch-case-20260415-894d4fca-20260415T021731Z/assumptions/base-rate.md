# Assumption

The market will treat Public Law 118-49 as resolution-qualifying legislation because the contract expressly says qualifying legislation includes Public Law 118-49 and the enacted law in fact continued Section 702 before expiration.

## Why this assumption matters

The base-rate view is dominated by whether the event has effectively already happened rather than by any fresh 2026 congressional action.

## What this assumption supports

- A very high Yes estimate.
- The conclusion that the market is only slightly underpricing an event that appears already satisfied by 2024 legislation.
- A decision to stop searching for speculative 2026 legislative catalysts.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- The market description itself names Public Law 118-49 as qualifying legislation.
- Official law text shows Public Law 118-49 was enacted on April 20, 2024.
- Current codification of 50 U.S.C. 1881a indicates Section 702 remained in force after the 2024 law rather than expiring unreauthorized.

## What would falsify it

- A clear Congress.gov or other official source showing that Public Law 118-49 did not actually extend Title VII / Section 702 before expiry.
- A formal market clarification excluding Public Law 118-49 despite the current contract text.
- Official legal authority showing the relevant sunset expired before the 2024 law could count.

## Early warning signs

- Credible dispute over whether the contract intended only future reauthorizations after market creation.
- Resolution-source language being interpreted in a narrower way than the market text currently suggests.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The probability would fall sharply and the case would revert to an ordinary legislative-process forecast with much larger uncertainty and lower base rate.
