# Assumption

Absent strong new direct evidence, a title that is not visible on the currently published US Netflix weekly movie chart and has no verified release-surge evidence should be treated as unlikely to jump to #1 on the next weekly update.

## Why this assumption matters

The market is priced at 90%, so the run needs a disciplined outside-view anchor rather than accepting a vivid title-specific narrative without supporting chart or release evidence.

## What this assumption supports

- A probability estimate materially below market.
- Skepticism toward a 90% price without direct evidence that "Thrash" is dominating the 4/6-4/12 US weekly window.
- Treating current published Netflix chart status as meaningful baseline context.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Netflix's own current US movies chart for 3/30/26-4/5/26 shows Anaconda at #1 and does not show Thrash in the published top 10.
- Weekly #1 status usually requires either strong carryover momentum or a major fresh release pulse.
- Before the 4/14 publication, this run did not verify either condition for Thrash from authoritative surfaces.

## What would falsify it

- Direct evidence that Thrash released into the 4/6-4/12 window with major US viewing traction.
- A Netflix or equivalent direct title surface showing Thrash was newly available and heavily promoted during the covered week.
- The 4/14 Netflix Tudum update itself listing Thrash at #1.

## Early warning signs

- Verified availability start during the covered week.
- Independent chart/context sources consistently showing Thrash dominating daily US Netflix rankings.
- Other current leaders dropping sharply before the reporting window closes.

## What changes if this assumption fails

If Thrash had a verified major release surge, the outside-view prior would give way quickly and the estimate should move substantially upward, possibly toward or above market.
