# Assumption

The market label "Thrash" cleanly refers to the same title Netflix will use on the US Top 10 movie chart for the 4/6/26 - 4/12/26 reporting window.

## Why this assumption matters

A very high market price only makes sense if traders are correctly mapping the market answer choice to Netflix's eventual charted title. If that mapping is wrong, confidence can be badly overstated even if traders have the broad entertainment thesis right.

## What this assumption supports

- Any estimate above roughly coin-flip for "Thrash."
- The idea that current market confidence mostly reflects content-performance expectations rather than labeling or contract risk.
- The thesis that resolution risk is low despite the page not yet showing the relevant week.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Polymarket lists "Thrash" as the dominant outcome.
- Entertainment weekly-top-chart markets often center on a clear new release expected to lead the next reporting window.
- It is plausible traders are keying off off-platform release/performance expectations.

## What would falsify it

- Netflix publishes the 4/6/26 - 4/12/26 US movies chart and the #1 title is not labeled "Thrash."
- Netflix never surfaces a US movie title called "Thrash" on its Top 10 chart or searchable title surfaces.
- A different film (for example Anaconda, Jumanji: Welcome to the Jungle, or another listed option) finishes #1.

## Early warning signs

- The authoritative Netflix page currently shows no visible support for "Thrash" in the latest published top 10.
- The relevant week had not yet posted at time of analysis, leaving traders exposed to timing and mapping error.
- The market was priced at an extreme despite thin directly visible public confirmation on the governing source.

## What changes if this assumption fails

If title mapping fails, the market should be treated as carrying much more operational/contract risk than the price implies, and the probability on "Thrash" should be marked down materially.
