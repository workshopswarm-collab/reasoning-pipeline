# Summary

This note captures the main identifiable near-term catalyst calendar item and a contextual ETF-flow signal relevant to whether BTC could revisit sub-72k before the market resolves.

## Key facts extracted

- The Federal Reserve Beige Book publication page lists April 15, 2026 as a scheduled release date.
- The Beige Book page is an authoritative Fed surface for the fact that the release is scheduled on April 15.
- A CoinMarketCap article summarizing Bloomberg analyst commentary says U.S. spot Bitcoin ETFs have seen sustained outflows since October 2025 even while cumulative net inflows remain large, and that Bitcoin was trading around the mid-60k area in the broader 2026 drawdown context.
- Search results also surfaced a same-day Lookonchain/Farside-style ETF flow update indicating notable April 14 outflows, but this was used only as weak contextual color rather than a core factual pillar.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- The Fed directly states April 15 as a Beige Book date.

## What is uncertain

- This note does not verify the exact intraday release time from the Fed page.

- ETF-flow reporting here is contextual and not fully independent because it leans on analyst aggregation.

## Why this source may matter

For a catalyst-hunter, the key question is what scheduled event could still force repricing before April 16 noon ET. The Beige Book is one identifiable macro calendar item inside that window, while ETF-flow discussion is a known sentiment/risk channel.

## Possible impact on the question

These catalysts matter mainly as downside volatility risks. They do not themselves create a strong positive catalyst requirement because BTC is already above the strike. The relevant question is whether any scheduled macro shock or renewed flow-driven selling can produce a roughly 2-3% drawdown by resolution.

## Reliability notes

Fed schedule reliability is high for the existence of the date. ETF-flow context is medium reliability and lower independence than the Binance direct data, so it should be weighted as background rather than settlement-grade evidence.
