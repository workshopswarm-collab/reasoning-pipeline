# Core assumption under review

The working Yes view assumes that at least one March UAE incident satisfies all contract conditions simultaneously, not just most of them separately.

## Fragile premises carrying the Yes case

1. **Impact premise:** BBC's Fujairah / Dubai reporting reflects successful Iranian drone impacts on UAE territory rather than an effects chain arising only from interception or falling debris.
2. **Attribution premise:** The same class of incidents can be tied to Iranian military forces / Iranian territory strongly enough for consensus credible reporting, not merely to a broader Iran-UAE war environment.
3. **Window premise:** The qualifying impacts occurred inside the March ET window and are date-confirmable under the contract's reporting deadline logic.
4. **Consensus premise:** A later resolver would treat BBC plus UAE-citing local outlets as enough of a consensus bundle rather than demanding a cleaner AP/Reuters-style cross-confirmation.

## Main failure mode

The market may be overconfident because traders are bundling together three different things:
- Iranian launches toward the UAE,
- interceptions and shrapnel effects inside the UAE,
- qualifying direct impacts by Iranian forces on UAE soil.

Only the third counts.

## What would most quickly invalidate the working Yes view

- A clearer full-text source showing the Fujairah / Dubai fires were consequences of interceptions rather than successful impacts.
- Evidence that the only cleanly attributable March events were interceptions and offshore/tanker incidents rather than impacts on UAE ground territory.
- A resolver-level discussion indicating that the consensus-reporting standard was not met for a qualifying UAE impact.

## Net assessment

This is still more likely Yes than No, but confidence should sit below the market because the proof chain is assembled across multiple sources and one weak link — especially impact-versus-interception — could break the thesis.
