# Assumption

The market’s 70% price is implicitly assuming that a 3,200-theater wide launch for this Warner Bros. horror title will translate into a mid-tier opening close to, but not far outside, the 10–15m range.

## Why this assumption matters

Without a directly inspected public gross forecast, the case depends on whether release scale plus genre/distributor context is enough to justify the market’s confidence in the 10–15m bracket.

## What this assumption supports

- A roughly market-aligned probability rather than a strong fade.
- Treating 10–15m as the modal bracket rather than an obviously stale or overextended one.
- Giving more weight to distribution breadth and generic horror-launch priors than to absent direct tracking data.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- The Numbers confirms a **wide** April 17 domestic launch by Warner Bros.
- The Numbers editorial context projects **3,200 theaters**, which is a substantial launch footprint.
- A 10–15m opening implies roughly **$3.1k–$4.7k per theater** on 3,200 locations, which is plausible for a non-franchise-explosive but still broadly released horror film.
- The contract’s bracket uses the finalized 3-day weekend figure, typically including Thursday previews, reducing one source of ambiguity versus headline Friday-to-Sunday chatter.

## What would falsify it

- Evidence of much weaker-than-expected launch conditions, such as a sharply reduced theater count, poor previews, or trade reporting pointing materially below 10m.
- Evidence of stronger-than-expected buzz/tracking suggesting a clear path above 15m.
- A settlement-interpretation surprise that changes what The Numbers weekend figure includes.

## Early warning signs

- Theater count materially under 3,200 by opening day.
- Negative critical/audience indicators that imply a soft opening rather than merely weak legs.
- Trade commentary framing the title as a low-single-digit or high-teens opener.

## What changes if this assumption fails

If launch scale is not translating into normal mid-tier horror demand, then the probability of landing in 10–15m should fall quickly because the contract is bracket-specific, not a directional success/failure question. If upside signals emerge instead, the main risk is overshooting the bracket rather than missing release entirely.
