# Assumption

The market's very high Yes price assumes that a qualifying >1000 BTC announcement either already occurred through official Strategy/Saylor channels during the April 7-13 ET window or will do so with minimal residual timing/attribution risk before cutoff.

## Why this assumption matters

A narrow date-window announcement market can fail even when the underlying corporate buying behavior is directionally obvious if the announcement timing, wording, or source attribution misses the exact contract language.

## What this assumption supports

- A high-probability Yes estimate.
- Rough agreement with the market despite acknowledging residual tail risk.
- Downweighting generic Bitcoin-treasury enthusiasm relative to contract mechanics.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Strategy maintains an official BTC purchases page on its current corporate domain.
- The market itself cites official information from MicroStrategy/Strategy or Michael Saylor as the governing source of truth.
- These weekly MSTR/Strategy BTC-announcement markets are often posted against a background of frequent corporate purchase disclosures, so a high Yes prior is not arbitrary.

## What would falsify it

- No official Strategy or Michael Saylor announcement of a >1000 BTC purchase is visible by end of April 13 ET.
- The official announcement, if any, falls outside the specified window.
- The company posts only holdings changes or contextual information without a clean qualifying purchase announcement.

## Early warning signs

- No visible update on official channels late in the resolution window.
- Ambiguous wording that reports aggregate holdings but not a distinct qualifying purchase.
- Reliance on third-party reporting without a traceable official source post.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The case should move sharply toward No, and the main lesson becomes that extreme market confidence can still be vulnerable to contract-mechanics/timing error even in apparently routine weekly treasury-buy cases.
