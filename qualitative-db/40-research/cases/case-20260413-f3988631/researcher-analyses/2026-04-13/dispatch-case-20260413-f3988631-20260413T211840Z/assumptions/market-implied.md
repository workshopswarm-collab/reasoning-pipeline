# Assumption

The market's ~80% pricing assumes that Velasco's first-round lead and broader anti-establishment/change appeal will transfer into a runoff majority without major anti-Velasco consolidation behind Otto Ritter.

## Why this assumption matters

If that transfer/consolidation assumption is wrong, an 80% probability is too high for a two-candidate runoff.

## What this assumption supports

- treating Velasco as a clear favorite
- accepting a high but not overwhelming win probability
- interpreting the market as broadly efficient rather than stale or euphoric

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Velasco appears to have led first-round preliminary reporting.
- He entered the race with post-2025 national visibility.
- Markets often compress toward the apparent frontrunner once a runoff field is known, especially when public reporting is thin.

## What would falsify it

- credible reporting that eliminated first-round blocs are consolidating behind Ritter
- late local polling showing a close or reversed race
- official/reporting evidence of weak Velasco organization or turnout conversion in the runoff

## Early warning signs

- narrowing market price without new settlement data
- local reports emphasizing anti-Velasco coalition building
- significant scandal or campaign-operation problems affecting Velasco late

## What changes if this assumption fails

The appropriate estimate drops materially toward a much closer runoff, likely closer to the 55-65% range rather than ~80%.
