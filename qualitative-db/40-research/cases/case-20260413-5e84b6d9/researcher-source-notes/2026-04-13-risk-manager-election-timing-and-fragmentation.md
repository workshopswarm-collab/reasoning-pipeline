# Summary
These sources clarify the election calendar and underscore why a post-election coalition bottleneck is the key risk to an extreme Radev-for-PM market price.

## Key facts extracted
- The Sofia Globe says the election is on April 19, 2026 and that the Central Electoral Commission has until April 23 to announce seat distribution and until April 26 to announce elected MPs.
- It notes the newly elected National Assembly must be convened within a month following the election.
- Robert Schuman describes Bulgaria as entering its eighth parliamentary election since 2021 after repeated coalition failures.
- Robert Schuman says Progressive Bulgaria is favored in polling but explicitly asks with whom Radev could form a coalition and notes at least three parties may be needed for a majority coalition.
- Robert Schuman cites a March poll with Progressive Bulgaria around 21.1%, i.e. first place but far from a majority on its own.

## Evidence directly stated by source
- Election and reporting timeline is explicit.
- Fragmented party system and repeated instability are explicit.
- Polling leadership for Radev's coalition is explicit but modest rather than dominant.

## What is uncertain
- Whether late polling remained stable into April.
- Whether first place is enough to secure an investiture deal.
- Whether a rival coalition could exclude Radev even if Progressive Bulgaria leads.

## Why this source may matter
The contract is not asking who wins the most votes; it asks who is officially sworn in as PM after the election, excluding caretakers. In a fragmented parliamentary system, that distinction is crucial.

## Possible impact on the question
This source bundle is the main disconfirming input against a 90%+ Radev probability: it supports the idea that Radev is a leading contender, but it also shows a structurally high risk that no quick swearing-in occurs or that coalition bargaining produces someone else.

## Reliability notes
- The Sofia Globe factfile is useful for procedural timing and election-administration details.
- Robert Schuman is useful as a contextual summary of fragmentation and coalition math, but polling/analysis should be treated as contextual rather than authoritative settlement evidence.
- Independence is moderate because both are secondary/contextual rather than primary state sources.
