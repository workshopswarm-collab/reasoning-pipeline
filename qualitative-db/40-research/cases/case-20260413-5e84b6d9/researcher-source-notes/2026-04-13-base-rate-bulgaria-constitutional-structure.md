# Summary

This note captures the structural prior from Bulgaria’s parliamentary constitutional design.

## Key facts extracted

- Bulgaria is a parliamentary republic.
- The prime minister is elected by the majority party or coalition in the National Assembly.
- The president is head of state rather than head of government.
- The president can play a role in government formation and scheduling elections, but does not ordinarily become prime minister by default.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- Prime minister selection depends on parliamentary majority or coalition support.
- The president and prime minister are institutionally distinct offices with different functions.

## What is uncertain

- This source is structural rather than current-cycle specific.
- It does not answer whether Rumen Radev now has a viable party apparatus or coalition path in 2026.

## Why this source may matter

This is the core outside-view anchor. In parliamentary systems, the next prime minister usually comes from post-election coalition bargaining and legislative arithmetic, not directly from a former president’s prominence.

## Possible impact on the question

Absent strong evidence that Radev leads or dominates a coalition-capable electoral vehicle, the structural base rate is lower than the current market price implies.

## Reliability notes

Strong for institutional structure; weaker for current political positioning and live candidate odds.
