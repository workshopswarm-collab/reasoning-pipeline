# Assumption

Rumen Radev does not currently possess a coalition-capable parliamentary vehicle strong enough to make him the likeliest post-election sworn-in prime minister.

## Why this assumption matters

The base-rate case against the market’s 90%+ pricing depends on parliamentary arithmetic still dominating over personal prominence.

## What this assumption supports

- A materially lower-than-market estimate for Radev.
- A view that "Other" or another coalition figure remains live.
- Skepticism toward candidate-specific certainty absent stronger coalition evidence.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

Bulgaria is a parliamentary republic in which the prime minister is typically produced by a majority party or coalition in the National Assembly. The country has also had repeated failed coalition formations and caretaker periods since 2021, which raises friction for any single named outcome.

## What would falsify it

- Credible independent reporting that Radev leads a major electoral list or coalition with real plurality/coalition-building capacity.
- Polling or seat projections showing his bloc clearly dominating coalition formation.
- Official post-election coalition negotiations centering on Radev as the agreed governing figure.

## Early warning signs

- Repeated credible references to Radev as head of a serious parliamentary project.
- Endorsements from coalition-relevant parties.
- Reporting that alternative coalition leaders are dropping away while Radev becomes the focal compromise candidate.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The probability should rise sharply, potentially toward market levels, because the structural objection would weaken substantially.
