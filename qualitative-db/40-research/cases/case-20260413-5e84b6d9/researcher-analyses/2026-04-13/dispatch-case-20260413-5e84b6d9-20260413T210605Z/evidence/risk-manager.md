# Summary
Radev looks like the leading named candidate, but the market appears to be pricing a cleaner conversion from electoral strength to formal swearing-in than the evidence justifies.

## Question being evaluated
Will Rumen Radev be the next individual officially sworn in as Prime Minister of Bulgaria following the April 19, 2026 parliamentary election, excluding caretaker or interim prime ministers?

## Current lean
Lean yes, but with materially more risk than the market price implies.

## Prior / starting view
Starting view was that a 90%+ market price was probably too high for a fragmented parliamentary system unless there were already strong signs of a near-locked coalition.

## Evidence supporting the claim
- Official and campaign reporting indicate Radev resigned the presidency and is actively leading Progressive Bulgaria into the election. Direct, high weight.
- Contextual reporting says Progressive Bulgaria is the polling favorite. Indirect but important, medium weight.
- Radev's own messaging is explicitly framed around forming a government after the election, which supports intent and seriousness. Indirect, low-medium weight.

## Evidence against the claim
- The contract requires formal swearing-in; electoral popularity alone does not count. Direct/rules-based, very high weight.
- Bulgaria has experienced repeated coalition failures and this is its eighth parliamentary election since 2021. Contextual, high weight.
- Contextual reporting suggests Progressive Bulgaria may lead with only around one-fifth of the vote and may need at least three parties for a majority coalition. Indirect but causally central, high weight.
- No source reviewed shows a secured coalition agreement or a clear majority path already in place. Negative evidence / missing confirmation, medium-high weight.

## Ambiguous or mixed evidence
- Radev's popularity cuts both ways: it helps him top the field but may also increase the incentive for rivals to coordinate against a Radev-led cabinet.
- Anti-establishment energy may improve his bargaining power, but a fragmented anti-status-quo parliament can also worsen deadlock.

## Conflict between inputs
The main conflict is not factual but weighting-based. Some public reporting treats Radev as the clear favorite because he leads polls; the risk-manager view gives more weight to institutional conversion risk between election day and swearing-in.

## Key assumptions
- Progressive Bulgaria remains competitive enough on election day to claim the initiative.
- At least one plausible coalition path to a Radev-led cabinet exists.
- No rival coalition or prolonged deadlock blocks formal appointment.

## Key uncertainties
- Seat distribution after threshold effects.
- Willingness of likely partners to support Radev personally.
- Speed of post-election bargaining and whether a non-caretaker PM emerges promptly.

## Disconfirming signals to watch
- Poll deterioration for Progressive Bulgaria.
- Public coalition exclusions by key parties.
- Reporting after the election that another figure has the stronger investiture path.

## What would increase confidence
- Fresh independent polling showing a wider Radev lead.
- Evidence of coalition openness from at least one or two plausible partners.
- Official or highly credible reporting pointing to Radev as the likely mandate-holder with a workable majority route.

## Net update logic
The evidence supports Radev as the lead contender, so this is not a bear case on his viability. But the decisive update is that the contract resolves on formal swearing-in in a system with chronic fragmentation. That keeps the estimate clearly below the market's extreme confidence.

## Suggested downstream use
Use as synthesis input and decision-maker review input, with special attention to contract wording, coalition arithmetic, and whether later researchers can verify a concrete majority path.
