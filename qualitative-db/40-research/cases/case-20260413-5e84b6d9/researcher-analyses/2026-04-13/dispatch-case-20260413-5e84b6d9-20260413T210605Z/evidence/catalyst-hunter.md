# Summary

Radev appears to be the central contender, but the decisive catalyst is whether post-election coalition bargaining produces his formal swearing-in rather than another deadlock or an alternative PM.

## Question being evaluated

Will Rumen Radev be the next individual officially sworn in as Prime Minister of Bulgaria following the 19 April 2026 parliamentary election, excluding caretaker PMs?

## Current lean

Lean yes, but materially below the market price.

## Prior / starting view

Starting baseline was the market’s 90.35%, which implicitly treats Radev as close to a done deal.

## Evidence supporting the claim

- Reuters-context reporting indicates Radev resigned the presidency, launched Progressive Bulgaria, and entered the election as a serious top-tier contender. Weight: high, but not dispositive.
- Public contextual reporting suggests his alliance is polling strongly / possibly first. Weight: medium because accessible reporting quality is limited and polling details are sparse.
- Official Bulgarian government materials confirm the country is in a caretaker phase headed by PM Andrey Gurov and preparing for the 19 Apr election. This reinforces that a post-election regular PM must still emerge. Weight: medium for timing and contract interpretation.

## Evidence against the claim

- Contract wording requires formal swearing-in; plurality, popularity, or leading polls do not count by themselves. Weight: very high.
- Bulgaria’s recent repeated snap-election instability raises nontrivial coalition-formation risk. Weight: high.
- Current PM is explicitly a caretaker PM, and caretaker PMs do not count. This highlights that resolution depends on a separate later institutional event. Weight: high.
- If no qualifying PM is appointed by 31 Mar 2027 11:59 PM ET, the market resolves Other. That creates deadlock risk not reflected in a near-certainty price. Weight: high.

## Ambiguous or mixed evidence

- Radev’s popularity helps him lead the field, but a strong anti-establishment profile can complicate coalition assembly.
- The election itself is imminent, but the resolving event may occur well after election day.

## Conflict between inputs

The main conflict is not factual but weighting-based: media context makes Radev look like the focal favorite, while the contract and parliamentary mechanics make “favorite” less than “near-certain resolver.”

## Key assumptions

- Bulgaria’s fragmentation still matters after the vote.
- No official pre-election coalition lock has already made Radev overwhelmingly likely to be sworn in.
- The relevant repricing catalyst is coalition math / nomination path, not just election-week momentum.

## Key uncertainties

- Exact seat distribution.
- Whether Radev can secure a governing majority or tolerated minority arrangement.
- How quickly a regular government can be formed after the election.

## Disconfirming signals to watch

- Independent reporting of a coalition agreement centered on Radev.
- Official statements from major parties backing a Radev-led cabinet.
- A rapid post-election nomination and investiture timetable.

## What would increase confidence

- Clean official CEC registration / polling / turnout data accessible without ambiguity.
- Multiple independent reports showing viable coalition partners for Radev.
- Clear constitutional / parliamentary guidance on the expected timeline after election day.

## Net update logic

The evidence supports Radev as the leading name, but the contract’s formal-swearing requirement and Bulgaria’s coalition fragility keep this from being a 90%+ case. The market appears to overcompress the gap between “electoral frontrunner” and “next sworn-in PM.”
