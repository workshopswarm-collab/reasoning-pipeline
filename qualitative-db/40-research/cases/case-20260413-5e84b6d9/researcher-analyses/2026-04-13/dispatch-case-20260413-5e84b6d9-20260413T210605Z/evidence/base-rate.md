# Summary

Outside-view evidence points to a much lower probability for Rumen Radev than the market price implies, but with a nontrivial disconfirming possibility that he now has a real partisan vehicle.

## Question being evaluated

Will Rumen Radev be the next individual officially sworn in as Prime Minister of Bulgaria after the 19 April 2026 parliamentary election, excluding caretaker prime ministers?

## Current lean

Lean no on Radev at current market pricing.

## Prior / starting view

Starting prior: in a fragmented parliamentary system with repeated coalition failure, any named individual should rarely trade near certainty unless there is very strong coalition evidence.

## Evidence supporting the claim

- Contextual election summaries indicate Radev is politically salient and may lead a named project/party, which keeps a path open. Indirect; medium weight.
- Bulgaria’s instability means unconventional coalition outcomes are possible. Indirect; low-to-medium weight.

## Evidence against the claim

- Bulgaria is a parliamentary republic where the prime minister is usually produced by parliamentary majority/coalition bargaining rather than by presidential prominence. Structural/direct-to-mechanism; high weight.
- The market contract excludes caretaker prime ministers, removing an easy confusion path in a country with repeated caretaker governments. Direct/rule-relevant; high weight.
- The country has experienced repeated fragmented elections and failed coalition formation since 2021, which argues against extreme certainty on a specific person absent hard coalition evidence. Contextual; high weight.

## Ambiguous or mixed evidence

- Public encyclopedia-style summaries claim Radev is associated with or leads a political project called Progressive Bulgaria, but this was not independently confirmed through stronger reporting during this run.
- The president’s formal role in government formation can make the presidency seem closer to the premiership than it actually is under normal parliamentary logic.

## Conflict between inputs

Main tension is not factual conflict about the constitutional structure; it is weighting conflict between structural base rates and candidate-specific narrative/current-cycle speculation.

## Key assumptions

- Radev lacks clearly verified coalition-dominant parliamentary backing.
- No contract-interpretation surprise changes the exclusion of caretaker prime ministers.
- The next sworn-in prime minister emerges through ordinary parliamentary coalition dynamics.

## Key uncertainties

- Whether Radev actually leads a significant electoral vehicle in 2026.
- Whether post-election coalition bargaining produces a compromise PM rather than a party leader.
- Whether no PM is sworn in by 31 March 2027, causing resolution to Other.

## Disconfirming signals to watch

- Independent reporting that Radev’s bloc is first or coalition-central.
- Official post-election government formation steps naming Radev.
- Broad credible reporting consensus that Radev is the expected sworn-in PM.

## What would increase confidence

- Official or widely confirmed party/coalition documents showing who leads viable electoral alliances.
- Independent seat projections or coalition math from credible reporting.
- Clear government announcement of who is sworn in post-election.

## Net update logic

The starting outside view was low because parliamentary coalition systems with fragmentation almost never justify 90% confidence in one named post-election PM without unusually strong direct evidence. The additional verification pass on contract wording strengthened the low view by confirming caretaker PMs do not count. The main thing preventing an even lower estimate is the possibility that Radev has become the real head of a viable electoral project.

## Suggested downstream use

Use as synthesis input and forecast-challenge material, especially against any narrative that confuses current prominence or prior presidency with high-certainty post-election premiership.
