# Summary

The strongest variant case is not that diplomacy will unexpectedly bloom from here; it is that the market may still be below fair value because the qualifying diplomatic-meeting condition appears likely to have been met already in February via Oman/Geneva indirect talks.

## Question being evaluated

Is the live market price of 0.76 underpricing a Yes outcome because publicly acknowledged in-person US-Iran mediated talks already occurred and probably satisfy the resolution criteria?

## Current lean

Lean **Yes above market**.

## Prior / starting view

Starting from the headline environment alone, the March war backdrop would normally argue for caution and possibly a lower Yes probability. After reviewing the February talk timeline, the more interesting question becomes whether the market is stale on the rules rather than right on the facts.

## Evidence supporting the claim

- **Reuters/AP timeline shows actual talks, not just rumored talks.**
  - Source: Oman/Geneva talks timeline note.
  - Why it matters: the existence of actual publicly reported talks is the core factual requirement.
  - Directness: direct at title/timeline level.
  - Weight: high.

- **The dispatch's market description explicitly says indirect meetings through mediators can qualify.**
  - Why it matters: this sharply lowers the bar versus a same-room direct bilateral summit requirement.
  - Directness: direct from manifest language.
  - Weight: high.

- **Multiple rounds were reported across Oman and Geneva.**
  - Source: Reuters/AP timeline note.
  - Why it matters: repeated rounds make it less likely the entire signal is a one-off misread.
  - Directness: direct.
  - Weight: medium-high.

- **March war coverage still references diplomacy/proposals/progress.**
  - Source: war-and-stale-framing note.
  - Why it matters: even in escalation, diplomatic process was not erased; this supports the idea that price could be lagging a more mechanical resolution logic.
  - Directness: indirect.
  - Weight: medium.

## Evidence against the claim

- **Rule-interpretation ambiguity remains real.**
  - The market may require specifics about authorization, in-person format, or mediator structure that are not fully visible from title-level reporting.
  - Weight: high.

- **The live price is already 0.76, not 0.30.**
  - This suggests the market is not oblivious; it may already partially price the February talks but still see enough rule ambiguity to avoid near-certainty.
  - Weight: medium-high.

- **Late-March war escalation can muddy public-acknowledgment and criteria interpretation.**
  - Even if talks occurred, market participants may think later developments alter whether the earlier contacts count in practice.
  - Weight: medium.

## Ambiguous or mixed evidence

- Reuters/BBC progress language cuts two ways: it supports the existence of diplomacy, but it may also tempt traders to think in forward-looking "will talks happen" terms rather than backward-looking "did a qualifying meeting already happen" terms.

## Conflict between inputs

The main disagreement is **interpretive/criteria-based**, not factual.

- Factual side: February Oman/Geneva contacts were publicly reported as US-Iran talks.
- Interpretive side: whether those contacts satisfy the exact resolution standard.
- What would resolve it: full article text, official statements naming participants/format, or explicit market-resolution commentary.

## Key assumptions

- The reported indirect talks were in-person and sufficiently official to qualify.
- The market is underweighting already-occurred qualifying contact because war headlines dominate attention.

## Key uncertainties

- exact participant authority
- same-room vs shuttle format
- how the market-resolution source set will operationalize the phrase "diplomatic meeting"

## Disconfirming signals to watch

- credible rule-based argument that Oman/Geneva rounds were non-qualifying
- evidence that the parties were not physically present in the relevant way
- official clarification denying that any qualifying diplomatic meeting took place

## What would increase confidence

- full-text Reuters/AP articles confirming authorized representatives met in person through mediation
- official US, Iranian, Swiss, or Omani acknowledgement of the talks' format and participants
- market-resolution discussion explicitly saying indirect in-person talks count

## Net update logic

The update is from a generic "war makes diplomacy less likely" frame to a stronger "the market may be asking the wrong temporal question" frame. What mattered most was the February timeline of already-held Oman/Geneva talks combined with manifest language that indirect mediator-based meetings count. The market may be underpricing Yes not because future diplomacy is likely, but because past diplomacy may already satisfy the condition.
