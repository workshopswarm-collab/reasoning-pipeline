# Assumption
The market’s 76% YES price appears to assume that active mediation plus recent talk-hosting signals will translate into a resolution-qualifying, publicly acknowledged in-person U.S.-Iran meeting before June 30 without being derailed by substantive deadlock or escalation.

## Why this assumption matters
This assumption does most of the work in converting “talks exist” into “meeting will definitely happen soon.” If it fails, the market may be materially overconfident even if diplomacy remains broadly alive.

## What this assumption supports
- A high YES probability close to the current 76% market price.
- Confidence that mediator announcements and named-envoy expectations are enough evidence of eventual execution.
- The idea that June 30 leaves plenty of time despite current volatility.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption
- Oman publicly confirmed another round of talks in Geneva.
- Araghchi reportedly expected to meet Witkoff.
- Pakistan publicly said it would soon host talks.
- Historical precedent exists for U.S.-Iran Oman-facilitated face-to-face contact.

## What would falsify it
- Another visible diplomatic breakdown over zero enrichment or sequencing.
- Continued claims of “progress” without an actual named, scheduled, acknowledged in-person meeting.
- Escalation that shifts both sides back to coercion and away from face-to-face diplomacy.
- Any indication that future contacts remain only indirect, remote, or too informal to satisfy market rules.

## Early warning signs
- Public contradiction between U.S. and Iranian accounts of whether meaningful talks are even occurring.
- Repeated mediator statements without concrete meeting details.
- Rising military deployments or threats overshadowing diplomatic logistics.
- Media reporting focused on backchannels rather than bilateral in-person engagement.

## What changes if this assumption fails
A risk-managed probability should move down materially, because several independent conditions would need to be re-earned rather than presumed: execution, format, authorization, public acknowledgement, and timing.
