# Assumption

PP–DB is more likely than not to remain ahead of its nearest rivals for the specific third-place seat ranking, but not by a margin wide enough to justify very high confidence.

## Why this assumption matters

The difference between “in the top cluster” and “exactly third on seats” is the whole market. If PP–DB’s edge over Revival or DPS-like competitors is thin, a seemingly supportive polling narrative can still fail at resolution.

## What this assumption supports

- A yes-lean rather than a no-lean.
- A probability estimate below the market price.
- A risk-manager conclusion that the market is directionally reasonable but too confident.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Outgoing-seat context places PP–DB above Revival and DPS, implying a nontrivial structural claim to third-place status.
- POLITICO’s current Bulgaria election framing still treats PP–DB as part of the lead competitive set, rather than as an obvious fade.
- The fragmented Bulgarian party system makes small changes in vote share potentially large in seat order, which argues for retaining caution even if PP–DB is currently favored.

## What would falsify it

- A late polling cluster showing PP–DB clearly fourth or lower behind Revival and/or DPS-New Beginning.
- Seat projections or election-night reporting placing PP–DB outside the top three on seats.
- Coalition restructuring or contract-relevant dissolution effects that materially weaken how PP–DB is counted for settlement.

## Early warning signs

- Repeated polling that shows Revival consolidating ahead of PP–DB.
- Evidence that DPS-New Beginning has stronger-than-expected seat efficiency.
- Reporting suggesting PP–DB’s support is softer or more turnout-sensitive than competitors’ support.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The case flips from “yes, but less confidently than market” to either near-even or outright no, because the market is about exact rank, not broad viability.
