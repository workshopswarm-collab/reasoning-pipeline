My base case is still that a qualifying extension is a bit more likely than not because diplomacy remains open and the contract only requires a publicly confirmed extension before April 21, not a full durable settlement. But I am below the market because no extension has yet been officially announced and the substantive sticking points remain large.

## Agreement or disagreement with market

I **disagree modestly** with the market. I think Yes remains more likely than No, but 70.5% looks too high given the evidence currently visible.

The market's strongest argument is straightforward: there is already a ceasefire framework from April 7, diplomacy continued into high-level direct talks, and the contract can resolve Yes on a pre-deadline agreement even if implementation is later. Where I think the market is fragile is that the most important recent concrete event was bearish: the April 12 Islamabad talks ended without an agreement. If there were a clear extension path, I would expect at least one visible official statement signaling movement toward extension terms. Instead, what is publicly visible is ongoing dialogue without announced convergence.

## Implication for the question

This should be treated as a **slight-Yes but not confident-Yes** market. The right interpretation is that the ceasefire-extension path is still alive, but the evidence does not justify pricing it as if extension is close to done.

## Resolution or source-of-truth interpretation

This is a contract-sensitive market.

Material conditions that all must hold for Yes:
1. there must be a publicly announced and mutually agreed extension or replacement agreement that qualifies as extending the April 7 ceasefire;
2. it must occur by **11:59 PM ET on April 21, 2026**;
3. there must be **no gap** where no ceasefire is in effect if the extension comes through a replacement arrangement;
4. the extension must be confirmed by official U.S. and Iranian statements, **or** by overwhelming credible media consensus confirming an official agreement.

What does **not** qualify:
- informal de-escalation;
- unilateral restraint;
- vague continued talks;
- humanitarian pauses or tactical stand-downs;
- future-negotiation frameworks that do not explicitly extend the ceasefire.

Date/timing check:
- Initial ceasefire was announced **April 7, 2026** and described as two weeks.
- Market deadline is **April 21, 2026 at 11:59 PM ET**.
- Because the wording allows new agreements scheduled to take effect before or at the initial agreement's scheduled end, timing and no-gap mechanics are central.
