# Assumption
The decisive variable is whether a second formal negotiating round happens quickly enough to produce a publicly confirmed rollover before the original two-week ceasefire expires, rather than whether the parties broadly prefer de-escalation.

## Why this assumption matters
The market contract rewards a specific timed event: an official extension or equivalently clear media-confirmed official agreement. Continued low hostilities without a timely formal process is not enough.

## What this assumption supports
- A moderate rather than very high Yes probability.
- Heavy weighting on the next-talks calendar as the main repricing trigger.
- A view that timing risk, not only substantive disagreement, is doing most of the work.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption
- AP and Al Jazeera both report the first major Islamabad round ended without agreement.
- AP reports Pakistan proposed another round in the coming days, indicating a concrete next catalyst still exists.
- The contract text makes bilateral public confirmation and no-gap timing central to resolution.

## What would falsify it
- An official US and Iranian extension announcement arrives without a visible second round.
- The parties publicly agree to keep the ceasefire in place through a broader framework that clearly qualifies under the contract.
- Clear evidence emerges that the ceasefire can be officially extended after the original window without violating the contract's no-gap rule.

## Early warning signs
- No official scheduling confirmation for further talks.
- White House or Iranian official messaging focuses on future dialogue but avoids extension language.
- New military incidents or coercive moves crowd out diplomacy before the ceasefire end date.

## What changes if this assumption fails
If the market can resolve Yes without the expected second-round catalyst, then the current estimate is too conservative and should move upward. If the second round proves irrelevant because talks are structurally dead, then the estimate should move lower and the market is overpricing mere diplomatic inertia.
