# Assumption
The market's ~70.5% Yes pricing appears to assume that an intact ceasefire plus active Pakistan-mediated follow-on talks will more likely than not produce a publicly confirmable extension before the existing two-week truce expires.

## Why this assumption matters
Without that assumption, current evidence supports only the existence of a temporary ceasefire and ongoing diplomacy, not an extension already agreed. The probability gap between "talks continue" and "qualifying extension is officially reached and publicly confirmable by deadline" is the core forecasting problem.

## What this assumption supports
- A moderate Yes probability above 50%
- Respect for the market prior rather than a reflexive downgrade after failed first-round talks
- The interpretation that continued negotiations before expiry are genuinely extension-relevant rather than mere noise

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption
- AP says the ceasefire remains intact and Pakistan has proposed a second round of talks before the truce ends.
- BBC says the ceasefire created a direct negotiation pathway and describes the truce as politically costly but still active.
- In practical diplomacy, intact ceasefires with live mediator engagement are more extendable than ceasefires that have already broken down.

## What would falsify it
- A direct US or Iranian statement rejecting extension or announcing renewed hostilities before April 21.
- Failure to hold a second round of talks before the truce expires.
- Credible reporting that one side will only accept a permanent settlement and refuses a temporary extension format.

## Early warning signs
- Escalatory military incidents during the two-week truce.
- Public hardening by the White House, Iran's foreign ministry, or top Iranian security bodies.
- Media reporting that talks are stalled on terms that block even a temporary extension.

## What changes if this assumption fails
The market would look overconfident. My estimate would move materially lower, likely below 50%, because the contract requires more than de-escalation: it needs an official or overwhelmingly confirmed extension agreement.
