# Assumption
The current ceasefire will be extended only if the parties can separate a narrow ceasefire rollover from the broader unresolved disputes that caused the April 12 talks to end without a deal.

## Why this assumption matters
A Yes outcome likely requires a limited procedural extension despite major disagreement on nuclear, sanctions, Hormuz, and regional-security issues. If those issues cannot be compartmentalized, the probability of a clean qualifying extension falls quickly.

## What this assumption supports
- A moderate-but-below-market Yes probability rather than a confident Yes
- The view that timing/path risk is underpriced
- The judgment that failed marathon talks are materially disconfirming, not just noise

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption
- Reporting indicates the first major direct talks ended without agreement and exposed multiple difficult sticking points.
- Third-party mediators are still urging continuation, which suggests the diplomatic channel remains open.
- The contract allows a qualifying extension even before implementation, so a narrow political announcement could still be enough if both sides choose to preserve the pause.

## What would falsify it
- Clear evidence that both sides have already converged on a narrow extension formula while deferring the harder issues.
- Official statements from both governments affirming a ceasefire extension irrespective of broader peace-deal deadlock.

## Early warning signs
- Public maximalist rhetoric from either side after April 12.
- Continued disagreement over whether the ceasefire covers linked theaters like Lebanon.
- Absence of synchronized official language from Washington and Tehran as the original two-week term nears expiry.

## What changes if this assumption fails
If a narrow rollover proves easy to separate from the broader agenda, the Yes probability should move higher and closer to or above the market. If instead broad-deal deadlock makes any extension impossible, the market is too high.
