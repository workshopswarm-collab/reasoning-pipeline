# Assumption

The failed April 12 Islamabad round did not represent the final feasible chance for a qualifying ceasefire extension before April 21.

## Why this assumption matters

The variant thesis depends on the market underweighting deadline diplomacy. If April 12 was effectively the last real opportunity, then the probability should be materially lower than the current market price.

## What this assumption supports

- A roughly market-level but slightly lower probability instead of a sharp bearish cut.
- The claim that the market may be too anchored to the headline "talks failed" rather than to the still-open diplomatic window.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Al Jazeera reporting indicates both sides left diplomacy open after the talks.
- Pakistan publicly called for the ceasefire to continue and for dialogue to proceed.
- Reuters-indexed reporting on April 13 suggests the US and Iran left the door open to dialogue after tense talks.
- The contract resolves Yes on a qualifying agreement reached before the deadline even if implementation is later, so the remaining window still matters.

## What would falsify it

- An official statement from either government that talks are over and no extension will be pursued.
- Renewed direct military engagement before an extension agreement.
- Clear evidence that the April 7 ceasefire has already lapsed with a gap, making extension mechanics non-qualifying.

## Early warning signs

- Public hard deadlines expiring without follow-up meetings.
- Officials framing the April 12 round as definitive failure rather than one round in a continuing process.
- Escalatory military or maritime actions replacing diplomatic messaging.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The market should be interpreted as too high, and the probability would likely move down into a clearly bearish range because the contract is date-limited and requires explicit confirmation.
