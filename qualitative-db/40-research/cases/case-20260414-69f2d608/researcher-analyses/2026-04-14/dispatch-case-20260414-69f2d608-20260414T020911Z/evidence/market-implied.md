# Summary
The market's Yes price is defensible but not fully proven. Evidence supports an intact ceasefire and ongoing diplomacy, but not yet a qualifying official extension.

## Question being evaluated
Will there be an official extension of the April 7 two-week US-Iran ceasefire, publicly agreed and confirmable by both governments or by overwhelming credible media consensus, by April 21, 2026?

## Current lean
Moderate lean Yes, but below the market.

## Prior / starting view
Start from market-implied 70.5% because an existing ceasefire plus mediator activity often embeds real information the open web alone may not immediately show.

## Evidence supporting the claim
- AP live blog: ceasefire remains intact as of April 13 according to Pakistan's prime minister. Direct to current status; medium-high weight.
- AP live blog: Pakistan proposed a second round of talks before the end of the ceasefire. Direct to extension pathway/timing; medium weight.
- BBC: ceasefire exists, talks are underway, and Iran accepted a temporary truce despite internal hardliner resistance. Contextual support that diplomacy is real and active; medium weight.

## Evidence against the claim
- AP: first talks ended without an agreement. Direct evidence that extension is not already done; medium-high weight.
- AP: White House would not confirm whether new talks were being weighed. Direct evidence of unresolved official commitment; medium weight.
- BBC: the war could resume if talks break down. Contextual evidence of fragility; medium weight.
- Contract wording: informal de-escalation, unilateral pauses, or future-negotiation frameworks do not qualify. High weight on resolution risk.

## Ambiguous or mixed evidence
- Pakistan's central mediation role could help reach an extension, but also means much public reporting may come through a third party rather than immediate dual confirmation.
- Internal Iranian hardliner opposition could either block extension or increase the value of a temporary extension as a face-saving off-ramp.

## Conflict between inputs
The main conflict is not factual but inferential: current reporting supports "ceasefire still holding" more strongly than "extension likely to be officially and publicly locked in by deadline." The market appears to compress those two steps more aggressively than the evidence alone compels.

## Key assumptions
- An intact ceasefire and live talks are strong enough predictors of extension to justify >50% odds.
- Both governments will prefer a temporary extension to immediate renewed conflict.
- Public confirmation standards will be met if a deal is reached.

## Key uncertainties
- Whether second-round talks actually occur before expiry.
- Whether any agreement is framed as a qualifying extension versus a vague de-escalation arrangement.
- Whether both governments publicly confirm the extension in a resolution-usable way.

## Disconfirming signals to watch
- Official rejection of extension by either side.
- Renewed strikes before the truce ends.
- Reporting that talks continue but without commitment to extend the ceasefire itself.

## What would increase confidence
- White House or State Department confirmation of additional talks focused on extending the truce.
- Iranian foreign ministry or SNSC confirmation that an extension is under discussion or accepted.
- Multiple independent wires reporting a reached extension agreement.

## Net update logic
The market's Yes bias is justified by the fact pattern that the ceasefire still exists and negotiations are still live close to expiry. I downweight the market somewhat because the contract requires a relatively specific form of official or overwhelmingly confirmed extension, and current evidence is one step short of that threshold.

Use as synthesis input and as a checklist for what official confirmation is still missing.
