# Summary

The evidence is not a clean bullish or bearish stack. The strongest bearish fact is that the first major post-ceasefire negotiating round ended without an agreement. The strongest variant point is that officials and independent reporting still describe an open diplomatic channel inside the contract window, and the contract only needs a qualifying extension agreement before April 21.

## Question being evaluated

Will there be a publicly announced and mutually agreed official extension of the April 7 two-week US-Iran ceasefire, with no gap in ceasefire coverage, by April 21, 2026?

## Current lean

Slight lean to Yes, but below market confidence.

## Prior / starting view

Starting baseline was the market-implied 70.5%, suggesting consensus expects extension more likely than not.

## Evidence supporting the claim

- Ongoing diplomacy after failed talks.
  - Source: Al Jazeera source note; Reuters-indexed April 13 headline.
  - Why it matters: extension remains procedurally alive.
  - Direct vs indirect: mostly contextual but built from attributed official statements.
  - Weight: medium.
- Pakistan publicly urged both sides to uphold the ceasefire and continue dialogue.
  - Source: Al Jazeera source note.
  - Why it matters: indicates mediator pressure toward continuity rather than collapse.
  - Direct vs indirect: direct quote/contextual relevance.
  - Weight: low-medium.
- Contract can resolve Yes on an agreement reached before deadline even if implementation comes later.
  - Source: market rules.
  - Why it matters: lowers the bar relative to a durable, fully implemented peace deal.
  - Direct vs indirect: direct contract interpretation.
  - Weight: high.

## Evidence against the claim

- April 12 Islamabad talks ended without a deal.
  - Source: Al Jazeera with attributed Vance comments.
  - Why it matters: best immediate evidence that extension was not easy or imminent.
  - Direct vs indirect: direct.
  - Weight: high.
- No visible official U.S. extension announcement found in verification pass.
  - Source: official-site search check note.
  - Why it matters: contract wants explicit official confirmation.
  - Direct vs indirect: negative verification evidence.
  - Weight: medium-high.
- Core sticking points remain substantive: nuclear commitments, sanctions, Hormuz, reparations, war-end terms.
  - Source: Al Jazeera source note.
  - Why it matters: unresolved major issues can prevent a qualifying extension.
  - Direct vs indirect: contextual.
  - Weight: medium.

## Ambiguous or mixed evidence

- Iranian statement that "Diplomacy never ends" is supportive of continued talks but not itself an extension confirmation.
- Trump/Vance rhetoric may be partly negotiating posture rather than true signal of final willingness.
- The absence of official announcement could mean no deal yet, or simply that talks remain in flight.

## Conflict between inputs

There is little factual conflict. The conflict is interpretive:
- bearish reading: no deal after high-level talks means extension odds should fall sharply;
- variant reading: no deal yet is not no extension, because the contract only needs a pre-deadline announcement and both sides kept diplomacy open.

Evidence that would resolve it:
- explicit official extension statement from both governments;
- explicit official breakdown statement;
- renewed hostilities showing ceasefire continuity failed.

## Key assumptions

- April 12 was not the final feasible extension opportunity.
- The existing ceasefire remains in force long enough that a no-gap extension is still mechanically possible.

## Key uncertainties

- Actual backchannel momentum after April 12.
- Whether both governments would publicly confirm the same extension in qualifying form.
- Whether there is hidden slippage risk around the initial two-week expiration timing.

## Disconfirming signals to watch

- Official declaration that talks failed permanently.
- Military escalation before a new agreement.
- Statements implying only de-escalation or future negotiations without explicit extension.

## What would increase confidence

- White House or State Department statement confirming talks continue toward extending the ceasefire.
- Iranian MFA or IRNA publication explicitly confirming willingness to extend the truce.
- Independent AP/Reuters reporting that both sides are working on a short extension text.

## Net update logic

The evidence moved the view down from the market's confident 70.5% but not to outright bearishness. The key reason is that the market contract is narrower and more timing-sensitive than a broad peace-deal narrative, yet still easier to satisfy than a full durable settlement. The failed Islamabad round matters most, but the market could be overreacting if it treats that as closing the extension path entirely.
