# Summary
The case still leans slightly Yes because the starting point is an existing ceasefire and active mediation, but the risk lens says the market is pricing too much confidence relative to the available evidence and the number of unresolved failure modes.

## Question being evaluated
Will there be a qualifying official extension of the April 7 US-Iran two-week ceasefire by the market deadline?

## Current lean
Slight lean Yes, but weaker than the market implies.

## Prior / starting view
Starting prior: extension more likely than not because both sides already accepted a short ceasefire and outside mediators want to preserve it.

## Evidence supporting the claim
- Existing ceasefire already in force, so an extension is operationally easier than creating a ceasefire from scratch. Direct/contextual. Medium weight.
- Pakistan and Oman publicly urged the ceasefire to continue and talks to proceed, signaling an active mediation channel rather than complete diplomatic collapse. Contextual. Medium weight.
- Contract allows a qualifying agreement even before implementation, so a last-minute political announcement could still resolve Yes. Contractual/direct. Medium weight.

## Evidence against the claim
- April 12 marathon talks ended without agreement after 21 hours. Direct high-weight disconfirming evidence.
- Reported sticking points are broad and severe: nuclear restrictions, Strait of Hormuz, reparations, sanctions/assets, and regional scope. Contextual high weight because these are hard to bridge quickly.
- The contract requires either official confirmation from both governments or overwhelming media consensus of an official extension; mere continuation of diplomacy or reduced fighting is insufficient. Direct high weight.
- Public ambiguity over whether the ceasefire covers related theaters such as Lebanon raises wording/source-of-truth risk. Contextual medium weight.

## Ambiguous or mixed evidence
- Iranian and US officials both kept diplomacy rhetorically alive after talks failed. This cuts both ways: channel remains open, but no extension exists yet.
- Strong incentive to avoid renewed war may support extension, but strong incentive alone does not guarantee an agreement that satisfies the contract wording.

## Conflict between inputs
The main conflict is not factual but weighting-based: some inputs emphasize the existence of a ceasefire and mediation momentum, while risk-focused inputs emphasize that the most important direct negotiation produced no deal and exposed multiple hard blockers.

## Key assumptions
- A narrow ceasefire extension can be separated from the broader peace-package disagreements.
- Official statements from both governments, or overwhelming media consensus of an official extension, would emerge quickly if a rollover is reached.

## Key uncertainties
- Whether the parties are willing to decouple ceasefire maintenance from broader geopolitical demands.
- Whether any eventual announcement will be explicit enough to satisfy the contract.
- The exact expiry mechanics/timing of the initial two-week arrangement from April 7 and whether a no-gap rollover is politically feasible.

## Disconfirming signals to watch
- Further official statements repeating no-agreement language without announcing follow-on talks.
- Renewed direct hostilities or explicit threats before synchronized extension language appears.
- Continued asymmetry in public descriptions of what the ceasefire covers.

## What would increase confidence
- Official US statement endorsing extension.
- Official Iranian statement matching that extension language.
- Independent confirmation from a second major wire/service that a concrete extension agreement was reached.

## Net update logic
The existence of the ceasefire keeps Yes above 50%, but the failed April 12 talks and contract wording materially cap confidence. The market appears to be pricing something closer to smooth continuation than to a fragile, high-friction rollover process.
