# Summary

AP reported that a 21-hour face-to-face U.S.-Iran negotiation in Islamabad ended without agreement, leaving the two-week ceasefire in place but its fate unclear. For this market, the key value is not settlement by itself but evidence that the most obvious near-term extension vehicle had already failed as of April 11.

## Key facts extracted

- AP says the U.S. and Iran ended a historic round of direct talks without reaching an agreement.
- Vice President JD Vance said negotiations ended after Iran refused U.S. terms tied to refraining from developing a nuclear weapon.
- Pakistani mediators publicly urged both countries to continue upholding the ceasefire.
- The source describes the ceasefire as fragile and unresolved rather than already extended.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- No agreement was reached in the April 11 talks.
- The existing ceasefire was still being discussed as active but fragile.
- Pakistani officials were pushing for additional dialogue, implying unfinished negotiation rather than a completed extension.

## What is uncertain

- Whether follow-on talks after April 11 could still produce a qualifying extension before the market deadline.
- Whether either government had parallel backchannel understandings not yet publicly confirmed.
- Whether a later broader deal could include a qualifying extension even if the April 11 round failed.

## Why this source may matter

This is a recent, credible wire report on the main diplomatic channel most likely to generate a formal extension. It is not an official U.S. or Iranian statement settling the market, but it materially lowers confidence that an extension was already near completion by the weekend before resolution.

## Possible impact on the question

This source pushes probability down relative to a naive assumption that an existing ceasefire usually gets rolled over automatically. It suggests a meaningful structural barrier: a live, high-level negotiation already ran and failed to produce the needed public agreement.
