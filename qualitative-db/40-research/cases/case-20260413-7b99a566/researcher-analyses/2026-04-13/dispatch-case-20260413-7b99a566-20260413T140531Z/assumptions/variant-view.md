# Assumption

The market is at least partly overpricing the proposition because scheduled or widely reported talks in Washington are being treated as near-equivalent to a completed, publicly acknowledged, contract-qualifying in-person diplomatic meeting.

## Why this assumption matters

This is the core variant thesis. If expected talks and qualifying talks are effectively the same thing here, then the market around 71.5% could be fair or even low. If they are different, the market is ahead of the evidence.

## What this assumption supports

- A modestly bearish view versus the market.
- Greater weight on execution and contract-fit risk than on generic diplomacy headlines.
- A probability estimate below the live market despite a real affirmative pathway.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- The contract is unusually specific: in-person only, indirect meetings allowed only if the relevant parties are present, and public acknowledgment or consensus credible reporting is required.
- Available reporting strongly supports imminence of talks but is thinner on meeting structure, participant authority, and whether the event has happened versus is merely scheduled.
- Geopolitical negotiation headlines often compress direct, indirect, shuttle, and preparatory contacts into the same language.

## What would falsify it

- A government statement from Israel or Lebanon confirming that authorized representatives met in person, directly or in an allowed indirect format, on or before April 19.
- Multiple independent credible reports after the event specifying that both sides’ representatives were physically present in a diplomatic meeting fitting the market rules.

## Early warning signs

- Named delegates and venue details from both sides.
- On-record acknowledgement by either foreign ministry or presidency office.
- Reporting that the talks are not merely mediated shuttle sessions but a co-located meeting framework.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The correct view would move upward quickly, likely toward or above the market, because the remaining disagreement is mostly about whether the procedural box-checking actually happens.
