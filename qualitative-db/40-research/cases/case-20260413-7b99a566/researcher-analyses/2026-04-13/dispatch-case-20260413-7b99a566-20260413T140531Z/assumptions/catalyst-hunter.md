# Assumption

The publicly announced Tuesday Washington meeting between official Israeli and Lebanese representatives will occur in person substantially as scheduled and be publicly reportable before the market deadline.

## Why this assumption matters

The bullish case for a Yes resolution depends less on long-run peace prospects than on a near-term procedural catalyst: an actual in-person diplomatic session before April 19.

## What this assumption supports

- A probability estimate above the market-implied baseline.
- The view that the key repricing catalyst is the scheduled April 14 Washington meeting itself.
- The interpretation that the contract can resolve Yes even if broader negotiations later fail.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Reuters, AP, Al-Monitor, and Times of Israel all reported serious planning for the Washington session.
- Multiple reports named specific diplomats and venue.
- Both Israeli and Lebanese public statements acknowledged a planned diplomatic engagement under U.S. mediation.
- The scheduled date is comfortably before the April 19 11:59 PM ET deadline.

## What would falsify it

- Official cancellation, postponement, or venue breakdown.
- A shift from in-person to remote-only engagement.
- Clarification that the session is not a deliberate diplomatic meeting between authorized representatives.
- Lack of public acknowledgment and lack of consensus credible reporting after the supposed meeting.

## Early warning signs

- Escalating Israeli or Hezbollah strikes that derail the trip.
- Lebanese insistence on ceasefire-first terms causing postponement.
- Conflicting official statements narrowing the meeting into a non-qualifying contact.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The probability should drop sharply because the most concrete near-term catalyst would disappear, leaving only vague intentions and much less time before resolution.
