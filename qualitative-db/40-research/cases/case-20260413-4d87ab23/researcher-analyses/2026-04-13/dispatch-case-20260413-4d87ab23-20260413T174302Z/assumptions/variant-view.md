# Assumption

The market should count the next major DeepSeek V-series release only if DeepSeek itself clearly positions it as the successor to DeepSeek-V3 and makes it publicly accessible to the general public by the governing deadline.

## Why this assumption matters

This is the core interpretive gate between a routine V3.x continuation and a qualifying Yes resolution. If V3.1/V3.2 are treated as iterative updates rather than the next major V model, then a Yes outcome requires a clearly new V-series successor such as V4 or V5.

## What this assumption supports

- A lower-than-market probability estimate.
- The view that current official V3.2 evidence does not itself satisfy the contract.
- The judgment that the main remaining path to Yes is a late, explicit, public successor launch.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- The market text explicitly excludes intermediate versions like V3.5.
- The contract says the next DeepSeek V model must be explicitly named as such or clearly positioned as a successor to DeepSeek-V3.
- DeepSeek's own release chronology publicly progressed through V3, V3-0324, V3.1, V3.1-Terminus, V3.2-Exp, and V3.2, which reads more like a continuing V3 family than a fresh major successor already launched.

## What would falsify it

- An official DeepSeek announcement stating that V3.2 itself is the next major V successor for purposes of product naming.
- A market clarifier or authoritative resolution note saying a V3.x release counts as the next DeepSeek V model.
- An explicit public DeepSeek V4/V5 launch before deadline.

## Early warning signs

- Official copy describing V3.2 or another release as the successor to V3 rather than an update within V3.
- A new API docs/news page announcing DeepSeek-V4, V5, or equivalent successor naming.
- Credible secondary reporting quoting DeepSeek that public access is open now.

## What changes if this assumption fails

If V3.2 or another V3.x artifact is deemed to count, the probability of Yes rises sharply and the current bearish variant thesis mostly collapses.
