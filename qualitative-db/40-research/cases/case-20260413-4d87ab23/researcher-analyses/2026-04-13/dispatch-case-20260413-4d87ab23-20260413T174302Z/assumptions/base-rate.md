# Assumption

Absent a clear official public launch signal very close to the deadline, major frontier-model successor releases usually do not materialize cleanly enough to satisfy a narrow public-access contract at the last minute.

## Why this assumption matters

My probability is driven less by rumor flow and more by the outside view that flagship model launches are lumpy, usually heavily signaled when imminent, and often slip or remain partially gated.

## What this assumption supports

- A below-market probability estimate.
- Heavier weight on missing official release evidence.
- Skepticism that ambiguous previews, leaks, or private testing should be treated as near-certain precursors to a qualifying launch.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- The contract requires multiple conditions at once: next major V successor, official positioning, public accessibility, and timing by the deadline.
- Official surfaces still center V3.
- Extreme market pricing requires more than generic optimism; it requires evidence that all contractual conditions are likely to be met on time.

## What would falsify it

- A clear official DeepSeek announcement of DeepSeek-V4 or another successor explicitly positioned as the next V flagship and accessible to the general public before the deadline.
- Credible independent reporting confirming such access and branding.

## Early warning signs

- Official teaser pages naming V4/V5 on DeepSeek-controlled domains.
- Open signup or public waitlist pages tied to a next major V-series release.
- Multiple credible outlets independently describing the successor as publicly available.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The case would move sharply toward Yes because the main negative mechanism here is missing official/public launch evidence, not an argument that DeepSeek is incapable of shipping a successor.
