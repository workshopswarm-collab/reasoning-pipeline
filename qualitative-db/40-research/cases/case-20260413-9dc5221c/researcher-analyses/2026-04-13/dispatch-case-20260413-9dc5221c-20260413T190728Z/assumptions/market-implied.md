# Assumption

A two-point lead after 12 of 14 rounds in an elite double round-robin will usually convert into an official tournament win absent an abrupt collapse or tie-break loss.

## Why this assumption matters

The market’s 95% price is basically a claim about lead conversion, not just player strength. If late-stage conversion rates are materially weaker than intuition suggests, the market is overextended.

## What this assumption supports

- A high probability estimate for Sindarov.
- The view that the market is mostly efficient rather than wildly mispriced.
- The interpretation that remaining downside is concentrated in a small set of tail paths.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Only two classical rounds remain.
- The closest listed rival is two full points back.
- Sindarov’s current score includes six wins, suggesting he is not merely drifting on draws.
- He is already an elite player by rating/context, so the lead is not obviously built on an unsustainable outlier versus weak opposition.
- FIDE-style first-place/tie-break procedures mean the remaining realistic failure modes are finite and visible.

## What would falsify it

- Official standings showing the lead is smaller than believed.
- A late loss sequence combined with a rival surge that forces or wins a tie-break.
- Any official dispute, forfeit, or fair-play issue materially affecting results.

## Early warning signs

- Round 13 cuts the lead to one point or less.
- Official reporting emphasizes instability, controversy, or procedural risk.
- A rival enters the final day with strong tiebreak leverage after closing the gap.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The market price would look too high, because 95% only makes sense if practical conversion from this state is near automatic. Failure of the assumption would push the estimate materially lower and elevate operational/tiebreak risk.
