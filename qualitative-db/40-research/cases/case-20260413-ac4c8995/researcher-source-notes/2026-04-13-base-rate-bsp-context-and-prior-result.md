# Summary

This note captures the accessible public contextual evidence most relevant to a base-rate view: BSP–United Left is an already seated parliamentary coalition with a prior result comfortably above the threshold.

## Key facts extracted

- The accessible public 2026 election page lists BSP–United Left as holding 19 current seats in the National Assembly.
- The same context page reports BSP–United Left won 7.32% in the October 2024 parliamentary election and 19 seats.
- The 2026 contesting-parties table lists BSP–United Left on the ballot again for the April 2026 election.
- The same contextual material shows the coalition’s 2024 ballot result as 6.85% in the contesting-parties table, still comfortably above the 4% threshold.
- The BSP party page describes BSP as an established legacy party with longstanding national organization, substantial membership base, and continuing parliamentary representation.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- BSP–United Left was a parliamentary entrant in the last election cycle with a result materially above the threshold.
- BSP itself remains a large, organized, durable party rather than a new or fringe formation.
- The coalition is listed among contesting formations for the 2026 election.

## What is uncertain

- These pages are contextual and not official final-results pages.
- The exact live polling situation close to election day was not independently recovered in this run because web search was unreliable.
- The two percentages shown in the accessible election context (7.32% current parliamentary parties table versus 6.85% contesting-parties prior result line) differ slightly, though both imply the coalition was safely above threshold in the prior cycle.

## Why this source may matter

- For a base-rate question about winning at least one seat, the key outside-view fact is whether the party is already a seat-winning, ballot-qualified, nationally organized coalition with recent threshold clearance.
- A party starting from 6.8-7.3% in the prior cycle usually has a much better-than-even chance of retaining at least one seat absent evidence of collapse.

## Possible impact on the question

- This source strongly supports a Yes-lean because the coalition does not need to become newly viable; it only needs to avoid falling from a prior above-threshold position to below 4%.
- The main residual risk is fragmentation, rebranding/ballot ambiguity, or a sharp vote-share collapse.
