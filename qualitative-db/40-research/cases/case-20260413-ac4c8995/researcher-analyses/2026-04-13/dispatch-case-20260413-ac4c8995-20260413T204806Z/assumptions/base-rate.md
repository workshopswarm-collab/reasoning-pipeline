# Assumption

BSP–United Left will remain a clearly ballot-qualified national formation and retain vote share safely above Bulgaria's 4% parliamentary threshold through election day.

## Why this assumption matters

The base-rate Yes case mostly rests on threshold retention rather than coalition expansion. If the coalition remains a recognizable, functioning entrant with support modestly above 4%, it should win seats; if it fractures or slips below threshold, the market resolves No.

## What this assumption supports

- A probability estimate materially above 50%
- A view that prior parliamentary representation is the dominant outside-view anchor
- A conclusion that the main risk is downside threshold breach rather than ordinary seat-allocation noise

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Accessible contextual election material lists BSP–United Left as an active contesting coalition in the 2026 election.
- The coalition won roughly 6.9-7.3% in the prior cycle, leaving nontrivial but still meaningful room above the 4% threshold.
- BSP is a longstanding, nationally organized party rather than a transient micro-party.

## What would falsify it

- Credible reporting or official filings showing coalition breakup, ballot disqualification, or name/identity mismatch relevant to the contract
- Fresh polling showing BSP–United Left consistently below 4%
- Major split/defection event causing a large vote-share collapse shortly before the election

## Early warning signs

- Repeated late polls clustering in the 3-4% range
- Conflicting media descriptions of whether BSP, United Left, and BSP–United Left are the same ballot line for resolution purposes
- Evidence that the coalition is not campaigning as a unified national list

## What changes if this assumption fails

The estimate should move sharply lower, potentially below the market, because Bulgarian threshold elections are discontinuous: a small drop under 4% can reduce seats from some to zero.
