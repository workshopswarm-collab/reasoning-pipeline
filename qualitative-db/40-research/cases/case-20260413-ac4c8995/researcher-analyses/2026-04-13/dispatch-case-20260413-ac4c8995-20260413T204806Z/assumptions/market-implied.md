# Assumption

The market is mainly assuming that BSP–United Left remains a ballot-qualified, organizationally intact coalition with enough residual support to stay above Bulgaria’s 4% parliamentary threshold.

## Why this assumption matters

If that continuity assumption is right, a yes probability well above 50% is sensible because an already seated coalition with recent threshold-clearing performance usually has many paths to at least one seat. If the assumption fails, the market is too high.

## What this assumption supports

- A market-implied probability in the low-to-mid 70s.
- A personal estimate modestly below but near the market.
- The interpretation that this is more a threshold-survival question than a true longshot-entry question.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- BSP–United Left is described in contextual sources as an extant parliamentary coalition with 19 seats.
- The same contextual election source describes a 4% threshold and shows the coalition previously above it.
- No contrary evidence was found in this run showing deregistration, coalition dissolution, or a polling collapse below viability.

## What would falsify it

- Official CIK material showing the coalition is not registered or is materially restructured away from the contract’s named party.
- Credible late polling showing BSP–United Left clearly below the threshold with little path back.
- A coalition split that strands the BSP label in a non-qualifying vehicle.

## Early warning signs

- Credible Bulgarian reporting about registration disputes or coalition fragmentation.
- A sustained run of polls placing BSP–United Left near or below 4%.
- Reporting that the market contract’s party label no longer maps cleanly to the official ballot entity.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The probability should drop sharply because the contract only needs one seat, but failure of ballot qualification or a clean break in party identity would directly attack the path to any representation.
