# Summary

Recent NFL.com analyst material shows Reese is firmly in the top tier of this class, but not in an uncontested way for the No. 2 slot. Daniel Jeremiah ranks Reese No. 5 overall and Bailey No. 4 overall; Mike Band mocks Bailey to the Jets at No. 2 on Apr. 15; Lance Zierlein mocks Reese to the Jets at No. 2.

## Key facts extracted

- Daniel Jeremiah’s Apr. 1 big board ranks David Bailey No. 4 and Arvell Reese No. 5 overall.
- Mike Band’s Apr. 15 mock draft sends Bailey to the Jets at No. 2 and explicitly says Reese is the more versatile piece, but Bailey is the cleaner premium-position swing.
- Lance Zierlein’s mock draft sends Reese to the Jets at No. 2.
- This creates a meaningful split among credible NFL.com analysts rather than a unanimous Reese consensus.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- Jeremiah: Bailey ranked No. 4; Reese ranked No. 5.
- Band at No. 2: “Reese is the more versatile front-seven chess piece, but Bailey’s FBS-leading 14.5 sacks and 21.3% pressure rate ... make this the cleaner premium-position swing.”
- Zierlein at No. 2: “Reese is an unpolished diamond, but the Jets can take a swing on the upside.”

## What is uncertain

- Mock drafts are not direct reporting of Jets intent.
- NFL.com analysts may not be independent of the same broad information ecosystem, so this is not fully independent evidence.
- The source set does not establish that Reese is a runaway favorite among teams rather than merely among market participants.

## Why this source may matter

This is the main disconfirming evidence against a very high Reese probability. It shows that even close to the draft, credible evaluators still see a live Bailey-at-No.-2 path.

## Possible impact on the question

It argues against treating Reese as near-certain. A reasonable outside view should leave substantial room for Bailey or a surprise board/fit decision.

## Reliability notes

These are credible, recent contextual sources from NFL.com, but they are still analyst opinions and mock projections rather than official team disclosures. Useful for framing consensus breadth, not for direct settlement.
