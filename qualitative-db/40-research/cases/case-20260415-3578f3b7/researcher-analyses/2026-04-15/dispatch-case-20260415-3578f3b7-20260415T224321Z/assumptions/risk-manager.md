# Assumption

The bullish Reese-at-No.-2 view assumes the Jets both stay at pick No. 2 and prefer Reese's versatility/ceiling over Bailey's cleaner edge-production profile.

## Why this assumption matters

The market asks about an exact slot, not whether Reese is an elite prospect or even a top-three pick. Slot-specific markets are fragile because being directionally right on player quality can still lose if one team preference or one trade decision goes the other way.

## What this assumption supports

- Any probability materially above 50% for Reese specifically at No. 2.
- The idea that current public consensus is strong enough to justify a price in the low-to-mid 70s.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Reese is clearly viewed as a premium prospect and commonly appears in the top three.
- He offers unusual front-seven versatility, which could appeal to a team valuing multiplicity.
- There does not appear to be serious doubt that he will be drafted very early.

## What would falsify it

- Credible late reporting that the Jets prefer Bailey or another player.
- Credible trade reporting indicating No. 2 is likely to move.
- Stronger consensus mocks or draft-intel consolidating around Bailey at No. 2.

## Early warning signs

- Repeated final-week analyst language emphasizing Bailey as the cleaner fit at No. 2.
- Team-need reporting that focuses on pure edge pressure rather than hybrid versatility.
- Market volatility or narrowing between Reese and Bailey without fresh pro-Reese reporting.

## What changes if this assumption fails

Reese can still be a top-three or even No. 3 favorite, but his exact No. 2 probability should fall materially because the thesis is highly slot-dependent.
