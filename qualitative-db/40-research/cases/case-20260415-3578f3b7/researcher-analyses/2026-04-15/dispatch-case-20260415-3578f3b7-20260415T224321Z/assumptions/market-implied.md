# Assumption

The market is mostly assuming the Jets keep pick No. 2 and prefer Reese’s upside/versatility over Bailey, Styles, and trade-down alternatives.

## Why this assumption matters

A 73.5% market price is difficult to justify unless both pick ownership and preference ordering are relatively stable. If either breaks, Reese’s true probability falls materially.

## What this assumption supports

- Treating Reese as the favorite rather than just one name in a broad cluster.
- Giving meaningful weight to market confidence.
- Framing the main uncertainty around preference/trade risk rather than around whether Reese belongs in the top tier.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- The Jets officially hold pick No. 2 as of Apr. 15.
- The Jets have an edge/front-seven need according to NFL.com.
- Reese is an elite prospect in public rankings and appears at No. 2 in at least one prominent mock.
- Polymarket has Reese far ahead of the field, indicating traders likely believe this preference ordering is more stable than public mock disagreement alone would imply.

## What would falsify it

- Credible late reporting that the Jets prefer Bailey, Styles, or a quarterback.
- Strong evidence the Jets are actively shopping the No. 2 pick.
- Injury, medical, or character information changing Reese’s board position.

## Early warning signs

- More final-week mocks converging on Bailey or a trade at No. 2.
- Reporting that New York prioritizes premium edge production over hybrid upside.
- Price drift downward in Reese without a compensating rise in one single alternative, suggesting rising uncertainty rather than informed conviction.

## What changes if this assumption fails

Reese likely remains plausible, but the market would look overextended rather than efficient; probability should compress closer to a contested-favorite range instead of a near-three-in-four event.
