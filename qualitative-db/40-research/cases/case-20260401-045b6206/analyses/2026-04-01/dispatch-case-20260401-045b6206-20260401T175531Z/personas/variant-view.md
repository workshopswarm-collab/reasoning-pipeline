# Claim

I disagree with the market’s 0.854 implied probability. My estimate is **~60%** that this market resolves **Yes**, not because Iraq looks especially likely on the exact rules, but because the broader conflict context suggests traders may be mixing a real high-regional-escalation environment with a narrower Iraq-specific contract. The variant view is that the market looks **overconfident**, not necessarily wrong in direction.

## Implication for the question

At 85.4%, the market is pricing this as close to a foregone conclusion. I think that is too high for an event that requires all of the following to line up:

- Iran itself, not a proxy, must be the actor
- the strike must be missile/drone/air and must impact qualifying Iraqi ground territory or official Iraqi diplomatic premises
- credible reporting must support the timing and attribution
- the event must occur within the March window, not merely in a surrounding period of broader war

The market’s strongest argument is obvious: Iraq is a plausible theater for Iranian retaliation because of geography, prior precedent, militia ecosystems, and its role in wider US-Iran confrontation. But the fragile part of the market is that it may be pricing **regional war salience** more than this contract’s **exact resolution standard**.

## Supporting evidence

- Iraq is a natural candidate in any Iran regional retaliation scenario because it is proximate, politically penetrated, and historically exposed to Iran-linked coercion.
- The vault’s Iraq entity note correctly frames Iraq as a high-risk overlap point of militia activity, state fragility, and US-Iran competition.
- The vault’s Iran and conflicts notes support the view that Iran sits at the center of recurring regional conflict dynamics.
- Recent media context accessible during this run shows a much broader active Iran war environment by 2026-04-01, which supports the idea that traders had reason to price elevated regional danger.
- However, that same broader-war framing is also the best argument that the market could be **too confident on this exact Iraq leg**, because broad escalation does not automatically imply a qualifying March strike on Iraqi soil by Iranian forces.

## Counterpoints

- A 60% estimate is still above even odds, because Iraq remains one of the most plausible theaters if Iran chose another direct regional signal.
- If there was already strong but imperfect reporting late in March pointing toward a qualifying Iraqi strike, then my estimate would be too low.
- Source access in this run was messier than ideal: search was blocked and some fetches were partial, so this finding leans more on rule interpretation and structural pricing logic than on a rich multi-outlet event chronology.

## Key assumptions

- The market price is at least partly incorporating generalized regional-war expectations rather than only confirmed Iraq-specific March evidence.
- Exact-resolution frictions matter materially here: attribution, launch origin, impact location, and time-window confirmation are nontrivial hurdles.
- The next likely source was unlikely to shift the estimate by more than ~5 points unless it provided explicit Iraq-specific confirmation satisfying the rules.

## Why this is decision-relevant

The key decision point is not whether Iran is dangerous regionally. It clearly is. The decision point is whether the contract’s **narrow Yes condition** is being overrun by a broader "Iran is striking places" narrative. That is the strongest credible disagreement with an 85%+ price.

Said differently: the market may be directionally sensible but **too compressed toward certainty**.

## What would falsify this interpretation

This variant view would be falsified by any clear, credible reporting set showing:

- a confirmed Iranian missile/drone/air strike impacting Iraqi soil in March,
- attribution to Iranian forces or clear origin from Iranian territory,
- and enough timestamp consensus to satisfy the contract’s reporting rule.

If such reporting exists, then the market’s 85.4% would look reasonable or even conservative.

## Recommended follow-up

- Verify against one or two explicit Iraq-strike reports or market-resolution-adjacent summaries if available.
- Check whether any late-March Iraq event failed the rules because of interception, proxy attribution, or uncertain timing.
- For synthesis, treat this note as an **overconfidence challenge**, not as a hard No thesis.

## Bottom line

- **Market-implied probability:** 85.4%
- **My probability:** 60%
- **View versus market:** disagree
- **Strongest reason for disagreement:** the market may be overweighting broad regional-war escalation and underweighting the exact March + Iraq + direct-Iran + qualifying-impact resolution hurdles.
