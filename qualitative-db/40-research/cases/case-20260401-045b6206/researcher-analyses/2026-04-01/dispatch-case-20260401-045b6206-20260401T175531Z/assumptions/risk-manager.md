# Assumption
At least one March 2026 Iraq-directed incident will satisfy the market’s narrow requirements for **direct Iranian attribution plus physical impact on Iraqi soil**, rather than failing as a proxy event, interception, or attribution dispute.

## Why this assumption matters
This is the core assumption carrying the gap between a generic “Iraq is in the war” narrative and an actual **Yes** resolution. The market can look obviously correct at a narrative level while still being wrong on contract mechanics.

## What this assumption supports
- A Yes-leaning view on the market.
- Any estimate materially above 50%.
- The belief that regional war context will convert into a qualifying contract event rather than a near miss.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption
- Iraq is a structurally plausible Iranian target in a broader regional war.
- Iraq has a long history of being an arena for Iran-related military action.
- Current conflict summaries place Iraq inside the active attacked-country set, implying the path to a qualifying event is not remote.

## What would falsify it
- Credible reporting shows Iraq incidents were conducted by proxies rather than Iranian military forces.
- Reported missiles or drones toward Iraq were intercepted and did not impact the ground.
- Attribution remains disputed and cannot be tied to Iran or Iranian territory by consensus reporting.
- No March ET impact event can be confirmed within the contract’s reporting window.

## Early warning signs
- Coverage relies on vague phrases like “Iran-backed” rather than direct Iranian responsibility.
- Reporting emphasizes militia launches from inside Iraq rather than launch from Iranian territory.
- Stories focus on interceptions, debris, or preventive shoot-downs.
- Event timelines become confused across local time vs. ET.

## What changes if this assumption fails
If this assumption breaks, the probability should drop sharply because many alternative escalation paths in Iraq are contract-invalid. In that world, the apparent intuitive Yes case is mostly a false friend created by regional-war headlines.
