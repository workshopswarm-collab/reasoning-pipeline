# Assumption

The market's near-certainty price assumes that if Iran chooses to pressure Bahrain again, it will do so via a direct, attributable, non-intercepted strike that physically impacts qualifying Bahraini territory before the deadline.

## Why this assumption matters

A lot of the difference between "regional war makes Bahrain dangerous" and "this specific contract should trade around 95%" sits inside resolution mechanics, not headline risk alone.

## What this assumption supports

- A yes probability close to the market price.
- The idea that Bahrain is not just in the broad threat set but in the actionable direct-fire set.
- The idea that operational friction will not convert the next event into an intercepted, proxied, ambiguous, or non-Bahrain incident.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- Bahrain is strategically relevant because of the U.S. Fifth Fleet and Gulf alignment.
- Broader regional war raises the chance Iran is willing to broaden target selection.
- Historical Bahrain-Iran hostility makes Bahrain a plausible symbolic and military signaling target.

## What would falsify it

- Credible reporting that Iran is deliberately avoiding direct attacks on Bahrain despite striking elsewhere.
- Evidence that Gulf defense/interception is consistently preventing impacts.
- Evidence that Iranian retaliation is shifting to proxies or ambiguity rather than direct launch from Iranian territory.
- Passage of more time without Bahrain-specific threat indicators while other target sets dominate.

## Early warning signs

- Public messaging by Iran narrowing target scope to U.S. bases elsewhere or Israel.
- Reporting emphasizing Bahraini/Saudi/U.S. defensive posture successfully deterring direct attack.
- New incidents that are close calls but fail market criteria.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The contract should trade materially below near-certainty because the remaining path to Yes becomes narrower than the market is implying.
