# Claim

The market is directionally right to favor $90+ because there is a real, severe Hormuz-linked supply/logistics shock behind current prices, but 86.8% looks too confident unless you believe that disruption and its risk premium will persist into the exact late-March settlement window. My estimate is **around 72%** for $90+, versus the market-implied **86.8%**.

## Implication for the question

I **roughly agree** with the market's sign but **disagree with the magnitude**. The strongest pro-market argument is that this is not a purely narrative rally: institutional reporting describes major tanker disruption through the Strait of Hormuz, meaningful shut-in production, and benchmark crude already around the low 90s. If those conditions remain largely intact through late March, $90+ is a sensible favorite.

But to justify an 86.8% probability, the market is implicitly assuming persistence, not just a recent spike. That is where I lean less bullish. Both EIA and IEA reporting also point to meaningful buffers: emergency inventories, reserve releases, some demand destruction at higher prices, and an underlying medium-term supply picture that still softens once flows normalize. So the key issue is not whether the shock is real; it is whether it remains binding at resolution.

## Supporting evidence

- **Strongest case for market efficiency:** IEA reports a near halt in tanker movements through Hormuz, roughly 20 mb/d of disrupted crude/product exports, at least 8 mb/d of curtailed crude production, and benchmark crude around $92 after a rapid war-driven spike. That is exactly the kind of genuine physical stress that can justify a very bullish front-month crude market.
- **Transparent public baseline also supports elevated prices:** EIA says the current move reflects a large risk premium tied to actual and feared supply disruptions around Hormuz. It expects Brent to average about $91 in 2Q26 under assumptions that disruption peaks in early April and then eases.
- **Why I stop short of the market:** the same public baselines also say inventories are large, emergency reserves are available, and once flows normalize the market returns to oversupply/inventory-build logic. That means the thesis for 86.8% is not simply "oil fundamentals are structurally screaming above 90"; it is "the current geopolitical choke point remains sufficiently unresolved through the settlement date."
- **Market-decoding takeaway:** the market seems to be pricing persistence of disruption more than medium-term balances. That is a coherent read, but I think it overweights persistence slightly relative to publicly visible buffers and normalization risk.

## Counterpoints

- A more bullish trader can reasonably argue that once the market is already in the low 90s, only modest persistence is needed to keep the active-month settlement above $90.
- War/chokepoint dynamics are nonlinear; if escalation worsens, even 86.8% may prove too low.
- I do not have strong direct evidence here on the exact CL active-month curve behavior and contract-roll effects around the final March settlement, which adds basis uncertainty versus broader Brent-oriented reporting.

## Key assumptions

- The market's embedded assumption is that Hormuz-related disruption and the geopolitical risk premium remain materially active into the final March settlement window.
- Buffers such as reserve releases, demand destruction, and partial traffic normalization are meaningful enough that $90+ is favored but not near-certain.
- Public institutional reporting captures the main mechanism well enough that an additional likely source would not move the estimate by ~5 points absent genuinely new information on de-escalation or escalation.

## Why this is decision-relevant

This is a good example of when respecting the market matters. A standalone researcher could easily say "soft balances later in 2026 imply sub-$90" and miss that the market is pricing a very specific, immediate geopolitical persistence story. I think the market's logic is strong; I just would not round it up to the high-80s without more confidence that the disruption persists through resolution.

## What would falsify this interpretation

- **More bullish than my view:** clear evidence of continuing severe tanker disruption, worsening shut-ins, or renewed escalation late in March would make 72% too low.
- **Less bullish than my view:** visible normalization in shipping/insurance access, easing shut-in production, or a sharp front-month retreat into the 80s before settlement would make even 72% too high.
- **Structural miss:** evidence that the relevant active-month CME settlement mechanics differ materially from the broader benchmark framing used in public reports.

## Recommended follow-up

- Check late-March front-month CL price action and any contract-specific settlement mechanics closer to resolution.
- Watch for direct signals on Hormuz tanker traffic, insurance availability, and emergency stock releases.
- Treat this market primarily as a persistence-of-disruption question, not as a generic long-run oil-fundamentals question.
