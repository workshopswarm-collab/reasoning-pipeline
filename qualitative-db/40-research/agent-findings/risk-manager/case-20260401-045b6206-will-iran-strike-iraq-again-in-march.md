# Claim
The market was pricing **Yes at 85.4%**, but my risk-manager view is lower at **~72%**. I still lean **Yes** because Iraq is clearly inside the active regional battlespace in the ongoing 2026 Iran war and there is evidence that Iraq has already been attacked in that broader conflict environment. But I **disagree with the market’s level of confidence** because this contract has unusually strict resolution filters: the strike must be by Iranian military forces, explicitly claimed by Iran or confirmed from Iranian territory, and the drones/missiles must **impact** Iraqi soil rather than merely be intercepted. Those are meaningful failure modes for an 85%+ price.

## Implication for the question
My directional view is that Iraq is more likely than not to resolve **Yes**, but the market appears to be embedding too much confidence in a clean qualifying event and not enough confidence discount for:

1. **resolution specificity risk** — many regional attacks can look Iran-linked without satisfying the market’s exact attribution standard;
2. **interception risk** — attempted strikes that are fully intercepted do not count;
3. **proxy contamination risk** — Iraq is crowded with Iran-aligned militias and PMF/Islamic Resistance actors, but proxy actions explicitly do not count;
4. **timing/reporting risk** — the event must be pinned to March ET and credibly reported within the contract’s reporting window.

So I lean **Yes**, but not at the current market confidence level.

## Supporting evidence
- The broader 2026 Iran war environment appears to include Iraq among countries attacked by Iran. Even allowing for Wikipedia-as-secondary-source limitations, the current conflict summary explicitly lists **Iraq** among states attacked by Iran and records casualties in Iraq during the ongoing war. That strongly suggests Iraq is a plausible and active target set rather than a speculative add-on.
- Iraq is structurally exposed to Iranian military and militia activity because it is both geographically adjacent and politically penetrated by Iran-linked armed networks. Even Iraq’s canonical entity note frames it as a core venue for U.S.-Iran competition and militia-driven instability.
- Iran has historical precedent for striking inside Iraq when it perceives security or deterrence reasons to do so. That outside-view precedent raises the base rate above a normal bilateral peace assumption.
- If the war expanded to multiple regional U.S.-aligned or operationally relevant states, Iraq is one of the most natural spillover theaters because it hosts transit, militia infrastructure, and strategic depth concerns.

## Counterpoints
- The contract is not asking whether Iraq is exposed to conflict. It asks whether **Iran itself** initiates a qualifying air/drone/missile strike that **impacts** Iraqi territory. A lot of plausible violence in Iraq will fail on attribution or weapon-type grounds.
- Iraq is one of the most proxy-saturated theaters in the region. That raises the chance that market participants over-map “Iranian influence in Iraq” into “qualifying Iranian state strike in Iraq,” even though the contract excludes proxy attacks.
- In a high-alert environment, Iraqi, U.S., coalition, or allied air defenses may intercept incoming drones or missiles. Intercepted weapons are specifically excluded from Yes resolution.
- Tehran may prefer to use Iraq as a proxy launchpad, logistics corridor, or political pressure zone rather than a theater for openly claimed direct state strikes from Iranian territory, especially if it wants to avoid alienating Baghdad further.
- Reporting ambiguity matters. Even if an impact occurs, if credible consensus on attribution/date is weak inside the market’s window, resolution can still fail.

## Key assumptions
- The key bullish assumption behind a high Yes price is that at least one Iraq-directed event will satisfy the contract’s unusually narrow attribution and impact requirements, not just the broader intuitive idea that Iraq is involved in the war.
- My lower estimate assumes a nontrivial share of plausible “Iraq incident” paths fail on one of the contract filters above.

## Why this is decision-relevant
At **85.4%**, the market is treating a qualifying Iraq strike as close to routine. That may be too confident for a contract where several common real-world paths do **not** resolve Yes. The distinction between:
- Iran itself vs. Iran-backed proxies,
- impact vs. interception,
- clear attribution vs. murky attribution,
- and ordinary regional escalation vs. contract-compliant strike,

is exactly where expensive forecasting mistakes happen.

If a decision-maker is considering whether the market is already efficient here, my answer is: **not fully**. I think the market is directionally right but too compressed toward certainty.

## What would falsify this interpretation
- A clearly reported March 2026 Iranian missile/drone/air strike impacting Iraqi territory, explicitly claimed by Iran or widely confirmed as originating from Iranian territory, would push me quickly toward the market or above it.
- Conversely, strong evidence that the March Iraq incidents were all proxy actions, interceptions, or attribution-ambiguous events would push me materially lower.
- A definitive resolution clarification from credible reporting showing no qualifying impact in Iraq during March would invalidate the current Yes lean.

## Recommended follow-up
- Verify whether a **specific March 2026 Iraq impact event** already has consensus reporting satisfying the contract standard.
- Separate direct-Iran strike evidence from PMF / Islamic Resistance in Iraq activity.
- Check whether any cited Iraq incidents involved only interceptions or debris rather than a weapon impact.
- Treat any source that casually says Iraq was “attacked by Iran” as insufficient until the contract filters are checked one by one.
