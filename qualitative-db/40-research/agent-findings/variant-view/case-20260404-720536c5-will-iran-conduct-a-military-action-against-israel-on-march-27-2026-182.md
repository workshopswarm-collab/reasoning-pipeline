# Claim

I **disagree** with the market’s implied probability of **95.05% Yes**. My estimate is **~70% Yes / 30% No**.

The strongest credible variant view is not that a direct Iran-to-Israel strike is implausible in the abstract; it is that **95% is too close to certainty for a one-day, rules-constrained contract**. Traders appear to be pricing a broad late-March Iran-Israel war narrative, while this market requires a much narrower outcome: a direct Iranian military drone/missile/air strike on **March 27 Israel time**, with a projectile that actually **impacts Israeli ground territory**, not merely an interception, proxy action, launch, or attempted strike.

## Implication for the question

Directionally, the market may be right that the environment was highly escalatory. But at 0.9505 it is effectively saying that almost every remaining branch leads to a qualifying Yes. I think that overstates certainty because this contract has multiple ways to fail even in a hot conflict environment:

1. **Timing risk** — a direct Iranian strike could occur just before or after March 27 Israel time.
2. **Attribution risk** — proxy or partner attacks do not count.
3. **Impact-location risk** — interceptions alone do not count; something must impact Israeli ground territory.
4. **Resolution-confirmation risk** — the reporting must support date/time and origin clearly enough within the specified confirmation window.

So the variant view is: **high event risk, but not 95% clean-resolution risk**.

## Supporting evidence

- The market rules are unusually narrow. They exclude:
  - proxy attacks,
  - interceptions that do not impact Israeli territory,
  - surface-to-air missile activity,
  - non-aerial Iranian operations,
  - and impacts in the West Bank or Gaza.
- Prior direct Iran-to-Israel attacks in **April 2024** and **October 2024** show two things at once:
  1. Iran has demonstrated willingness to strike Israel directly in extreme circumstances.
  2. Large barrages can still produce **less decisive qualifying damage than headlines imply**, because many projectiles are intercepted and some attacks are deliberately calibrated rather than all-out.
- Accessible BBC reporting on the April 2024 attack described more than 300 drones/missiles launched, with **99% intercepted** according to Israeli military claims. That is a reminder that “Iran attacked Israel” and “this contract resolves Yes” are related but not identical objects.
- Accessible background on the October 2024 strike likewise supports the idea that Iran can launch a large direct barrage while effects remain more limited and strategically calibrated than raw strike headlines suggest.
- The vault’s conflict/deterrence context is consistent with a regime on both sides balancing retaliation and signaling against escalation control. That tends to produce **branchy outcomes**, not true near-certainty on a single date-specific resolution condition.

## Counterpoints

- The market’s strongest argument is straightforward: by late March 2026 the regional situation may already have moved so far into direct Iran-Israel confrontation that a March 27 qualifying strike was overwhelmingly likely or already widely understood.
- Iran has already crossed the once-taboo threshold of direct strikes on Israel, reducing the force of any argument based only on historical reluctance.
- If traders had access to credible late-March reporting indicating a direct Iranian-origin impact on Israeli territory on the date in question, then 95% could be justified.

## Key assumptions

- The market price is partly reflecting **broad escalation certainty** rather than only the contract’s exact resolution mechanics.
- Resolution frictions remain material even during active war: launch origin, impact location, and same-day timing all matter.
- The next likely source was unlikely to move my estimate by more than ~5 points unless it provided explicit March-27-specific confirmation satisfying the rules.

## Why this is decision-relevant

This is the kind of contract where traders can overpay for the obvious story.

The obvious story is: “Iran and Israel are already in direct conflict, so of course this resolves Yes.”
The variant story is: “Maybe yes, but 95% assumes away too many mechanical failure paths for a one-day resolution market.”

That difference matters a lot. The edge is not a heroic No call. The edge is identifying that **a very high probability event is still being priced as closer to certainty than the resolution language deserves**.

## What would falsify this interpretation

This view would be falsified by clear consensus reporting showing that on **March 27, 2026 Israel time**:

- Iranian military forces launched a qualifying missile/drone/air strike,
- at least one munition impacted Israeli ground territory,
- origin from Iran or explicit Iranian claim was established,
- and credible reporting settled the timing cleanly.

If that evidence exists cleanly, then the market was not overconfident; it was simply ahead.

- Check whether late-March reporting mixed together direct Iranian launches, proxy actions, and intercept-only events.
- For synthesis, treat this note as an **overconfidence challenge to a near-certain price**, not as a hard contrarian No thesis.

- **Market-implied probability:** 95.05%
- **My probability:** 70%
- **View versus market:** disagree
- **Strongest reason for disagreement:** the market likely compresses a broad high-escalation narrative into a one-day, narrow-resolution contract and thereby understates timing/attribution/impact-path failure risk.
