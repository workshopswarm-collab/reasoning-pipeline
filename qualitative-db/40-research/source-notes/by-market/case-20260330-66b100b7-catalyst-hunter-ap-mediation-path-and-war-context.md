# Summary

AP's mediation explainer says Pakistan has already been involved in relaying U.S. proposals and Iranian responses, indicating a live diplomatic channel exists even though Iran publicly denies formal negotiations and the exact point of contact remains unclear.

## Key facts extracted

- Pakistani officials acknowledged that a U.S. proposal had been conveyed to Iran.
- AP reported Iran dismissed the U.S. proposal publicly but acknowledged responding with its own proposals.
- Pakistan's foreign minister said Turkey and Egypt are also working behind the scenes to bring the sides to the negotiating table.
- The report frames Pakistan's effort as following weeks of quiet diplomacy.
- An analyst cited by AP suggested U.S. delays in broader attacks and measured Iranian responses may reflect an effort to preserve space for diplomacy.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- Message-passing between Washington and Tehran is already occurring through intermediaries.
- Multiple regional mediators are active, not just one.
- Diplomatic space exists despite active military escalation.

## What is uncertain

- Whether the existing channel converts into a publicly acknowledged in-person meeting.
- Which actor on the Iranian side has authority to lock in a qualifying diplomatic meeting.
- Whether battlefield escalation, Hormuz pressure, or domestic politics will interrupt the channel before a meeting is staged.

## Why this source may matter

This source matters because it distinguishes two different catalysts: one for terminal resolution and one for repricing. The market can reprice upward on proof that message-passing is real even before a qualifying meeting is locked in, but it can also stall if mediation remains purely indirect.

## Possible impact on the question

Supports a view that the market is not pricing fantasy; there is real mediation infrastructure. At the same time, it highlights the central catalyst risk for this market: indirect diplomacy can persist for a while without producing the exact qualifying event.

## Reliability notes

AP is high credibility, but some details rely on unnamed Pakistani officials and interpretive analyst commentary. Good for establishing that mediation exists; weaker for proving that a qualifying meeting is already scheduled.
