# Summary

EIA's March 2026 outlook says the recent spike in oil is tied to Middle East military action and an effectively closed Strait of Hormuz, but its base forecast still expects the disruption to ease, inventories to build, and Brent to fall below $80 in 3Q26 after averaging about $91 in 2Q26.

## Key facts extracted

- Brent rose from about $71/b on Feb. 27 to $94/b on Mar. 9 after Middle East conflict began.
- EIA says the current price includes a large risk premium driven by actual and feared disruption around Hormuz.
- EIA's model assumes shut-in production peaks in early April and eases as transit resumes.
- Under that assumption, Brent averages $91/b in 2Q26, then falls to about $70/b in 4Q26 and $64/b in 2027.
- EIA expects global production to outpace consumption over the forecast period, leading to inventory builds.
- OPEC+ agreed to begin increasing production in April 2026 by 206 kb/d, though future supply decisions remain contingent on disruption duration.

## Evidence directly stated by source

The source directly states that a prolonged Hormuz disruption is the main upside risk. It also directly states that once flows are reestablished, underlying supply-demand balances look soft enough that inventories rise and prices fall.

## What is uncertain

- Exact mapping from Brent forecasts to CME WTI/CL settlement outcomes in late March.
- Whether the Hormuz disruption lasts through the final trading day of March or fades sooner.
- Whether geopolitical escalation overwhelms otherwise bearish inventory/supply fundamentals.

## Why this source may matter

This is a high-credibility baseline for what an informed public forecaster thinks the current spike means: not that $90+ is impossible, but that the price level mainly reflects a live geopolitical shock plus risk premium rather than a durable tight-fundamentals regime.

## Possible impact on the question

This source supports taking the market's bullish pricing seriously because there is a real supply-disruption mechanism. But it also argues against treating $90+ as near-certain, because the same source expects easing disruptions and softer balances beyond the immediate shock window.

## Reliability notes

Government outlook with explicit assumptions and clear scenario dependence. Useful as a transparent benchmark, but highly model-sensitive to conflict duration.
