# Summary

Unemployment is a core labor-market variable that interacts with recession risk, policy, technology, and structural economic change.

## Key facts extracted

- Unemployment is measured relative to the labor force.
- It has cyclical, structural, and frictional components.
- Labor-market weakness often reflects deeper macro stress or transition.

## Possible impact on the question

Helpful when a macro question depends on whether labor-market conditions support or weaken recession, inflation, or rate-cut narratives.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- The source directly explains what unemployment measures and how labor-market slack is usually discussed.
- It supports baseline claims about unemployment as a macro indicator.

## What is uncertain

- It does not reveal whether current unemployment prints are hiding composition shifts, labor-force effects, or lagging policy impact.
- One labor indicator rarely tells the whole macro story.

## Why this source may matter

Useful because labor data often moves macro markets, but the concept is frequently used too loosely without grounding in what is being measured.

## Reliability notes

Good conceptual baseline; later pair with official labor releases.
