# Assumption

The market's very high $90+ probability mainly assumes that the Hormuz-linked disruption and embedded geopolitical risk premium persist strongly enough into the final March settlement window to keep the active-month CL contract at or above $90.

## Why this assumption matters

If this persistence assumption is right, a high-80s implied probability can make sense. If it is wrong and the disruption starts normalizing faster, the current price can look materially overstretched.

## What this assumption supports

- Treating the market as mostly efficient rather than euphoric
- A forecast that remains above 50% for $90+
- A view that late-March settlement is dominated by geopolitical persistence more than soft medium-term balances

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

Recent institutional reporting indicates genuine physical/logistical disruption, curtailed supply, and a large risk premium tied to Hormuz. Those conditions can sustain elevated front-month crude even if medium-term balances are softer.

## What would falsify it

- Clear evidence that shipping/insurance access through Hormuz is being durably restored before late March
- Visible easing of shut-in production
- Sharp front-month crude decline back into the 80s despite no new escalation
- Public evidence that emergency inventory releases and demand destruction are overwhelming the supply shock

## Early warning signs

- Front-month crude failing to hold recent spike levels
- Policy or military signals indicating de-escalation
- Reports of tanker traffic normalization
- OPEC+ / producer behavior implying confidence that the disruption is temporary

## What changes if this assumption fails

The market-implied probability should come down materially; the right frame becomes 'real shock, but overextended probability' rather than 'market efficiently pricing persistence.'
