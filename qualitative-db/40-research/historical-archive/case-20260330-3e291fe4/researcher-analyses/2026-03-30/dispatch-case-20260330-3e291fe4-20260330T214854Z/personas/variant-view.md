# Claim

The strongest credible disagreement with the market is that **0.775 is too high** because traders may be overvaluing narrative closeness to the threshold. My estimate is **0.45**, versus a market-implied **0.775**.

## Implication for the question

I **disagree with the market to the downside by 32.5 points**. The variant thesis is simple and mechanical: AAA is **$3.990 today**, which is **still not $4.000+**, and the most recent pace of increase appears to be slowing after the major March repricing already happened.

## Supporting evidence

- **Direct resolution-source fact:** AAA Fuel Prices shows the national average for regular at **$3.990** on **2026-03-30**. Under the market rules, this remains the **$3.99** bracket, not a qualifying $4.00 print.
- **Threshold friction matters more than headline framing:** the contract resolves on the published AAA number, not on whether gasoline is broadly described as "basically $4" or whether crude has surged.
- **Latest pace has slowed:** March 30 was only **+1 cent** vs March 29, and reporting described the national average as **hovering just below $4** after the earlier, much steeper March run-up.
- **Earlier shock may already be mostly transmitted:** EIA weekly data and AAA reporting show that the violent move happened earlier in March; the remaining question is whether enough pass-through is left for one final threshold-crossing print.

## Counterpoints

- Only **one cent** is needed, which is a serious risk to any bearish view.
- AAA itself said on March 26 that the national average **could reach $4 in the coming days**.
- Ongoing Middle East conflict, oil pressure, and spring demand are still directionally supportive for gas prices.

## Key assumptions

- The market is treating **$3.990** as nearly equivalent to a Yes state, when the contract requires the actual print.
- Retail pass-through from earlier oil spikes is slowing enough that the final penny is not overwhelmingly likely on the last available day.
- The final daily AAA print behaves more like a lagged national retail average than a pure spot-crude proxy.

## Why this is decision-relevant

This is the best non-consensus angle because it does not deny the obvious macro story. Gas prices really did spike. The disagreement is that the market may be **overconfident about exact threshold timing** in a contract that is now almost entirely about one last published decimal move.

## What would falsify this interpretation

- AAA prints **$4.000 or above** on March 31.
- Evidence emerges that national retail averages were still accelerating rather than flattening into the final day.
- Late-day state/regional pass-through data show the last penny was much more likely than the pace data suggested.

## Recommended follow-up

- Watch the next AAA print directly; this case is now mostly a one-number resolution problem.
- If AAA prints **below 4.000**, treat this as a clean example of market overconfidence caused by narrative proximity to a threshold.
- If AAA prints **4.000+**, the variant was wrong, but the postmortem should still examine whether the market was pricing a genuine edge or just one last coin-flip threshold crossing.
