# Summary

This note captures the core official tariff orders relevant to the market’s March 31, 2026 resolution mechanics. The main takeaway is that by early April 2025 the Trump administration had already established at least two broad China-relevant tariff layers that are not paused in the cited materials: a China-wide additional duty under the opioid-supply-chain orders (10% from Feb. 4, 2025, raised to 20% on Mar. 3, 2025) and a separate reciprocal tariff framework imposing a 10% universal baseline from Apr. 5, 2025, with higher individualized rates for some countries from Apr. 9, 2025. That combination pushes the general China tariff far above the 5%-15% band unless later modified materially downward.

## Key facts extracted

- White House EO 14195 (Feb. 1, 2025) imposed an additional 10% ad valorem duty on all products of the PRC effective 12:01 a.m. ET Feb. 4, 2025.
- White House EO 14228 (Mar. 3, 2025) amended EO 14195 by replacing “10 percent” with “20 percent.”
- The Feb./Mar. China opioid orders say the duty is additional to other duties and remains in effect until expressly reduced, modified, or terminated.
- White House / Federal Register EO 14257 (Apr. 2 / Apr. 7, 2025 publication) states Trump will impose a 10% tariff on all countries effective Apr. 5, 2025 at 12:01 a.m. EDT, with higher individualized reciprocal tariffs for some countries effective Apr. 9, 2025.
- The reciprocal-tariff fact sheet says these tariffs remain in effect until the President determines the threat is satisfied, resolved, or mitigated.
- The market description counts “general tariff rate,” including tariffs imposed on all imports plus tariffs “on top of that on Chinese imports,” and excludes item-specific exceptions/increases.
- EO 14256 (Apr. 2 / Apr. 7 publication) addresses low-value PRC imports and de minimis treatment. It matters for boundary-setting because the market excludes paused announcements and item-specific exceptions, and this order partly concerns a special import-treatment path rather than the broad general tariff rate.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- EO 14195 sec. 2(a): “All articles that are products of the PRC ... shall be ... subject to an additional 10 percent ad valorem rate of duty.”
- EO 14228 sec. 2: section 2(a) of EO 14195 amended by striking “10 percent” and inserting “20 percent.”
- EO 14195 sec. 2(b): these rates are “in addition to any other duties, fees, exactions, or charges applicable.”
- EO 14257 / White House fact sheet: 10% tariff on all countries effective Apr. 5, 2025; higher individualized reciprocal tariff for some countries effective Apr. 9, 2025.
- Federal Register publication of EO 14257 confirms it is an official presidential order and establishes it as a high-quality resolution-relevant source.

## What is uncertain

- The fetched excerpts do not fully show the China-specific individualized reciprocal tariff schedule inside EO 14257, so this note cannot alone quantify the total China general tariff precisely after Apr. 9, 2025.
- Later modifications in 2025-2026 could reduce or restructure the China tariff regime before March 31, 2026.
- There is some ambiguity around whether special de minimis/postal treatment should count toward the market’s “general tariff rate”; likely not, except as evidence that the administration was adding, not removing, China trade restrictions.
- The market’s primary resolution source is “official information from the Trump administration,” but consensus reporting may still matter if official texts are fragmented or modified over time.

## Why this source may matter

This is the governing source class named by the contract. It directly addresses what counts, what is in effect, and whether tariffs stack. It is much more probative than commentary or market chatter.

## Possible impact on the question

These orders strongly push against a YES outcome on the 5%-15% band. Once the China-specific duty is already 20% before adding the Apr. 5 global 10% baseline, the base general tariff on China appears structurally above the target band unless there is a later explicit rollback, replacement, or non-stacking interpretation that meaningfully lowers the countable total.
