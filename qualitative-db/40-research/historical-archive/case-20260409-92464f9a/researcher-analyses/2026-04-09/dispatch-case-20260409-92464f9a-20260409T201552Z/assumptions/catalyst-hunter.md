# Assumption

The decisive catalyst is NASA GISS publishing the March 2026 value, and no credible pre-release contextual source is likely to move the true probability by more than a few percentage points once that publication status is known.

## Why this assumption matters

The catalyst-hunter role is timing-sensitive, so the entire analysis depends on distinguishing real settlement catalysts from merely narrative climate headlines.

## What this assumption supports

- A low value on further broad research once NOAA context and contract mechanics have been verified.
- A view that the market should mostly reprice on NASA publication rather than on additional commentary.
- A decision to stop after the additional verification pass.

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

The contract explicitly names the NASA GISS table and says the first available March 2026 figure controls regardless of later revision. That makes publication timing and source accessibility more important than softer contextual reporting.

## What would falsify it

- Evidence that Polymarket is relying on a different NASA publication than the named table.
- Evidence that the NASA table is unavailable and the fallback source meaningfully changes interpretation.
- Evidence that an independent official pre-release source can be mapped tightly enough to the NASA threshold to move the estimate materially.

## Early warning signs

- Conflicting platform language about source-of-truth or fallback logic.
- Government reports implying an unusually large discrepancy between NOAA and NASA March values.
- Last-minute release delays or table-access issues.

## What changes if this assumption fails

The run would need deeper source triangulation and probably a higher uncertainty discount on any timing-based conclusion.
