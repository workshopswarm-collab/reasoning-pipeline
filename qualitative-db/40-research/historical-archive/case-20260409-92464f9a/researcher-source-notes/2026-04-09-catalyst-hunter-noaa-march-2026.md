# Summary

NOAA’s March 2026 global monthly report provides contextual confirmation that early-2026 global warmth remained elevated but not obviously extreme enough to make a very high NASA March anomaly a clear favorite.

## Key facts extracted

- NOAA reports January-March 2026 global surface temperature at 1.19°C above the 20th century average, fourth-warmest in the 177-year record.
- NOAA’s annual outlook page assigns only a 2.9% chance that 2026 becomes the warmest year on record, though a 98.4% chance of top-5 and >99.9% chance of top-10.
- NOAA’s ENSO explainer page highlights that recent months are being tracked dynamically and are subject to change in classification, implying climate-state context is still important but not a singular bullish catalyst for a fresh extreme spike.
- NOAA’s March page on monthly record coverage shows widespread warmth, but that is area-based context rather than the exact NASA settlement metric.

## Evidence directly stated by source

NOAA directly states the January-March 2026 year-to-date anomaly and the annual-ranking outlook probabilities.

## What is uncertain

- NOAA uses a different baseline and dataset than NASA GISS, so the reported number is not directly mappable to the contract threshold.
- The page is more contextual than dispositive for the exact `>1.29°C` NASA-GISS settlement question.

## Why this source may matter

This is an independent government climate source that helps test whether a NASA March value above 1.29°C would be consistent with the broader observed climate state.

## Possible impact on the question

The NOAA context modestly argues against treating a March 2026 NASA reading above 1.29°C as the default outcome. It supports a cautious or bearish stance versus a 72% yes market unless the NASA source itself is already known to print above the threshold.

## Reliability notes

High credibility as an official NOAA publication and independent from Polymarket contract wording. However, it is not the governing source of truth and uses a different baseline, so weight should be moderate rather than decisive.
