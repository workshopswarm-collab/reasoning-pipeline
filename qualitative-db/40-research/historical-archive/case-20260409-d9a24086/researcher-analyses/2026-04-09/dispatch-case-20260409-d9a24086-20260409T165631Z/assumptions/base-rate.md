# Assumption

Absent a new inflation shock, March 2026 seasonally adjusted CPI-U is more likely to resemble the recent 0.0% to 0.4% regime than the rare 0.8%+ surge regime seen mainly in 2021-2022.

## Why this assumption matters

The base-rate case against the market depends on treating 0.8% as an outlier threshold rather than as a normal tail risk for current conditions.

## What this assumption supports

- A low own probability for "yes"
- A view that the market is likely overweighting the chance of a very hot print
- Preference for the outside-view prior unless a strong pre-release catalyst emerges

## Evidence or logic behind the assumption

- The latest official BLS release shows February 2026 at 0.3% SA after 0.2% in January.
- Long-run historical frequency of 0.8%+ monthly CPI prints is low.
- March-specific 0.8%+ readings are rare, especially post-2000 outside March 2022.
- BLS seasonal-adjustment guidance implies the contract should be judged on the adjusted series, not noisy unadjusted spring price movements.

## What would falsify it

- Credible late evidence of a broad-based March price surge across shelter, energy, and core services consistent with a 0.8%+ SA print
- A reputable nowcast / preview cluster pointing near or above 0.8%
- Unexpected BLS methodology or seasonal-adjustment effect producing an unusually large adjusted monthly move

## Early warning signs

- Sharp broad energy spike in March that survives adjustment
- Multiple pre-release macro previews converging on an extreme headline print
- Evidence that March seasonal factors would amplify, not damp, observed raw price increases

## What changes if this assumption fails

The market's current very high yes probability would look much more justified, and the base-rate stance should move substantially upward toward the market.
