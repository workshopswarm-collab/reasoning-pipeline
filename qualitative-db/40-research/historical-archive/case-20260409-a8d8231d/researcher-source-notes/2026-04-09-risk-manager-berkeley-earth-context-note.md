# Summary

Berkeley Earth does not settle the market, but it adds useful risk context: February 2026 was already very warm, and Berkeley Earth explicitly warned about elevated uncertainty caused by degraded NOAA upstream data services.

## Key facts extracted

- Berkeley Earth estimated **February 2026 at 1.55 ± 0.12°C above the 1850-1900 average**, nominally the second-warmest February on record.
- The note says month-to-month temperatures ticked upward in February 2026.
- Berkeley Earth explicitly says February 2026 had **unusually high uncertainty** because of ongoing delays and disruptions from NOAA data providers.
- It states revisions are normal and current disruptions are larger than typical delays.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- February 2026 was very warm in Berkeley Earth's analysis.
- Data availability problems increased uncertainty versus normal monthly updates.
- Additional station data could revise estimates modestly higher or lower.

## What is uncertain

- This note is about February, not March, so it is contextual rather than direct evidence.
- Berkeley Earth's baseline differs from NASA GISS, so numeric comparisons are not one-for-one.
- It does not provide a March 2026 result here.

## Why this source may matter

It highlights an operational-risk channel: even if the settlement source is explicit, monthly climate products in early 2026 were being produced under noisier-than-normal data conditions, so extreme confidence should be discounted somewhat.

## Possible impact on the question

This source supports two competing inferences: the climate was running warm enough that exceeding 1.29°C was plausible, but data-quality noise means market participants should be cautious about near-certainty before the NASA number is checked directly.

## Reliability notes
