# Summary

Independent contextual source showing February 2026 remained very warm and that some upstream NOAA data feeds were degraded, increasing uncertainty and revision risk in non-NASA analyses.

## Key facts extracted

- Berkeley Earth estimated February 2026 at 1.55 ± 0.12°C above the 1850-1900 average.
- It described February 2026 as nominally the 2nd warmest February on record.
- It explicitly noted ongoing NOAA data problems and reduced station counts, which raised uncertainty and could modestly change later estimates.
- It said La Niña was fading and many forecasts expected El Niño later in 2026, but that timing is more relevant to later 2026 than to March 2026 settlement.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- "February 2026 was nominally the 2nd warmest February on record... 1.55 ± 0.12 °C above the 1850-1900 average."
- "February 2026 does have an unusually high uncertainty due to ongoing delays in weather station data availability."
- The report says revisions remain possible as additional station data arrives.

## What is uncertain

- Berkeley Earth uses a different baseline and methodology than NASA GISTEMP, so it cannot directly settle the contract.
- This source does not provide the March 2026 NASA number.
- The degree to which NOAA disruptions affected NASA's own published March value is not directly quantified here.

## Why this source may matter

It provides an independent cross-check that the climate state entering March 2026 was still very warm, while also surfacing operational-data uncertainty that could matter for confidence around marginal bracket markets.

## Possible impact on the question

The warm February backdrop makes a very low March print less likely, but the data-disruption caveat slightly increases caution around overconfidence if the contract bracket is narrow.

## Reliability notes

Useful independent contextual source, but secondary to NASA for settlement. Independence from NASA is meaningful; baseline mismatch limits direct comparability.
