# Summary

This is an independent contextual source rather than the contract’s settlement source. It helps with outside-view calibration and with checking whether a low 1.25–1.29°C March reading would have been plausible before the NASA cell appeared.

## Key facts extracted

- Berkeley Earth estimated February 2026 at **1.55 ± 0.12°C above the 1850-1900 average** and described it as nominally the second-warmest February on record behind 2024.
- The report said February 2026 was warmer month-to-month than January 2026 and that conditions remained elevated even though 2023-2024 extremes had cooled somewhat.
- It noted fading La Niña conditions and a trend toward neutral, with possible El Niño later in 2026.
- It also flagged increased uncertainty due to NOAA data disruptions and reporting delays.

## Evidence directly stated by source

- February 2026 remained very warm globally by independent analysis.
- Input-data disruptions increased uncertainty, though Berkeley Earth still considered the global average adequately constrained.

## What is uncertain

- Berkeley Earth uses a different baseline and methodology than NASA GISS, so its value is not numerically interchangeable with the contract’s target bracket.
- This source does not directly state the March 2026 NASA GISS anomaly.

## Why this source may matter

As an outside-view check, it suggests that a very low March collapse into a narrow 1.25–1.29°C NASA bracket was not the central prior once February came in hot again.

## Possible impact on the question

This contextual evidence modestly supports skepticism toward a sub-1.30 March outcome, but it is secondary to the direct NASA settlement source.

## Reliability notes

Reasonably strong contextual source with some independence from NASA, but not authoritative for settlement and explicitly subject to temporary input-data degradation.
