# Overview summary

Leadership-changes captures how a change in the top decision-maker or controlling figure alters strategy, execution, incentives, and market interpretation.

## Why it matters

It matters because the same institution can behave very differently after a leader change, especially when authority is concentrated or succession is contested.

## Where it applies

Relevant in politics, central banks, public companies, AI labs, sports organizations, and founder-led businesses, including parliamentary systems such as Canada where a leader swap can materially change party viability without changing the party itself.

## Common signals

- succession announcements or credible rumors
- policy or strategy shifts tied to a new leader
- changes in coalition support or internal control
- market repricing around style, credibility, or execution expectations

## Common failure modes

- assuming a leader change automatically means strategic change
- over-personalizing systems where structure matters more than the individual
- underestimating transition friction even when the successor is strong

## Interactions

Leadership-changes often interacts with `governance`, `elections`, `media-narratives`, and `sentiment`.
