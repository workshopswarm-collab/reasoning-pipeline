# Overview summary

Deterrence captures whether an actor can credibly prevent another actor from taking a costly action by threatening enough punishment or denial.

## Why it matters

It matters because many geopolitical outcomes depend less on intent alone than on whether the other side believes the cost of acting is too high.

## Where it applies

Relevant in war risk, alliance credibility, Taiwan, Israel/Iran, nuclear signaling, and regional security markets.

## Common signals

- credible capability and willingness signals
- alliance or support commitments that appear operationally real
- restraint following strong warning signals
- actors behaving as though cost imposition is believable

## Common failure modes

- mistaking rhetoric for credible deterrence
- assuming deterrence is stable when incentives are changing
- ignoring that partial deterrence can redirect rather than eliminate conflict

## Interactions

Deterrence often interacts with `conflict`, `escalation`, `sanctions`, and `diplomacy`.
