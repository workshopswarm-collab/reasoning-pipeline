# Overview summary

Escalation captures whether a conflict or confrontation is moving into a broader, more costly, or less controllable phase.

## Why it matters

It matters because market pricing often swings hardest not on the existence of tension, but on whether the conflict ladder is being climbed in a way that changes scope or consequence.

## Where it applies

Relevant in war-risk, Middle East conflict, Taiwan, sanctions retaliation, and energy-sensitive geopolitical markets.

## Common signals

- broadened target set or theater
- greater force commitment or mobilization
- retaliation spirals or reduced off-ramps
- actions that make de-escalation harder politically or militarily

## Common failure modes

- treating every dramatic incident as structural escalation
- ignoring slow escalation because it occurs through logistics, posture, or authorization changes
- confusing tactical intensity with durable strategic broadening

## Interactions

Escalation often interacts with `conflict`, `deterrence`, `diplomacy`, and `media-narratives`.
