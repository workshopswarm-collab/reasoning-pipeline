# Overview summary

Chips and compute is the infrastructure-constrained subdomain of tech & AI: semiconductors, foundries, GPUs, networking, memory, cloud access, and supply bottlenecks. It deserves a standalone overview because AI capability and product deployment are often bottlenecked by physical infrastructure rather than model quality alone.

## Why this subdomain matters

This subdomain matters because many AI outcomes are shaped by access to compute, fabrication capacity, packaging, networking, and deployment infrastructure. Researchers need a structural note for hardware and supply-chain realities so they do not reduce the whole tech-AI domain to model benchmarks and product launches.

## Core conclusions

- Compute is a strategic constraint layer, not just a cost input.
- Foundry concentration, packaging, and hardware supply chains can materially reshape AI timelines and winners.
- Chips and compute should be evaluated across the full stack: design, fabrication, cloud access, and deployment infrastructure.
- Policy and trade restrictions can alter compute availability as much as technological progress does.

## Main evidence clusters

- chip design and accelerator competition

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- treating benchmark leadership as independent from compute access
- focusing only on chips while ignoring power, networking, or packaging bottlenecks
- assuming supply growth is smooth and globally fungible
- underweighting export-control and foundry-concentration risk

## Missing coverage

- better stack-wide compute bottleneck maps
- stronger source hierarchy for semiconductor and supply-chain reporting
- clearer links between compute scarcity and product-availability outcomes

## Most fragile assumptions

- that compute supply will expand linearly with demand
- that model progress can be interpreted without infrastructure context
- that hardware constraints are temporary implementation details rather than strategic determinants
