# Overview summary

Economics is the domain of macro systems, policy transmission, prices, growth, credit, and measurable indicators that shape expectations across many other domains. Good economics memory should separate durable conceptual background from the faster-moving cycle of data releases and policy events.

## Why this area matters

Economics matters because inflation, growth, rates, liquidity, trade, and fiscal and monetary policy shape market expectations and spill directly into politics, geopolitics, and corporate outcomes. It is one of the core background domains that often determines whether other event interpretations are realistic.

## Core conclusions

- Economics is best understood through transmission channels, not isolated headline labels.
- Inflation and trade currently justify standalone subdomains because they have distinct measurement and institutional structures; other macro topics should stay in the parent domain or research layer until they earn more structure.
- Policy headlines matter less when they are disconnected from realized data and financial conditions.
- Good macro work requires separating nominal movement, real effects, and market interpretation.

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- treating top-line labels like inflation or growth as self-explanatory without transmission detail
- overweighting policy headlines relative to actual realized data and financial conditions
- ignoring how macro variables interact rather than move independently
- confusing nominal moves with real economic changes

## Missing coverage

- official data sources and release calendars
- central-bank and treasury source maps
- indicator-specific playbooks
- recession / base-rate research notes
- more structured coverage of credit and financial conditions

## Most fragile assumptions

- that high-level macro labels alone are enough for signal extraction
- that policy announcements matter more than actual transmission data
- that one indicator can stand in for the whole macro state
