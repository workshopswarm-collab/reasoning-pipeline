# Overview summary

Inflation is the price-level and policy-transmission subdomain of economics: indices, expectations, category composition, supply shocks, wage interaction, and central-bank reaction. It deserves a standalone overview because inflation is one of the most reused macro evidence classes and has a distinct measurement stack from broader macro growth or trade analysis.

## Why this subdomain matters

This subdomain matters because inflation prints, inflation expectations, and category-level price dynamics repeatedly drive market repricing, policy narratives, and political interpretation. Researchers need a structural note for how inflation is measured and transmitted rather than treating it as a generic label for “prices went up.”

## Core conclusions

- Inflation should be studied as a measurement and transmission problem, not just a headline print.
- Category composition, persistence, and expectation effects matter more than a single top-line number.
- Inflation interacts tightly with labor, energy, housing, and monetary policy, but should not be reduced to any one of them.
- Policy relevance depends on what type of inflation pressure is driving the move and how durable it appears.

## Main evidence clusters

- headline and core price indices
- category composition and persistence
- wage and service inflation interaction
- energy, supply, and imported-cost shocks
- expectations and central-bank reaction
- real-income and demand implications

## Important recurring objects

- CPI, PCE, and related price measures
- central banks and policy committees
- energy and commodity inputs
- wage and labor-market indicators
- housing and shelter components
- inflation expectations and breakeven-style measures

## Important recurring drivers

- macro
- inflation
- rates
- energy
- trade

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- treating one print as the full inflation story
- ignoring category composition and base effects
- flattening temporary shock-driven inflation and persistent broad inflation into one concept
- assuming the same inflation impulse has the same policy meaning across regimes

## Missing coverage

- stronger source maps for official releases and expectation measures
- clearer handling of base effects and category decomposition
- better linkage between inflation data and policy-response interpretation

## Most fragile assumptions

- that headline inflation alone is enough for macro judgment
- that inflation drivers are stable across time
- that central banks respond identically to all inflation surprises
