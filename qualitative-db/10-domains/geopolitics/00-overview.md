# Overview summary

Geopolitics is the domain of states, conflicts, diplomacy, sanctions, strategic resources, and cross-border power relationships. Useful geopolitical memory should track not just direct conflict, but also the institutional, industrial, energy, and logistics structures through which power is exercised.

## Why this area matters

Geopolitics matters because war risk, alliance behavior, sanctions, industrial strategy, energy systems, and strategic chokepoints can reshape outcomes far beyond the immediate conflict zone. It is a domain where background structure often matters as much as headline events.

## Core conclusions

- Geopolitics should be read through state capacity, strategic dependence, and institutional channels, not only battlefield headlines.
- `diplomacy`, `energy`, and `sanctions` currently justify standalone subdomains because they have distinct process and evidence structures; other themes should stay in the parent domain or research layer unless they earn more separation.
- Logistics, industry, and resource flows are often as important as formal public signaling.
- Conflict risk is best understood through escalation, deterrence, and dependence structure rather than isolated events.

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- treating direct conflict as the only high-signal geopolitical mechanism
- ignoring industrial capacity, logistics, or energy structure behind headline events
- confusing official signaling with actual capability or willingness to act
- assuming supply abundance removes geopolitical risk

## Missing coverage

## Most fragile assumptions

- that direct conflict is the only high-signal mechanism
- that energy transition reduces geopolitical risk automatically
- that public diplomacy cleanly reflects private strategic intent
