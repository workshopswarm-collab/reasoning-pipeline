# Overview summary

The sanctions subdomain is the institutional and enforcement layer of legal authorities, designated parties, financial restrictions, export controls, compliance systems, and evasion pathways. It is distinct from the `sanctions` driver note, which explains how sanctions pressure changes outcomes in specific cases.

## Why this subdomain matters

This subdomain matters because sanctions are not just announcements; they are systems of legal scope, enforcement capacity, financial plumbing, and adaptation. Researchers need a stable orientation note for the machinery of sanctions, not just the market impact of the latest designation or package.

## Core conclusions

- Sanctions effectiveness depends on scope, enforcement, and coalition durability, not headline severity alone.
- Financial restrictions, export controls, shipping constraints, and entity designations should be analyzed as linked but distinct instruments.
- Adaptation, rerouting, and intermediary actors are central to real sanctions outcomes.
- Sanctions analysis belongs at the intersection of law, trade, finance, and geopolitics.

## Main evidence clusters

- legal authorities and designation regimes
- banking and payment restrictions
- export controls and industrial constraints
- shipping, insurance, and logistics enforcement
- intermediary states, evasion, and rerouting
- coalition coordination and compliance behavior

## Important recurring objects

- treasury and finance authorities
- trade and export-control agencies
- banks, payment rails, and insurers
- shipping firms and maritime chokepoints
- designated entities, intermediaries, and front networks

## Important recurring drivers

- sanctions
- trade
- diplomacy
- energy

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- treating announced sanctions as fully effective sanctions
- ignoring leakage, adaptation, and third-country rerouting
- flattening different tools into one generic pressure measure
- focusing on target pain without checking coalition enforcement capacity

## Missing coverage

- better source maps for designations, export controls, and maritime enforcement
- stronger notes on sanctions-evasion patterns and intermediary states
- clearer links between sanctions regimes and sector-specific choke points

## Most fragile assumptions

- that formal legal scope maps cleanly to real-world enforcement
- that sanctions pressure is uniform across channels
- that adaptation is too slow to matter

- keep current sanctions packages and designation updates in `40-research/`
- use this note for sanctions-system orientation rather than rolling package commentary
- deepen regime-specific notes only where repeated use warrants them
