# Overview summary

The diplomacy subdomain is the institutional layer of negotiations, summits, envoys, formal agreements, backchannel signaling, and international process. It is distinct from the `diplomacy` driver note, which captures how diplomatic movement changes expectations in a specific case.

## Why this subdomain matters

Diplomacy matters because a large share of geopolitical change happens through process, signaling, sequencing, and coordination before it becomes visible as conflict or formal agreement. Researchers need a stable orientation layer for the actors, venues, and process logic that shape those shifts.

## Core conclusions

- Diplomatic process often matters before diplomatic outcome.
- Institutional channels, sequencing, and signaling quality can materially change risk even without a final deal.
- Public diplomatic statements are only one part of the process; venue choice, delegation level, and follow-through matter too.
- Diplomacy should be analyzed alongside deterrence, escalation, sanctions, and alliance structure rather than in isolation.

## Main evidence clusters

- summit and meeting structure
- official statements and communiqué language
- envoys, ministries, and intermediary actors
- treaty and agreement process
- confidence-building measures and de-escalation steps
- coordination with allies or multilateral institutions

## Important recurring objects

- foreign ministries and state departments
- heads of government and senior envoys
- multilateral institutions and mediators
- treaties, summits, and working groups
- backchannel or intermediary states

## Important recurring drivers

- diplomacy
- escalation
- deterrence
- sanctions

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- treating statements as equivalent to commitment
- ignoring the difference between signaling progress and operational progress
- overreading summit optics without checking follow-through
- separating diplomacy from military or sanctions context when they are tightly linked

## Missing coverage

- venue- and institution-specific source maps
- stronger guidance on communiqué interpretation and signaling quality
- more explicit process templates for negotiations and ceasefire-style diplomacy

## Most fragile assumptions

- that public messaging reflects private intent cleanly
- that talks automatically imply de-escalation
- that one meeting can substitute for process durability

## Recommended next research steps

- keep current negotiations and summit developments in `40-research/`
- use this note for diplomatic process orientation rather than rolling event logs
- deepen institution-specific diplomacy notes only where repeated use justifies them
