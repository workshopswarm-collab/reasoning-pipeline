# Overview summary

The cross-strait subdomain is the geopolitical and operational layer around China-Taiwan tensions, Taiwan Strait deterrence, military signaling, alliance credibility, and semiconductor-linked strategic risk. It is distinct from the `escalation` or `deterrence` driver notes because this note is about the recurring institution set, evidence stack, and failure modes specific to cross-strait analysis.

## Why this subdomain matters

This subdomain matters because cross-strait risk is one of the highest-consequence recurring event classes in the vault. Researchers need a stable orientation note for how Chinese pressure, Taiwanese signaling, U.S. coordination, military posture, diplomacy, and semiconductor concentration interact rather than treating every headline as an isolated crisis signal.

## Core conclusions

- Cross-strait analysis is about persistent deterrence architecture, not just headline tension.
- Taiwan, China, and the United States form the core actor triangle, but military and executive institutions matter more than country labels alone.
- Taiwan Strait risk cannot be separated cleanly from semiconductor concentration because TSMC is a real transmission channel from geopolitics into the global economy.
- Many short-window scares are signaling episodes rather than true structural escalations.
- The highest-value analytical task is distinguishing coercive signaling from durable changes in posture or capability.

## Main evidence clusters

- military exercises, force posture, and readiness signals
- executive and diplomatic signaling from Taipei, Beijing, and Washington
- alliance credibility and crisis-coordination behavior
- semiconductor and supply-chain concentration risk
- sanctions, export controls, and gray-zone pressure
- official statements versus actual operational movement

## Important recurring objects

- Taiwan, China, and the United States as state anchors
- presidencies, defense ministries, and national-security councils
- military organizations and command structures
- semiconductor choke points and foundry concentration
- diplomatic institutions and crisis-signaling channels

## Important recurring drivers

- deterrence
- escalation
- diplomacy
- operational-risk

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- treating every exercise or rhetoric burst as imminent invasion risk
- ignoring slow-moving force-preparation or political-conditioning signals
- separating Taiwan Strait risk from semiconductor and supply-chain consequences
- overfitting social-media panic or pundit certainty instead of institutional evidence

## Missing coverage

- stronger source hierarchy for military-readiness and operational-posture tracking
- better mapping between signaling events and actual changes in force availability
- more explicit framework notes for crisis-probability versus structural-background-risk judgment

## Most fragile assumptions

- that strategic ambiguity will remain equally stable across administrations and crises
- that deterrence can be inferred from rhetoric alone
- that semiconductor dependence always reduces escalation rather than sometimes increasing pressure
