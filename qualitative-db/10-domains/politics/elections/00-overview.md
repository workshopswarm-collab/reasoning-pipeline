# Overview summary

The elections subdomain is the institutional and procedural layer of candidacies, calendars, ballot rules, vote counting, certification, and power transfer. It is distinct from the `elections` driver note, which captures how electoral competition moves outcomes across cases.

## Why this subdomain matters

This subdomain matters because many political errors come from treating elections as pure sentiment contests rather than structured legal and administrative processes. Researchers need a stable place to track system rules, actors, and recurring procedural bottlenecks that sit underneath campaign narratives.

## Core conclusions

- Elections are rule-bound processes, not free-form popularity contests.
- Timing, ballot access, certification, and legal procedure can materially shape outcomes.
- Candidate and party competition must be read through the institutional system they are operating in.
- Election research should separate campaign narrative from process architecture.

## Main evidence clusters

- electoral system and ballot structure
- filing, qualification, and candidacy rules
- calendars, deadlines, and sequencing
- vote counting, certification, and dispute processes
- party competition and coalition structure
- official election administration and results

## Important recurring objects

- election administrators
- candidates and parties
- legislatures and executives
- courts and legal challengers
- constitutions, statutes, and electoral rules
- official results and certification bodies

## Important recurring drivers

- elections
- polling
- legal
- leadership-changes

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- assuming public attention is enough to infer institutional viability
- ignoring ballot access, certification, or legal timing constraints
- flattening very different electoral systems into one generic model
- overfitting campaign momentum while underweighting process rules

## Missing coverage

- country-specific electoral-system notes
- election-calendar guidance and official-source maps
- recurring legal-dispute patterns by jurisdiction

## Most fragile assumptions

- that candidate popularity is sufficient to infer procedural success
- that election timelines are simple and uniform across systems
- that polling can substitute for institutional knowledge

## Recommended next research steps

- keep election-cycle-specific developments in `40-research/`
- use this note for stable process orientation, not rolling campaign updates
- deepen country-specific election-system coverage only where repeated use justifies it
