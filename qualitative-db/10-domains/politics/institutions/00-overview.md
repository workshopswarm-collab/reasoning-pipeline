# Overview summary

Institutions is the subdomain of political system architecture: executives, legislatures, bureaucracies, courts, administrative process, and the rules that translate power into action. It matters because many political outcomes are constrained less by campaign messaging than by institutional design, authority, and implementation capacity.

## Why this area matters

Institutions matter because they determine who can act, how quickly they can act, what veto points exist, and how formal authority is converted into policy reality. Researchers need a dedicated institutions layer so they do not reduce politics to elections, polling, or leader preferences alone.

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- treating electoral outcomes as immediate policy outcomes
- ignoring veto points, implementation lag, or bureaucratic friction
- assuming formal authority maps cleanly to actual state capacity
- underweighting system differences across countries and regimes

## Missing coverage

## Most fragile assumptions

- that winning office is the same as controlling the system
- that formal legal authority guarantees implementation
- that institutional constraints are secondary to narrative or personality
