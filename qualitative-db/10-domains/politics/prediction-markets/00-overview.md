# Overview summary

The prediction-markets subdomain is the institutional and market-structure layer around event contracts, venue design, liquidity, legal treatment, and information quality. It is distinct from any one platform because the core question is not just who is listing markets, but how event forecasting interfaces with regulation, exchange structure, and public legitimacy.

## Why this subdomain matters

This subdomain matters because prediction markets sit at an unusual boundary: part financial exchange, part information market, part political object, and often part crypto rails problem. Researchers need a stable orientation note for venue differences, U.S. versus offshore treatment, event-contract legitimacy, and the specific ways regulation can reshape market access and trust.

## Core conclusions

- Prediction markets should be analyzed as market structure, not just as websites with odds on them.
- U.S. prediction-market questions usually reduce to event-contract legality, CFTC posture, and how much regulated venues can expand product scope.
- Crypto-linked venues add liquidity and access advantages but also import stablecoin, custody, and jurisdiction risk.
- Platform price action is useful but not automatically equivalent to clean information quality.
- Venue comparison often means Kalshi versus Polymarket, plus the institutions and rails around them.

## Main evidence clusters

- legal disputes, enforcement posture, and public legitimacy

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- treating prediction markets as pure truth machines rather than venue-shaped information outputs
- ignoring the difference between regulated U.S. event contracts and crypto-native offshore structures
- overreading thin or attention-skewed markets as if they were deep consensus prices
- confusing platform popularity with durable legal viability

## Missing coverage

- stronger source hierarchy for venue-level liquidity and market-quality measurement
- better notes on U.S. legal boundaries for event contracts by category

## Most fragile assumptions

- that more volume always means better information quality
- that U.S. regulation will evolve cleanly toward broader prediction-market acceptance
- that platform incentives and legal incentives are naturally aligned
