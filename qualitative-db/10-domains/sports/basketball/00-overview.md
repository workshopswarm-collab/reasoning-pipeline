# Overview summary

Basketball is a star-concentrated, possession-rich sport where elite player availability, lineup fit, late-game creation, and playoff translation can overwhelm surface-level team records. It deserves a standalone overview because high-leverage individual impact and lineup interactions create a distinct research structure from other team sports.

## Why this subdomain matters

This subdomain matters because basketball markets are unusually sensitive to a small number of players, rotation continuity, and on-court fit. Researchers need an orientation layer that keeps health, lineup context, and role concentration front and center instead of relying on generic team-strength summaries.

## Core conclusions

- Basketball is highly star-driven, and elite player health can swing outcomes disproportionately.
- Team strength depends on current rotation quality and fit, not just aggregate talent or record.
- Statistical baselines are valuable, but they need current-state interpretation around injuries, roles, and coaching.
- Playoff and high-leverage basketball reward shot creation, matchup resilience, and lineup stability more than surface depth charts imply.

## Common conflicts or failure modes

- treating record as a clean measure of title or playoff strength
- underweighting injuries, lineup fit, and player-role changes
- overreading recent streaks without enough long-run context
- assuming star power alone solves continuity or matchup problems

## Missing coverage

- stronger injury-source and availability guidance
- better lineup and on/off workflow notes
- clearer coaching-style and scheme summaries
- more explicit handling of regular-season versus playoff translation

## Most fragile assumptions

- that raw team record captures current team quality well enough
- that star power is sufficient without lineup fit and health
- that recent form cleanly separates signal from schedule or shooting variance
